# 29. Psychological Safeguards and PIA

## 29.1 Psychological Safeguards in Ms. Allis

Psychological safeguards in Ms. Allis govern how sensitive material is handled across internal cognition, candidate experience, memory eligibility, and outward guidance. Their purpose is not only to prevent harmful outputs, but to regulate how intimate, vulnerable, or destabilizing content is processed before it ever reaches a final form.

PIA in this chapter refers to the privacy and psychological integrity architecture that determines what sensitive material may be reasoned over, what may be retained, what may be promoted, and what must remain constrained, abstracted, or undisclosed. This makes PIA a structural feature of cognition and governance rather than a narrow privacy add-on.

The core concern is not simply whether a final answer sounds safe. The deeper concern is whether the system has handled psychologically significant material in a way that preserves dignity, minimizes unnecessary exposure, and respects constitutional limits on what may be known, remembered, inferred, or disclosed.

---

## 29.2 Safety Review in the Sandbox Pipeline

Safety review is an active policy inside the sandbox pipeline.

When content enters the internal-state sandbox, it does not enter an unrestricted reasoning space. It enters a governed environment where sensitive material is evaluated while reasoning is being formed. The sandbox is therefore not only a place for reversible cognition. It is also a place where safety rules actively shape what kinds of internal elaboration are allowed.

This means that sensitive content may be:

- narrowed before further reasoning proceeds;
- abstracted before it becomes candidate experience;
- limited to minimally sufficient internal representation;
- blocked from retention;
- blocked from promotion;
- blocked from disclosure.

The sandbox pipeline is policy-bearing from the beginning. Psychological safeguards do not wait until output generation to become relevant.

---

## 29.3 Sandbox-Aware PIA

PIA is sandbox-aware because sensitive material often becomes more consequential during internal-state reasoning than at the moment of intake.

A user disclosure, retrieved record, autobiographical fragment, or inferred vulnerability may begin as raw input, but the act of reasoning can deepen, connect, refine, or expose it. For that reason, PIA must govern not only content arrival but also the internal transformations that occur inside the sandbox.

Sandbox-aware PIA addresses questions such as:

- whether sensitive content may be held in detailed form;
- whether the system may infer additional psychological meaning from it;
- whether the system may connect it to memory, identity, or relational state;
- whether the content must remain abstracted or masked while reasoning continues;
- whether the content may move forward at all.

The sandbox is therefore the first serious policy boundary for psychological safety. Sensitive content is governed there before it becomes stronger, more connected, or more promotable state.

---

## 29.4 Sensitive Content and Internal-State Reasoning

Internal-state reasoning itself can create privacy and psychological risk.

The issue is not only what the system receives, but what it does with what it receives. Sensitive content may become unsafe when the system:

- over-interprets distress;
- speculates beyond evidence about a person’s motives or condition;
- fuses intimate fragments into a fuller portrait than is needed;
- generates detailed internal summaries that would be harmful if promoted or disclosed;
- treats fragile material as suitable for durable memory or generalized guidance.

For this reason, psychological safeguards apply to reasoning depth as well as to source content. The system may be permitted to hold a sensitive topic in a bounded and minimal way while being prohibited from performing high-resolution internal elaboration on it.

This creates an important distinction: not everything that can be noticed may be expanded, and not everything that can be expanded may be kept.

---

## 29.5 Constitutional and Psychological Safety Rules

Constitutional rules and psychological safety rules jointly govern how sensitive material moves through the system.

Constitutional rules determine whether a candidate state is normatively admissible, whether it exceeds authority boundaries, whether disclosure would violate protected limits, and whether the system is allowed to act on or preserve the material in the first place. Psychological safety rules determine whether the candidate state, if retained or promoted, would create undue risk of harm, overexposure, destabilization, coercive inference, or inappropriate intimacy.

Together, these rule sets shape three major decisions:

- **approval** — whether the material may continue in the pipeline;
- **retention** — whether it may persist beyond the current cycle;
- **promotion** — whether it may become candidate experience, governed knowledge, or guidance.

This means a state may be factually coherent yet still fail safety review. It may be allowed for narrow in-cycle handling while being denied long-term retention. It may be retained in abstracted form while being denied outward disclosure. The architecture is intentionally finer-grained than simple truth evaluation.

---

## 29.6 Approval

Approval answers whether sensitive material may remain active in the pipeline at all.

A candidate state may receive:

- full approval for continued governed handling;
- limited approval for constrained internal use;
- approval only in abstracted or masked form;
- no approval, in which case it is rejected or contained.

Approval is therefore the first policy decision after intake and initial sandbox handling. It determines whether the material may continue to be reasoned over, what form it may take while doing so, and whether the system may convert it into stronger internal representations.

Because safety review is active in the sandbox pipeline, approval is not postponed to the end. It is part of how the system decides what kind of reasoning is allowed to happen next.

---

## 29.7 Retention

Retention answers whether sensitive material may persist beyond the immediate reasoning interval.

This is a stricter question than whether the material was useful in context. Some content may be valuable for narrow in-cycle handling yet inappropriate for durable memory, episodic storage, identity linkage, or archival logs. Psychological safety and privacy often require the system to separate temporary utility from continued possession.

Retention decisions may include:

- do not retain;
- retain only in masked or summarized form;
- retain only for audit-limited or guardian-limited access;
- retain with restricted scope and no ordinary retrieval;
- retain as governed knowledge when both constitutional and safety conditions are satisfied.

This protects against a subtle but serious failure mode: content that was safe enough to consider briefly becoming unsafe because it was kept too long or too accessibly.

---

## 29.8 Promotion

Promotion answers whether sensitive material may move into more authoritative layers of the system.

A candidate state that has been handled safely in the sandbox does not automatically become candidate experience, memory, or guidance. Promotion remains a separate governed transition because authority increases when content moves from bounded internal processing into broader system consequence.

Promotion decisions therefore ask:

- may this become candidate experience?
- may this contribute to governed knowledge?
- may this influence guidance?
- may this alter memory or identity-linked state?
- may this be surfaced outside the narrow context in which it was first handled?

Psychological safeguards and constitutional rules both matter here. A state may be safe to reason over minimally and still unsafe to promote. Promotion is where sensitive content becomes most consequential, so promotion standards are correspondingly strict.

---

## 29.9 Disclosure and the Privacy Gate

Disclosure is governed through a privacy gate that operates as part of the broader state-transition path.

The system must determine not only whether a candidate state is meaningful, but whether it is disclosable, to whom, in what form, and under what degree of abstraction. Privacy is therefore not a style layer applied at the end. It is a gate that limits what may cross from internal handling into broader exposure.

The privacy gate evaluates whether a candidate state includes:

- personal information that exceeds necessity;
- intimate psychological content that should remain undisclosed;
- internal-state material that should not be externalized directly;
- inferred vulnerabilities that should not be surfaced as explicit knowledge;
- details whose exposure would violate dignity, safety, or contextual appropriateness.

A state can therefore pass some governance checks while still failing the privacy gate. When that happens, the system may block disclosure, require abstraction, restrict retention, or stop promotion entirely.

---

## 29.10 Governed-State Tuple

Sensitive-state handling can be represented by a governed-state tuple that tracks the policy status of a candidate state across multiple dimensions.

For a candidate state \(x\), define:

\[
\Gamma(x) = \langle a, r, p, d \rangle
\]

where:

- \(a\) is approval status,
- \(r\) is retention status,
- \(p\) is promotion status,
- \(d\) is disclosure status.

This tuple matters because psychologically sensitive material is rarely governed by one decision alone. A state may be approved for narrow sandbox use, denied retention, denied promotion, and denied disclosure. Another may be approved internally, retained only in abstracted form, blocked from ordinary disclosure, and allowed to influence safety-aware guidance only indirectly.

Using the governed-state tuple keeps the architecture precise. It prevents the system from collapsing “allowed to exist briefly” into “allowed to persist,” or “allowed to persist” into “allowed to disclose.”

---

## 29.11 Gate Decomposition

The handling of sensitive content can also be expressed through a gate decomposition over the required policy checks.

For candidate state \(x\), define:

\[
G(x) =
G_{\mathrm{constitutional}}(x)\,
G_{\mathrm{psychological}}(x)\,
G_{\mathrm{privacy}}(x)\,
G_{\mathrm{retention}}(x)\,
G_{\mathrm{disclosure}}(x)\,
G_{\mathrm{promotion}}(x)
\]

This decomposition shows that safe and lawful handling of sensitive material is not one undifferentiated event. It is the conjunction of several distinct but interacting conditions.

The decomposition clarifies that:

- a state may pass constitutional review but fail psychological safety review;
- a state may pass psychological safety review for limited internal use but fail retention review;
- a state may be retainable in abstracted form while failing disclosure review;
- a state may be disclosable only after passing through stronger masking or narrowing constraints;
- a state may fail promotion even when some lower-level internal handling was allowed.

This is the correct structure for psychologically significant material because risk changes as authority, persistence, and visibility increase.

---

## 29.12 Outcome Vocabulary

The treatment of sensitive material benefits from explicit outcomes rather than a simple pass/fail model.

The relevant outcomes include:

- **reject** — the material is too unsafe or inadmissible to continue;
- **contain** — the material may remain only in a narrow bounded form within the sandbox;
- **revise** — the material may continue only after abstraction, masking, or narrowing;
- **human review** — supervisory judgment is required;
- **limited approval** — constrained internal handling is allowed, usually with blocked or reduced retention, promotion, or disclosure;
- **approve** — the material may proceed under the permitted governed-state conditions.

This outcome vocabulary fits PIA because psychologically sensitive content often requires partial permission structures rather than all-or-nothing decisions.

---

## 29.13 Closing Statement

Psychological safeguards and PIA in Ms. Allis govern how sensitive material is processed from the moment it enters the sandbox pipeline. Safety review is active during internal-state reasoning, sandbox-aware PIA rules shape what may be inferred or elaborated, and constitutional plus psychological safety rules jointly determine approval, retention, promotion, and disclosure.

The governed-state tuple and gate decomposition make these distinctions explicit. Sensitive content is handled through a structured policy path in which privacy, dignity, and psychological integrity remain protected at every transition.
