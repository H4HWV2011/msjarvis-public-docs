# 7. RAG Pipeline and Routers

## Why This Matters for Polymathmatic Geography

This chapter explains how Ms. Allis routes retrieval, context assembly, model inference, and validation through the now-documented reasoning pipeline rather than treating RAG as a direct bridge from query to answer or from retrieval to durable state.

It supports:

- **P1 – Every where is entangled** by showing that semantic, spatial, temporal, governance, and registry context are assembled into a common reasoning path rather than left as disconnected retrieval outputs.
- **P3 – Power has a geometry** by making router behavior inspectable as a sequence of retrieval, sandbox reasoning, truth filtering, constitutional review, DGM gating, and controlled promotion.
- **P5 – Design is a geographic act** by clarifying that routing decisions determine not only what information is seen, but what may become consequential within the system.
- **P12 – Intelligence with a ZIP code** by preserving WV-first and place-aware routing while distinguishing ephemeral reasoning context from approved action-bearing state.
- **P16 – Power accountable to place** by documenting that no retrieved or generated content becomes durable memory, production action, or authoritative answer merely because it was routed into context.

As such, this chapter belongs to the **Computational Instrument** tier. It defines the router not as a direct answer engine but as the upstream coordinator of a gated reasoning pipeline whose outputs remain provisional until validated and promoted.

## 7.1 Purpose and Scope

This chapter describes how Ms. Allis assembles and routes context from semantic memory, spatial stores, registries, and related services into the live reasoning path. It now requires revision so that the routing model reflects the actual operational pipeline in which retrieval feeds the internal-state sandbox rather than directly producing production action, persistence, or self-authorizing conclusions.

RAG retrieval remains essential, but it should no longer be documented as if retrieval output itself were the decisive system state. Retrieved material is routed into a provisional internal reasoning regime, where it serves as candidate context for interpretation, comparison, and validation.

The central rule of the revised chapter is simple: **RAG provides context to sandboxed reasoning, not direct authority to production consequence**.

---

## 7.2 Routing Path

The chapter should now include an explicit routing-path section.

A user query, internal signal, or scheduled reasoning task first triggers router selection over available sources such as semantic memory, GIS-aware stores, registry data, governance text, and other task-specific layers. The retrieved material is then assembled into a context package and passed into the internal-state sandbox, where it becomes part of a reversible reasoning cycle rather than an immediately committed answer or memory update.

This means the routing path should be documented as:

\[
Q \rightarrow R_{\mathrm{retrieve}} \rightarrow C_{\mathrm{assembled}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{sandbox}}
\]

not as a direct path from retrieval to production output. The router is therefore a context-construction mechanism feeding deliberation, not a bypass around validation.

---

## 7.3 Ephemeral RAG Context

RAG outputs should be treated as **ephemeral reasoning context until promoted**.

A retrieved passage, GIS feature set, registry result, landowner record, or semantic memory hit may be highly relevant, but relevance alone does not make it durable memory, public output, or actionable system state. Retrieval creates a temporary interpretive window inside the internal-state sandbox, where the material may be compared, rejected, refined, contradicted, or staged for further review.

For that reason, this chapter should avoid implying that retrieval has intrinsic persistence. The correct statement is that RAG outputs remain sandbox-local context until applicable gates approve promotion into validated response, memory, or action-bearing state.

---

## 7.4 Candidate Reasoning Steps

The revised routing chapter should describe model outputs as **candidate reasoning steps** rather than final authoritative conclusions.

Once context has been routed into the sandbox, model-generated statements, intermediate inferences, hypotheses, summaries, or proposed next steps should be understood as provisional transformations over the current state. They are candidate reasoning steps that may support interpretation, but they do not yet carry durable authority.

This distinction is important because it aligns the routing chapter with the sandbox architecture already documented elsewhere. A model output can be useful, coherent, or suggestive while still failing later truth checks, constitutional checks, or DGM admissibility requirements.

---

## 7.5 Truth, Constitutional, and DGM Gates

This chapter should make the post-routing validation sequence explicit.

After candidate reasoning steps are formed inside the sandbox, they must face truth filtering, constitutional review, and DGM gating before they can become validated state. Truth filtering evaluates whether claims are sufficiently supported by evidence, provenance, consistency, or grounded retrieval. Constitutional review checks the content against the governing principles, permissions, and boundary conditions of the system. DGM gating then evaluates whether the candidate state is admissible for promotion under the system’s operational and safety criteria.

In prose, the routing model should say: router output becomes sandbox context; sandbox context yields candidate reasoning; candidate reasoning faces truth filtering, constitutional review, and DGM gating; only approved results proceed toward consequence.

---

## 7.6 Raw to Validated Chain

The chapter should formalize router behavior with the raw-to-sandbox-to-validated transition chain.

A concise formulation is:

\[
S_{\mathrm{raw}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{sandbox}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{validated}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{memory}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{action}}
\]

where:

- \(S_{\mathrm{raw}}\) is the initial retrieved and assembled context;
- \(S_{\mathrm{sandbox}}\) is the provisional internal reasoning state;
- \(S_{\mathrm{validated}}\) is the state that has passed truth, constitutional, and DGM checks;
- \(S_{\mathrm{memory}}\) is the subset approved for durable retention;
- \(S_{\mathrm{action}}\) is the subset approved for external consequence or operational execution.

This chain clarifies that routing is only the opening movement of the pipeline. The router is upstream of commitment, not identical with commitment.

---

## 7.7 Router Semantics and Non-Commitment

The revised chapter should say plainly that the router is a proposal-forming subsystem.

Its job is to select sources, assemble context, and shape the initial conditions under which reasoning occurs. It does not itself authorize memory writes, public claims, tool execution, geospatial state changes, or governance consequence.

This means that even a successful routing pass should be interpreted as non-committing. The router may have done its job perfectly and still produce a candidate state that is later rejected, revised, quarantined, or escalated.

---

## 7.8 Relation to GeoDB and Hilbert Framing

This chapter should remain linked to the spatial and formal chapters.

RAG routing may gather material from semantic stores, GeoDB resources, registries, and other domain-specific layers, but those routed materials only become operationally meaningful through the same governed transition logic described in the Hilbert and governed-state revisions. In that sense, routed context is an input to state formation across \(H_{\text{App}}\), \(H_{\text{geo}}\), temporal, and people-linked domains, but it does not directly determine the committed state of those domains.

This keeps the chapter consistent with the revised view that vectors, retrievals, and generated outputs are not self-authorizing. They must become governed, validated, and promoted before they count as operational state.

---

## 7.9 Revision Principle

The governing revision principle for this chapter is:

- RAG retrieval provides context into the internal-state sandbox, not direct production action or persistence.
- RAG outputs should be treated as ephemeral reasoning context until promoted.
- Model outputs inside the routed context should be described as candidate reasoning steps rather than final conclusions.
- Candidate reasoning steps must face truth filtering, constitutional review, and DGM gating before they become validated state.
- Router behavior should be formalized through the transition chain \(S_{\mathrm{raw}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{sandbox}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{validated}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{memory}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{action}}\).
- The router is upstream of commitment and should not be documented as a direct persistence or action mechanism.

Under this revised framing, the RAG pipeline remains central to Ms. Allis, but it is now documented as the context-routing front end of a governed reasoning architecture: retrieval feeds sandbox deliberation, deliberation yields candidate reasoning, validation determines admissibility, and only promoted state becomes durable or consequential.
