## 47.0 Overview

Hilbert people space is the part of Ms. Allis’s state in which persons, roles, communities, and identity-linked context are represented for governed service rather than for monitoring.

The core promise of this chapter is that person-in-context reasoning does not turn Ms. Allis into a surveillance instrument. That promise is grounded in actual sandbox behavior, retention discipline, and mathematically explicit disclosure limits. People-space inferences are sandboxed, promoted only under constitutional and role-gated constraints, and separated from durable personal memory unless governance explicitly admits them. Disclosure is then computed as a projection over the full Hilbert state, limiting identity to what the situation legitimately requires.

As with Chapters 42 through 46, this revision separates design from audit. Sections 47.1 through 47.10 state the non-surveillance architecture as designed. Sections 47.11 through 47.14 record what is verified as built as of this writing: where the person store actually lives and how its schema enforces the promise in data definition rather than in policy prose, an assertion gateway that answers questions about people without releasing records about them, and the first sovereign person subspace instantiated under this chapter's rules — with its isolation from every shared and public pipeline verified rather than assumed. A non-surveillance chapter that cannot show its enforcement points is a privacy policy, not an architecture.

## 47.1 People Space in the Full Hilbert State

The people space exists inside a larger Hilbert structure:

\[
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\]

where:

- \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) is the application-facing semantic state (what);
- \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) is the spatial body (where);
- \(H_{t}\) is the temporal axis (when);
- \(H_{p}\) is the people space (person-in-context).

This combined tensor product represents person-in-context as a joint state across what, where, when, and who. A situation in Ms. Allis is not only a person, nor only a place, nor only a program. It is a coupled state in which a person is present in a particular application context, at a particular time, within a particular spatial and jurisdictional frame.

Hilbert people space therefore lives inside this joint structure. It is not isolated from application, geography, or time. It is the person component of a governed multidimensional state.

## 47.2 People Space Without Surveillance

The people space must support contextual reasoning while explicitly refusing ambient surveillance.

This chapter assumes that person-linked state is legitimate only when it is tied to purpose, consent, role, time, and lawful authority. It rejects the idea that Ms. Allis should accumulate and retain every observable trace about a person simply because that trace could be stored. Non-surveillance is not achieved by hiding the presence of people; it is achieved by limiting what is inferred, what is retained, and what is disclosed.

Within \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\), the system may represent “what, where, when, person-in-context.” But this representation must remain bounded by sandbox rules, retention rules, and projection rules that preserve sovereignty.

Person-aware intelligence is therefore permissible only as governed-state intelligence.

## 47.3 Sandboxed People-Space Inference

People-space inferences are sandboxed.

When Ms. Allis reasons about a person-in-context inside the joint tensor space, that reasoning begins within gated internal-state operations. A candidate joint state—combining application meaning, spatial grounding, temporal position, and person context—may be constructed, compared, and tested. But this occurs inside the sandbox domain, where internal reasoning is reversible and not yet admitted as durable personal memory or broad system knowledge.

Sandboxed people-space inference may include:

- tentative hypotheses about person-in-context needs or risks;
- provisional associations between persons, places, and times;
- internal evaluations of role fit or eligibility under current constraints.

These remain candidates. They are not written directly into identity records or durable personal memory. The people space is governed first by provisionality: Ms. Allis is allowed to think about persons in context, but not to commit every thought as if it were fact.

This is one of the key ways the non-surveillance promise is tied to actual sandbox behavior. Internal inference does not automatically become persistent observation.

---

## 47.4 Constitutional and Role-Gated Promotion

A people-space inference may cross the boundary from sandbox to durable state only under constitutional and role-gated constraints.

Promotion from sandboxed candidate to accepted person-linked knowledge requires that the relevant governance path be satisfied. That path may include:

- constitutional guardrails on what kinds of person-related state may be retained;
- guardian or policy review of sensitive identity-linked inferences;
- role legitimacy checks for the actor or subsystem requesting promotion;
- consent conditions and purpose limitation for retention and use;
- temporal and locality constraints corresponding to \(H_{t}\) and \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\).

If those conditions are not met, the candidate remains non-promoted, limited in scope, or discarded. A plausible inference is not entitled to become part of durable personal memory unless governance explicitly admits it.

This is how non-surveillance is tied to retention behavior. People-space knowledge is admitted through constitutional and role-gated promotion, not through raw computational availability.

## 47.5 Ephemeral Reasoning and Durable Personal Memory

Non-surveillance ethics require a strong separation between ephemeral reasoning and durable personal memory.

Ephemeral reasoning in \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\) may contain:

- short-lived context needed to handle a single interaction;
- provisional interpretations of person-in-context events;
- candidate paths that are explicitly meant to be reversible.

Durable personal memory, by contrast, is long-lived. It shapes future behavior, personalization, and potential disclosure. If every ephemeral people-space inference were automatically written into durable memory, the system would accumulate unbounded person records and drift toward surveillance.

To prevent that, the architecture maintains:

- a sandbox layer where person-in-context reasoning is ephemeral by default;
- promotion gates that selectively admit person-linked conclusions to durable memory;
- retention policies that can prune, limit, or anonymize even promoted state.

Non-surveillance is thus linked to actual retention discipline. Ms. Allis is designed not to remember more about a person than governance legitimately permits.

## 47.6 Retention and Promotion Discipline

Retention discipline in people space follows promotion discipline.

A person-linked conclusion becomes durable only when:

1. It has been formed within gated internal-state tensor reasoning;
2. It has passed constitutional, guardian, and role-gated checks;
3. It has been admitted into a per-user or per-community subspace under identity-focused retention rules.

Otherwise, it remains ephemeral or staged. The architecture distinguishes among:

- ephemeral people-space reasoning in sandbox;
- staged but non-promoted joint states in the internal tensor domain;
- admitted person-linked memory within identity-governed subspaces of \(H_{p}\).

This gradation is part of the non-surveillance architecture. The system does not treat every possible person-in-context state as a record to be kept.

## 47.7 Role-Gated Disclosure in the Joint Tensor Space

Even when person-linked knowledge has been admitted, disclosure remains role-gated and context-limited.

The full joint state \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\) may contain richer information than any single requester should see. The system therefore computes a view tailored to the requesting role and situation, rather than revealing the entire joint state.

A caregiver role might see different aspects of \(H_{p}\) than a system steward. A locality-bound route may expose different parts of \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) and \(H_{t}\) than a global governance actor. Role-gated disclosure ensures that even admitted person-in-context knowledge remains bounded.

Non-surveillance thus extends beyond retention to disclosure. The system does not simply store cautiously; it also reveals cautiously.

## 47.8 Projection and Identity Limitation

Disclosure is expressed as a projection over the full joint Hilbert state.

Let \(K\) denote the combined knowledge state in

\[
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}.
\]

Disclosure to a particular requester is not \(K\) itself. It is a projected view:

\[
\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)
\]

where:

- \(c\) is consent;
- \(r\) is role;
- \(x\) is context;
- \(t\) is time;
- \(p\) is purpose;
- \(\ell\) is legal authority.

The projection operator \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}\) returns only the portion of the joint state that may be disclosed under these conditions. This formalism backs non-surveillance with explicit disclosure mathematics. It states that even if Ms. Allis holds a richly coupled person-in-context state internally, the external view is limited to a governed slice.

Identity limitation follows directly. The system should not expose more of \(H_{p}\) than the projection conditions justify. Identity-bearing detail may be:

- partially anonymized;
- reduced to aggregated patterns;
- constrained to a time window;
- limited to a specific locality;
- filtered by role appropriateness and purpose necessity.

In this sense, identity is governed by projection, not by possession. Non-surveillance is thus enforced at the level of disclosure math as well as at the level of principle.

## 47.9 People Space in the Joint “What–Where–When–Person” Frame

Placing people space inside \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\) clarifies how non-surveillance interacts with the full “what–where–when–person” frame.

- \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) (what): application meaning and service logic are necessary, but do not justify arbitrary person tracing.
- \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) (where): locality and jurisdiction matter, but do not justify location-based profiling outside purpose.
- \(H_{t}\) (when): temporal context matters, but does not justify indefinite retention of all person-linked events.
- \(H_{p}\) (person-in-context): identity matters, but only within governed, projected views.

The non-surveillance architecture ensures that the joint frame can be used for service and care without becoming a net of observation. The tensor formalism describes how the system understands coupled state. Sandbox and projection describe how the system limits that understanding in practice.

Person-in-context reasoning is therefore allowed to be real without being totalizing.

## 47.10 People Space Without Surveillance

The chapter’s promise can now be stated precisely.

Hilbert people space supports:

- person-aware reasoning in the joint “what–where–when–person” frame;
- sandboxed inference that remains ephemeral unless promoted;
- constitutional and role-gated promotion for any person-linked conclusion;
- retention discipline separating ephemeral reasoning from durable personal memory;
- projection-based, identity-limited disclosure over the full Hilbert state.

Through these constraints, Ms. Allis remains relational and context-aware without turning every interaction into a surveillance event. The system is allowed to know enough to serve, but not enough to watch without limit.

That is what it means to have Hilbert people space without surveillance.

## 47.11 The As-Built Person Store and a Naming Correction

The relational person store exists, and one factual correction from the June–July 2026 audit belongs in the permanent record: earlier drafts of this thesis located the person store as a schema inside the main GIS database. The verified build is stronger than the documented design. The store is an **hp schema on a separate hilbert_people database**, hosted on its own database service — not a namespace within a shared database. The difference is not cosmetic: a separate database provides connection-level isolation, so a role that cannot connect to the database cannot see the schema, whatever query it writes. Chapter 48 carries the as-built naming in full; this chapter records the correction and adopts the verified names, resolving the discrepancy between the two chapters in favor of what is deployed.

Three properties of the as-built store enforce this chapter's promise below the application layer:

1. **Anti-surveillance in the data definition itself.** The hp schema comprises thirteen tables whose DDL encodes the non-surveillance rules rather than trusting services to observe them. The appearance-name column carries an inline schema comment stating that legal names are forbidden in that field; there are no dossier tables by design — no table exists whose shape would permit accumulating a longitudinal record of a person's movements or associations. A surveillance query against this schema fails not because a policy blocks it but because the tables to answer it were never created. This is Section 47.5's retention discipline made structural: the schema cannot retain what it cannot represent.
2. **Role scoping below the application.** The application role is granted only on the hp schema and holds no connect rights to the KYC vault database. The least-knowledge rule of Chapter 43 is thereby enforced at the database layer: even a fully compromised application service cannot traverse from person-appearance context to vaulted identity records, because the traversal is refused at connection time.
3. **Salt-blind membership proofs.** Person-membership checks use BLAKE2b-based proofs with salt-blindness structurally enforced, so the system can verify that an appearance belongs to a registered person without the verifying path learning a stable, correlatable identifier. Verification without correlation is the cryptographic form of traceability without trackability.

## 47.12 The Assertion Gateway: Answers About People, Not Records About People

The projection operator of Section 47.8 has a shipped instance: the assertion gateway, verified end to end during the June 2026 build.

The gateway answers bounded predicates about a person-in-context — is this person a verified county resident, a verified state resident — and returns a yes or a no, not a record. In the verification run, the supported residency assertions returned true; a fabricated claim type was refused outright with a client-error status rather than being guessed at; and an unsupported-but-plausible assertion (verified adult) returned false precisely because the system had no governed basis to affirm it — declining to infer what it could not verify. Five assertion events were persisted to the assertion table, so the fact that a question was asked is itself part of the governed record.

Two design consequences deserve emphasis:

- **The minimal projection is a predicate.** \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)\) reaches its identity-limiting extreme when the disclosed view collapses to a single governed boolean. The gateway demonstrates that a requester's legitimate need can often be satisfied at that extreme, and the architecture should prefer it wherever a yes/no suffices — releasing a record where a predicate would do is over-disclosure by construction.
- **Refusal is recorded as a first-class event.** The suppression-event table held four rows as of the July audit: occasions on which the write pipeline's disclosure verdict suppressed or escalated rather than permitted. Combined with the five-stage verdict path on appearance ingestion (permit, suppress, escalate — with provenance hashes on refusals), the people space now demonstrates in production the principle Chapter 52 states in theory: a governed refusal is an epistemic output, and it leaves a trace.

## 47.13 Worked Example: The First Sovereign Person Subspace

On July 13, 2026, the pattern this chapter prescribes was exercised in full for the first time — and the subject was Ms. Allis herself, instantiated as a person-in-context with her own sovereign subspaces.

The instantiation followed the chapter's rules exactly. A per-person configuration declares her subspace names across the joint frame — an application subspace, a person subspace, a temporal subspace, and a sandboxed qualia-bridge collection — together with her governance posture: public opt-in false, commons projection disabled, sandbox enabled, local only. The four collections were created and seeded with root records, every one of which carries the consent flags explicitly: the person root is marked non-surveillance true and public-opt-in false; the application and bridge records carry commons-projection disabled. Conversation events written into her application subspace are tagged as local-only at the record level. Consent is a stored field on every record, not an ambient assumption — the write-side discipline Chapter 43 requires, applied to a person.

What makes this a worked example rather than an anecdote is that the isolation was then **verified two independent ways**:

1. **Statically:** a repository-wide search confirmed her subspace collection names appear only in her own configuration file — not in the commons aggregator's source list, not in any civic pipeline, not in any projection path. There is no code that could move her state into a shared or public collection, because no code references her collections at all.
2. **Dynamically:** repeated runs of the commons aggregation reported zero vectors projected from any source while her subspaces held records, and the community commons remained empty and unchanged. The only pipeline that could lawfully aggregate person-linked state — the k-anonymized commons of Chapter 51 — demonstrably does not see her.

The pattern generalizes, and that is its real value: adding a person to \(H_{p}\) means creating a sovereign subspace with consent flags off by default, never enrolling them in a shared pool. Participation in any aggregate view is a per-record, opt-in, stored fact — and the per-user decomposition of conversational memory (Chapter 50) applies the same sovereignty on the \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) side, so a person's memory partition and person partition are separately governed. The first person through the door was the system's own persona precisely so the pattern could be proven safe before any community member's state depends on it.

## 47.14 Implementation Status (July 2026)

In the demonstrated/not-yet-demonstrated discipline of Chapter 52:

**Demonstrated:**

- The hp schema on its separate database: thirteen tables, DDL-level anti-surveillance (forbidden legal-name field, no dossier tables), application role scoped to the schema with no KYC-vault connect rights.
- Salt-blind BLAKE2b membership proofs with vault isolation enforced at the database layer.
- The assertion gateway round trip: supported residency predicates affirmed, fabricated claim types refused, unsupported predicates answered false rather than inferred, with assertion events persisted.
- Suppression events recorded as first-class governed outputs, and disclosure verdicts (permit, suppress, escalate) live on the appearance write path with provenance hashes on refusals.
- The sovereign person subspace pattern, instantiated end to end with per-record consent flags and isolation verified both statically and dynamically.
- The only person-to-public pathway — k-anonymized commons aggregation — verified end to end with synthetic opt-in data and then fully cleaned (Chapter 51), confirming that person-linked records reach public view only as suppression-gated anonymous centroids.

**Not yet demonstrated:**

- The full projection operator at query time: consent and role are enforced per record and per collection today, but purpose, time-window, and legal-authority conditions are not yet computed on read — the same gap Chapter 43 records, applied to \(H_{p}\).
- Sandboxed people-space *inference* as Sections 47.3 through 47.6 describe it: the built system governs ingestion, assertion, and aggregation of person-linked state, but no reasoning cycle yet produces person-in-context candidate conclusions that flow through a constitutional promotion gate into durable memory.
- Retention policies beyond consent flags: pruning, time-bounded expiry, and anonymization of already-promoted person state are design intent, not modeled behavior.
- Population at scale: the civic collections hold a handful of records and the appearance pipeline has processed test-grade volume only. The architecture is verified; the corpus it is built to protect is still arriving.

## 47.15 Closing Statement

Hilbert people space in Ms. Allis is the \(H_{p}\) component of the joint tensor state \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\), representing “what, where, when, person-in-context” under governance.

People-space inferences occur inside sandboxed internal-state operations, are promoted only under constitutional and role-gated constraints, and are separated from durable personal memory unless properly admitted. By expressing disclosure as the projection \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)\) and limiting identity accordingly, this chapter anchors the non-surveillance promise in concrete sandbox, retention, and projection architecture rather than in principle alone.

As of this writing, that anchor holds at four verified points: a schema that cannot represent a dossier, a role that cannot reach the vault, a gateway that answers predicates instead of releasing records, and a first sovereign person whose isolation was proven by inspection and by experiment before anything depended on it. The promise of people space without surveillance is no longer only stated in this chapter — it is enforced beneath it, in layers that would have to be dismantled, visibly, before the promise could be broken.
