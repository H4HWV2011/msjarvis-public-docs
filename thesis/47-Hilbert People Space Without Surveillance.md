## 47.0 Overview

Hilbert people and noun space is the part of Ms. Allis’s state where persons, roles, communities, programs, places, events, and other identity-linked context are represented for governed service rather than for monitoring.

The core claim of this chapter is that person‑in‑context and noun‑in‑context reasoning does not turn Ms. Allis into a surveillance instrument. That claim is anchored in sandbox behavior, retention discipline, and mathematically explicit disclosure limits. People‑space and noun‑space inferences run in sandboxes, cross into durable memory only under constitutional and role‑gated conditions, and remain separate from long‑lived identity stores unless governance explicitly admits them. Disclosure is computed as a projection over the full Hilbert state, constraining identity and subject detail to what a situation legitimately requires.

Sections 47.1–47.10 describe the non‑surveillance architecture for people and general nouns. Sections 47.11–47.14 document what is actually built and verified as of July 2026: where the person store and general noun store reside, how their schemas encode the promise in data definition rather than policy prose, how assertion paths answer questions about people and nouns without releasing records about them, and how a sovereign person subspace instantiated under this chapter’s rules has its isolation from shared and public pipelines verified. A non‑surveillance chapter that cannot show its enforcement points is a privacy policy, not an architecture.

## 47.1 People and Nouns in the Full Hilbert State

People and noun spaces live inside a larger Hilbert structure:

\[
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\]

where \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) is application‑facing semantic state (what), \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) is spatial state (where), \(H_{t}\) is temporal state (when), and \(H_{p}\) is people space (person‑in‑context). The general noun space \(H_{n}\) is layered across these factors, because nouns may be persons, places, programs, events, organizations, or documents.

A situation in Ms. Allis is thus not merely a person, a place, or a program; it is a coupled state in which a person or other noun appears in a particular application context, at a particular time, within a particular spatial and jurisdictional frame. People space and general noun space are therefore components of a governed multidimensional state, not isolated modules detached from application, geography, or time.

## 47.2 People and Noun Space Without Surveillance

People and noun spaces must support contextual reasoning while explicitly refusing ambient surveillance.

Person‑linked state is legitimate only when tied to purpose, consent, role, time, and lawful authority. Noun‑linked state (about programs, places, events, and other subjects) is legitimate only when subject to similar constraints and kept from drifting into dossier‑style accumulation. The architecture rejects the idea that Ms. Allis should capture and retain every observable trace simply because it can. Non‑surveillance is not achieved by hiding that people or programs exist; it is achieved by limiting what is inferred, what is retained, and what is disclosed.

Within \(H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}\), the system may represent “what, where, when, person‑in‑context and subject‑in‑context.” That internal representation must remain bounded by sandbox rules, retention rules, and projection rules that preserve sovereignty. Person‑aware and subject‑aware intelligence is permissible only as governed‑state intelligence.

## 47.3 Sandboxed People‑ and Noun‑Space Inference

People‑space and noun‑space inferences are sandboxed.

When Ms. Allis reasons about a person‑in‑context or noun‑in‑context inside the joint tensor space, that reasoning starts as gated internal‑state operations. A candidate joint state—combining application meaning, spatial grounding, temporal position, and person or subject context—may be constructed, compared, and tested within a sandbox domain, where internal reasoning is reversible and not yet admitted as durable personal memory, durable subject memory, or broad system knowledge.

Sandboxed people‑ and noun‑space inference may include:

- tentative hypotheses about person‑in‑context or subject‑in‑context needs or risks  
- provisional associations among persons, places, programs, and times  
- internal evaluations of role fit, eligibility, or validity under current constraints  

These are candidates, not facts. They are not written directly into identity records, noun records, or durable memory. The governing principle is provisionality: Ms. Allis may think about persons and other nouns in context, but may not treat every thought as a persistent observation.

---

## 47.4 Constitutional and Role‑Gated Promotion

A people‑space or noun‑space inference crosses from sandbox to durable state only under constitutional and role‑gated constraints.

Promotion from sandbox candidate to accepted person‑linked or subject‑linked knowledge requires that a governance path be satisfied, including:

- constitutional guardrails on which person‑ or subject‑related state may be retained  
- guardian or policy review of sensitive identity‑linked or program‑linked inferences  
- role legitimacy checks for the actor or subsystem requesting promotion  
- consent and purpose conditions for retention and use  
- temporal and locality constraints aligned with \(H_{t}\) and \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\)  

If these conditions fail, the candidate remains non‑promoted, remains limited in scope, or is discarded. A plausible inference is not entitled to become durable people or noun memory unless governance explicitly admits it; knowledge enters through constitutional and role‑gated promotion, not through raw computational availability.

## 47.5 Ephemeral Reasoning and Durable Memory

Non‑surveillance ethics require a strong separation between ephemeral reasoning and durable memory for people and other nouns.

Ephemeral reasoning inside \(H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}\) may carry:

- short‑lived context for a single interaction  
- provisional interpretations of person‑in‑context or subject‑in‑context events  
- candidate paths explicitly designed to be reversible  

Durable memory, by contrast, is long‑lived and shapes future behavior, personalization, program behavior, and potential disclosure. If every ephemeral people‑ or noun‑space inference were written into durable memory, the system would accumulate unbounded records and drift toward surveillance and uncontrolled tracking.

To prevent this, the architecture maintains:

- a sandbox layer where people‑ and noun‑space reasoning is ephemeral by default  
- promotion gates that selectively admit person‑linked and subject‑linked conclusions into durable memory  
- retention policies able to prune, limit, or anonymize even promoted state  

Ms. Allis is designed not to remember more about a person or governed subject than governance legitimately permits.

## 47.6 Retention and Promotion Discipline

Retention discipline in people and noun space follows promotion discipline.

A person‑linked or subject‑linked conclusion becomes durable only when:

1. It is formed within gated internal‑state tensor reasoning.  
2. It passes constitutional, guardian, and role‑gated checks.  
3. It is admitted into a per‑user, per‑program, or per‑community subspace under identity‑ and subject‑focused retention rules.

Otherwise, it remains ephemeral or staged. The architecture distinguishes:

- ephemeral people‑ and noun‑space reasoning in sandbox  
- staged but non‑promoted joint states in the internal tensor domain  
- admitted people‑ and subject‑linked memory within identity‑ and subject‑governed subspaces  

This gradation is part of the non‑surveillance architecture; the system does not treat every possible person‑ or subject‑in‑context state as a record to be kept.

## 47.7 Role‑Gated Disclosure in the Joint Tensor Space

Even when person‑linked or subject‑linked knowledge has been admitted, disclosure remains role‑gated and context‑limited.

The full joint state \(H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}\) with its attached noun space may contain richer information than any single requester should see. The system therefore computes a view tailored to the requesting role and situation, rather than revealing the entire joint state.

A caregiver role might see different aspects of \(H_{p}\) than a system steward. A locality‑bound route may expose different parts of \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) and \(H_{t}\) than a global governance actor. A program steward might see restricted aspects of program nouns without seeing person nouns, while a civic guardian might see aggregated patterns only. Role‑gated disclosure ensures that even admitted person‑ and subject‑in‑context knowledge remains bounded.

The system does not simply store cautiously; it also reveals cautiously.

## 47.8 Projection and Identity Limitation

Disclosure is expressed as a projection over the full joint Hilbert state, including the noun space.

Let \(K\) denote the combined knowledge state in

\[
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}.
\]

Disclosure to a particular requester is not \(K\) itself but a projected view:

\[
\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K),
\]

where \(c\) is consent, \(r\) is role, \(x\) is context, \(t\) is time, \(p\) is purpose, and \(\ell\) is legal authority.

The projection operator \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}\) returns only the portion of the joint state that may be disclosed under these conditions. Even if Ms. Allis holds a richly coupled person‑ and subject‑in‑context state internally, the external view is a governed slice.

Identity‑bearing detail about persons and detail‑bearing subject information may be:

- partially anonymized  
- reduced to aggregated patterns  
- constrained to a time window  
- limited to a specific locality  
- filtered by role appropriateness and purpose necessity  

Identity and subject detail are governed by projection, not by mere possession.

## 47.9 People and Nouns in the Joint “What–Where–When–Person” Frame

Placing people and noun space inside \(H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}\) clarifies how non‑surveillance interacts with the full “what–where–when–person” frame.

- \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) (what): application meaning and service logic are necessary, but do not justify arbitrary person or subject tracing.  
- \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) (where): locality and jurisdiction matter, but do not justify unconstrained location‑based profiling.  
- \(H_{t}\) (when): temporal context matters, but does not justify indefinite retention of all person‑ or subject‑linked events.  
- \(H_{p}\) (person‑in‑context): identity matters, but only within governed, projected views.

The general noun space overlaps these factors. Programs and services live primarily in \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\), places in \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\), events across \(H_{t}\) and \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\), organizations across \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) and \(H_{p}\), and documents across application and governance subspaces.

The non‑surveillance architecture ensures that the joint frame can be used for service and care without becoming a net of observation. The tensor formalism describes how the system understands coupled state; sandboxing and projection describe how the system limits that understanding in practice. Person‑in‑context and subject‑in‑context reasoning are allowed to be real without being totalizing.

## 47.10 People and Noun Space Without Surveillance

Hilbert people and noun space supports:

- person‑ and subject‑aware reasoning in the joint “what–where–when–person” frame  
- sandboxed inference that remains ephemeral unless promoted  
- constitutional and role‑gated promotion for person‑linked and subject‑linked conclusions  
- retention discipline separating ephemeral reasoning from durable memory  
- projection‑based, identity‑limited and subject‑limited disclosure over the full Hilbert state  

Through these constraints, Ms. Allis remains relational and context‑aware without turning interactions into surveillance events. The system is allowed to know enough to serve, but not enough to watch without limit.

This is the meaning of Hilbert people and noun space without surveillance.

## 47.11 As‑Built Person Store and Naming

The relational person store exists as an `hp` schema on a separate `hilbert_people` database, hosted on its own database service. It is not a namespace within a shared database. A separate database provides connection‑level isolation: a role that cannot connect to the database cannot see the schema, regardless of the queries it writes.

Three properties of the as‑built store enforce this chapter’s promise below the application layer:

1. **Anti‑surveillance in data definition.** The `hp` schema comprises a small set of tables whose DDL encodes non‑surveillance rules. Display‑name fields carry comments forbidding legal names; there are no tables shaped as dossiers that would permit accumulating longitudinal records of a person’s movements or associations. A surveillance query against this schema fails not because a policy blocks it but because the tables to answer it do not exist. Retention discipline is made structural: the schema cannot retain what it cannot represent.  
2. **Role scoping below the application.** The application role is granted only on the `hilbert_people.hp` schema and holds no connect rights to the KYC vault database. The least‑knowledge rule of the broader architecture is enforced at the database layer: even a fully compromised application service cannot traverse from person‑appearance context to vaulted identity records, because the traversal is refused at connection time.  
3. **Salt‑blind membership proofs.** Person‑membership checks use BLAKE2b‑based proofs with salt‑blindness enforced structurally, so the system can verify that an appearance belongs to a registered person without the verifying path learning a stable, correlatable identifier. Verification without correlation yields traceability without trackability.

## 47.12 Assertion Path: Answers About People, Not Records About People

The projection operator of Section 47.8 has a concrete instance in the assertion path exercised through the GBIM query router.

The router exposes an `/assert` endpoint that accepts:

- `source_id` identifying an appearance source  
- `subject_ref` describing the subject of the claim  
- `claim_type` describing the kind of assertion  
- `context` documenting the situation  
- `claim_result` indicating the boolean outcome  

The endpoint performs a governed sequence:

1. Resolves `source_id` to `hp.appearance.appearance_id` using a database query against `hilbert_people.hp`.  
2. Derives a deterministic assertion identifier using UUID5 over `(appearance_id, claim_type, context)`.  
3. Writes to `hp.appearance_assertion` using an `INSERT ... ON CONFLICT (id) DO UPDATE` pattern, so repeated calls with the same logical assertion update the same row rather than creating duplicates.

The projection here is minimal: a caller receives a structured predicate result, not a person record. The response confirms whether a claim about a person‑in‑context holds, and records the fact that the question was asked, but does not expose the underlying appearance or suppression events.

Two design consequences follow:

- **Predicate‑first disclosure.** The projection \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)\) reaches its identity‑limiting extreme when the disclosed view collapses to a governed boolean with minimal context. The assertion endpoint shows that a requester’s legitimate need can often be met at that extreme. Releasing a record where a predicate would suffice is over‑disclosure by construction.  
- **Refusal and suppression as governed outputs.** Unsupported or ungoverned claim types are refused, and suppression events record occasions when disclosure is withheld or escalated. A refusal to assert is itself a first‑class epistemic output, leaving a trace that can be audited without exposing private detail.

Repeated assertion calls with the same inputs return the same assertion identifier and maintain a single row keyed by that identifier, demonstrating idempotency in practice: retries do not produce duplicated person‑in‑context records.

## 47.13 Sovereign Person Subspace Pattern

The pattern prescribed in this chapter is exercised by creating sovereign person subspaces inside \(H_{p}\) and corresponding application subspaces inside \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\), with consent flags and commons‑projection settings stored per record.

A per‑person configuration declares:

- subspace names across the joint frame (application, person, temporal, and sandboxed bridge collections)  
- governance posture (public opt‑in, commons projection, sandbox enabled, locality constraints)  
- consent defaults (local‑only, non‑surveillance true, public‑opt‑in false)  

Collections are created and seeded with root records that carry explicit consent fields:

- person roots marked with non‑surveillance `true` and public‑opt‑in `false`  
- application and bridge records carrying commons‑projection disabled  
- conversation events tagged as local‑only at the record level  

Consent is a stored field on every record, not an ambient assumption. Write‑side discipline enforces sovereignty at the moment data enters the system.

Isolation of these person subspaces is validated two ways:

- **Static inspection.** A repository‑wide search confirms that person‑specific collection names appear only in their own configuration, not in commons aggregators, civic pipelines, or projection paths. No code exists that would move these subspace records into shared or public collections.  
- **Dynamic aggregation checks.** Commons aggregation runs report zero vectors projected from any source while sovereign subspaces hold records, and community commons remain unchanged. The only pipeline that lawfully aggregates person‑linked state—the k‑anonymized commons described elsewhere—does not see these sovereign subspaces at all.

Adding a person to \(H_{p}\) thus means creating a sovereign subspace with aggregation disabled by default. Participation in any aggregate view is per‑record, opt‑in, and stored. Conversational memory partitions on the \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) side apply the same sovereignty pattern, so a person’s application memory and person state are separately governed.

## 47.14 Implementation Status (July 2026)

In the demonstrated / not‑yet‑demonstrated discipline of the broader design:

**Demonstrated:**

- An `hp` schema on a separate `hilbert_people` database, with DDL‑level anti‑surveillance (forbidden legal names, absence of dossier‑shaped tables) and application roles scoped to the `hp` schema only.  
- Role isolation between `hilbert_people` and the KYC vault database at the connection layer, preventing traversal from appearance context to vaulted identity without explicit governance.  
- Salt‑blind BLAKE2b membership proofs verifying person membership without disclosing stable identifiers.  
- An assertion path through the GBIM query router: supported predicates affirmed, unsupported claim types refused, unsupported‑but‑plausible predicates answered `false` rather than inferred, with assertion events persisted in `hp.appearance_assertion`.  
- Idempotent assertion writes keyed by deterministic UUID5 identifiers over `(appearance_id, claim_type, context)`, using `INSERT ... ON CONFLICT (id) DO UPDATE` to ensure retries do not produce duplicate rows.  
- Suppression events recorded as first‑class governed outputs, capturing occasions when disclosure is withheld or escalated.  
- Sovereign person subspace creation and isolation, with per‑record consent flags and static / dynamic verification that these subspaces do not feed shared or public collections.

**Not yet demonstrated:**

- Full projection enforcement at query time: consent and role are enforced per record and per collection, but purpose, time‑window, and legal‑authority conditions are not yet computed on read.  
- Sandboxed people‑space inference cycles that produce person‑in‑context candidate conclusions and flow them through constitutional promotion gates into durable memory.  
- Retention policies beyond consent flags: pruning, time‑bounded expiry, and anonymization of already‑promoted person state.  
- Population at scale: civic collections hold limited records and appearance pipelines have processed test‑grade volume rather than community‑scale corpora.

## 47.15 Closing Statement

Hilbert people and noun space in Ms. Allis is the governed subject component of the joint tensor state \(H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}\), representing “what, where, when, person‑in‑context and subject‑in‑context” under governance.

People‑ and noun‑space inferences occur inside sandboxed internal‑state operations, are promoted only under constitutional and role‑gated constraints, and are separated from durable memory unless properly admitted. By expressing disclosure as the projection \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)\) and limiting identity and subject detail accordingly, this chapter anchors the non‑surveillance promise in concrete sandbox, retention, and projection architecture.

The enforcement points are visible: a schema that does not support dossier queries, roles that cannot reach the vault, assertion paths that answer predicates instead of releasing records, sovereign person subspaces with consent fields stored and isolation validated, and idempotent assertion writes that can be retried safely. Hilbert people and noun space thus remains a governed, subject‑aware layer of Ms. Allis’s intelligence rather than an instrument of ambient surveillance.
