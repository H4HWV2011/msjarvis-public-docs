## 47.0 Overview

Hilbert people space is the part of Ms. Allis’s state in which persons, roles, communities, and identity-linked context are represented for governed service rather than for monitoring.

The central promise of this chapter is that person-in-context reasoning does not turn Ms. Allis into a surveillance instrument. That promise is grounded in sandbox behavior, retention discipline, and mathematically explicit disclosure limits. People-space inferences are sandboxed, promoted only under constitutional and role-gated constraints, and separated from durable personal memory unless governance explicitly admits them. Disclosure is computed as a projection over the full Hilbert state, limiting identity to what a situation legitimately requires.

Sections 47.1 through 47.10 state the non-surveillance architecture as designed. Sections 47.11 through 47.14 record what is verified as built as of July 2026: where the person store lives and how its schema enforces the promise in data definition rather than in policy prose, an assertion path that answers questions about people without releasing records about them, and a sovereign person subspace instantiated under this chapter’s rules with its isolation from shared and public pipelines verified. A non-surveillance chapter that cannot show its enforcement points is a privacy policy, not an architecture.

## 47.1 People Space in the Full Hilbert State

The people space exists inside a larger Hilbert structure:

\[
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\]

where:

- \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) is the application-facing semantic state (what);
- \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) is the spatial body (where);
- \(H_{t}\) is the temporal axis (when);
- \(H_{p}\) is the people space (person-in-context).

This combined tensor product represents person-in-context as a joint state across what, where, when, and who. A situation in Ms. Allis is not only a person, nor only a place, nor only a program. It is a coupled state in which a person is present in a particular application context, at a particular time, within a particular spatial and jurisdictional frame.

Hilbert people space therefore lives inside this joint structure. It is not isolated from application, geography, or time. It is the person component of a governed multidimensional state.

## 47.2 People Space Without Surveillance

The people space must support contextual reasoning while explicitly refusing ambient surveillance.

Person-linked state is legitimate only when it is tied to purpose, consent, role, time, and lawful authority. The architecture rejects the idea that Ms. Allis should accumulate and retain every observable trace about a person simply because that trace could be stored. Non-surveillance is not achieved by hiding the presence of people; it is achieved by limiting what is inferred, what is retained, and what is disclosed.

Within \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\), the system may represent “what, where, when, person-in-context.” That internal representation must remain bounded by sandbox rules, retention rules, and projection rules that preserve sovereignty.

Person-aware intelligence is permissible only as governed-state intelligence.

## 47.3 Sandboxed People-Space Inference

People-space inferences are sandboxed.

When Ms. Allis reasons about a person-in-context inside the joint tensor space, that reasoning begins within gated internal-state operations. A candidate joint state—combining application meaning, spatial grounding, temporal position, and person context—may be constructed, compared, and tested. This occurs inside a sandbox domain, where internal reasoning is reversible and not yet admitted as durable personal memory or broad system knowledge.

Sandboxed people-space inference may include:

- tentative hypotheses about person-in-context needs or risks;
- provisional associations between persons, places, and times;
- internal evaluations of role fit or eligibility under current constraints.

These remain candidates. They are not written directly into identity records or durable personal memory. The people space is governed first by provisionality: Ms. Allis is allowed to think about persons in context, but not to commit every thought as if it were fact.

Internal inference does not automatically become persistent observation.

## 47.4 Constitutional and Role-Gated Promotion

A people-space inference may cross the boundary from sandbox to durable state only under constitutional and role-gated constraints.

Promotion from sandboxed candidate to accepted person-linked knowledge requires that the relevant governance path be satisfied. That path may include:

- constitutional guardrails on what kinds of person-related state may be retained;
- guardian or policy review of sensitive identity-linked inferences;
- role legitimacy checks for the actor or subsystem requesting promotion;
- consent conditions and purpose limitation for retention and use;
- temporal and locality constraints corresponding to \(H_{t}\) and \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\).

If those conditions are not met, the candidate remains non-promoted, limited in scope, or discarded. A plausible inference is not entitled to become part of durable personal memory unless governance explicitly admits it.

People-space knowledge is admitted through constitutional and role-gated promotion, not through raw computational availability.

## 47.5 Ephemeral Reasoning and Durable Personal Memory

Non-surveillance ethics require a strong separation between ephemeral reasoning and durable personal memory.

Ephemeral reasoning in \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\) may contain:

- short-lived context needed to handle a single interaction;
- provisional interpretations of person-in-context events;
- candidate paths that are explicitly meant to be reversible.

Durable personal memory is long-lived. It shapes future behavior, personalization, and potential disclosure. If every ephemeral people-space inference were automatically written into durable memory, the system would accumulate unbounded person records and drift toward surveillance.

To prevent that, the architecture maintains:

- a sandbox layer where person-in-context reasoning is ephemeral by default;
- promotion gates that selectively admit person-linked conclusions to durable memory;
- retention policies that can prune, limit, or anonymize even promoted state.

Ms. Allis is designed not to remember more about a person than governance legitimately permits.

---

## 47.6 Retention and Promotion Discipline

Retention discipline in people space follows promotion discipline.

A person-linked conclusion becomes durable only when:

1. It has been formed within gated internal-state tensor reasoning;
2. It has passed constitutional, guardian, and role-gated checks;
3. It has been admitted into a per-user or per-community subspace under identity-focused retention rules.

Otherwise, it remains ephemeral or staged. The architecture distinguishes among:

- ephemeral people-space reasoning in sandbox;
- staged but non-promoted joint states in the internal tensor domain;
- admitted person-linked memory within identity-governed subspaces of \(H_{p}\).

This gradation is part of the non-surveillance architecture. The system does not treat every possible person-in-context state as a record to be kept.

## 47.7 Role-Gated Disclosure in the Joint Tensor Space

Even when person-linked knowledge has been admitted, disclosure remains role-gated and context-limited.

The full joint state \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\) may contain richer information than any single requester should see. The system therefore computes a view tailored to the requesting role and situation, rather than revealing the entire joint state.

A caregiver role might see different aspects of \(H_{p}\) than a system steward. A locality-bound route may expose different parts of \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) and \(H_{t}\) than a global governance actor. Role-gated disclosure ensures that even admitted person-in-context knowledge remains bounded.

The system does not simply store cautiously; it also reveals cautiously.

## 47.8 Projection and Identity Limitation

Disclosure is expressed as a projection over the full joint Hilbert state.

Let \(K\) denote the combined knowledge state in

\[
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}.
\]

Disclosure to a particular requester is not \(K\) itself. It is a projected view:

\[
\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)
\]

where:

- \(c\) is consent;
- \(r\) is role;
- \(x\) is context;
- \(t\) is time;
- \(p\) is purpose;
- \(\ell\) is legal authority.

The projection operator \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}\) returns only the portion of the joint state that may be disclosed under these conditions. This formalism backs non-surveillance with explicit disclosure mathematics. Even if Ms. Allis holds a richly coupled person-in-context state internally, the external view is limited to a governed slice.

Identity-bearing detail may be:

- partially anonymized;
- reduced to aggregated patterns;
- constrained to a time window;
- limited to a specific locality;
- filtered by role appropriateness and purpose necessity.

Identity is governed by projection, not by possession.

## 47.9 People Space in the Joint “What–Where–When–Person” Frame

Placing people space inside \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\) clarifies how non-surveillance interacts with the full “what–where–when–person” frame.

- \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) (what): application meaning and service logic are necessary, but do not justify arbitrary person tracing.
- \(H_{\mathrm{geo}}\) (where): locality and jurisdiction matter, but do not justify location-based profiling outside purpose.
- \(H_{t}\) (when): temporal context matters, but does not justify indefinite retention of all person-linked events.
- \(H_{p}\) (person-in-context): identity matters, but only within governed, projected views.

The non-surveillance architecture ensures that the joint frame can be used for service and care without becoming a net of observation. The tensor formalism describes how the system understands coupled state. Sandbox and projection describe how the system limits that understanding in practice.

Person-in-context reasoning is allowed to be real without being totalizing.

## 47.10 People Space Without Surveillance

Hilbert people space supports:

- person-aware reasoning in the joint “what–where–when–person” frame;
- sandboxed inference that remains ephemeral unless promoted;
- constitutional and role-gated promotion for person-linked conclusions;
- retention discipline separating ephemeral reasoning from durable personal memory;
- projection-based, identity-limited disclosure over the full Hilbert state.

Through these constraints, Ms. Allis remains relational and context-aware without turning interactions into surveillance events. The system is allowed to know enough to serve, but not enough to watch without limit.

This is the meaning of Hilbert people space without surveillance.

## 47.11 As-Built Person Store and Naming

The relational person store exists as an hp schema on a separate hilbert_people database, hosted on its own database service. It is not a namespace within a shared database. A separate database provides connection-level isolation: a role that cannot connect to the database cannot see the schema, whatever query it writes.

Three properties of the as-built store enforce this chapter’s promise below the application layer:

1. **Anti-surveillance in data definition.** The hp schema comprises a small set of tables whose DDL encodes non-surveillance rules. Display-name fields carry comments forbidding legal names; there are no tables shaped as dossiers that would permit accumulating longitudinal records of a person’s movements or associations. A surveillance query against this schema fails not because a policy blocks it but because the tables to answer it were never created. Retention discipline is made structural: the schema cannot retain what it cannot represent.
2. **Role scoping below the application.** The application role is granted only on the hilbert_people.hp schema and holds no connect rights to the KYC vault database. The least-knowledge rule of the broader architecture is enforced at the database layer: even a fully compromised application service cannot traverse from person-appearance context to vaulted identity records, because the traversal is refused at connection time.
3. **Salt-blind membership proofs.** Person-membership checks use BLAKE2b-based proofs with salt-blindness enforced structurally, so the system can verify that an appearance belongs to a registered person without the verifying path learning a stable, correlatable identifier. Verification without correlation is traceability without trackability.

## 47.12 Assertion Path: Answers About People, Not Records About People

The projection operator of Section 47.8 has a concrete instance in the assertion path exercised through the GBIM query router.

The router exposes an `/assert` endpoint that accepts:

- `source_id` identifying an appearance source;
- `subject_ref` describing the subject of the claim;
- `claim_type` describing the kind of assertion;
- `context` documenting the situation;
- `claim_result` indicating the boolean outcome.

The endpoint performs a governed sequence:

1. Resolves `source_id` to `hp.appearance.appearance_id` using a database query against hilbert_people.hp.
2. Derives a deterministic assertion identifier using UUID5 over `(appearance_id, claim_type, context)`.
3. Writes to `hp.appearance_assertion` using:

   ```text
   INSERT ... ON CONFLICT (id) DO UPDATE
   ```

   so repeated calls with the same logical assertion update the same row rather than creating duplicates.

The projection here is minimal: a caller receives a structured predicate result, not a person record. The response confirms whether a claim about a person-in-context holds, and records the fact that the question was asked, but does not expose the underlying appearance or suppression events.

Two design consequences follow:

- **Predicate-first disclosure.** The projection \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)\) attains its identity-limiting extreme when the disclosed view collapses to a governed boolean with minimal context. The assertion endpoint demonstrates that a requester’s legitimate need can often be satisfied at that extreme. Releasing a record where a predicate would do is over-disclosure by construction.
- **Refusal and suppression as governed outputs.** Unsupported or ungoverned claim types are refused, and suppression events record occasions when disclosure is withheld or escalated. A refusal to assert is itself a first-class epistemic output, leaving a trace that can be audited without exposing private detail.

Repeated assertion calls with the same inputs return the same assertion identifier and maintain a single row keyed by that identifier, demonstrating idempotency in practice: retries do not produce duplicated person-in-context records.

## 47.13 Sovereign Person Subspace Pattern

The pattern prescribed in this chapter is exercised by creating sovereign person subspaces inside \(H_{p}\) and corresponding application subspaces inside \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\), with consent flags and commons-projection settings stored per record.

A per-person configuration declares:

- subspace names across the joint frame (application, person, temporal, and sandboxed bridge collections);
- governance posture (public opt-in, commons projection, sandbox enabled, locality constraints);
- consent defaults (local-only, non-surveillance true, public-opt-in false).

Collections are created and seeded with root records that carry explicit consent fields:

- person roots marked with non-surveillance true and public-opt-in false;
- application and bridge records carrying commons-projection disabled;
- conversation events tagged as local-only at the record level.

Consent is a stored field on every record, not an ambient assumption. Write-side discipline enforces sovereignty at the moment data enters the system.

Isolation of these person subspaces is validated two ways:

- **Static inspection.** A repository-wide search confirms that person-specific collection names appear only in their own configuration, not in commons aggregators, civic pipelines, or projection paths. No code exists that would move these subspace records into shared or public collections.
- **Dynamic aggregation checks.** Commons aggregation runs report zero vectors projected from any source while sovereign subspaces hold records, and community commons remain unchanged. The only pipeline that lawfully aggregates person-linked state—the k-anonymized commons described elsewhere—does not see these sovereign subspaces at all.

Adding a person to \(H_{p}\) thus means creating a sovereign subspace with opt-in to aggregation disabled by default. Participation in any aggregate view is per-record, opt-in, and stored. Conversational memory partitions on the \(H_{\mathrm{App}}\) side apply the same sovereignty pattern, so a person’s application memory and person state are separately governed.

## 47.14 Implementation Status (July 2026)

In the demonstrated/not-yet-demonstrated discipline of the broader design:

**Demonstrated:**

- An hp schema on a separate hilbert_people database, with DDL-level anti-surveillance (forbidden legal names, absence of dossier-shaped tables) and application roles scoped to the hp schema only.
- Role isolation between hilbert_people and the KYC vault database at the connection layer, preventing traversal from appearance context to vaulted identity without explicit governance.
- Salt-blind BLAKE2b membership proofs verifying person membership without disclosing stable identifiers.
- An assertion path through the GBIM query router: supported predicates affirmed, unsupported claim types refused, unsupported-but-plausible predicates answered false rather than inferred, with assertion events persisted in hp.appearance_assertion.
- Idempotent assertion writes keyed by deterministic UUID5 identifiers over `(appearance_id, claim_type, context)`, using `INSERT ... ON CONFLICT (id) DO UPDATE` to ensure retries do not produce duplicate rows.
- Suppression events recorded as first-class governed outputs, capturing occasions when disclosure is withheld or escalated.
- Sovereign person subspace creation and isolation, with per-record consent flags and static/dynamic verification that these subspaces do not feed shared or public collections.

**Not yet demonstrated:**

- Full projection enforcement at query time: consent and role are enforced per record and per collection, but purpose, time-window, and legal-authority conditions are not yet computed on read.
- Sandboxed people-space inference cycles that produce person-in-context candidate conclusions and flow them through constitutional promotion gates into durable memory.
- Retention policies beyond consent flags: pruning, time-bounded expiry, and anonymization of already-promoted person state.
- Population at scale: civic collections hold limited records and appearance pipelines have processed test-grade volume rather than community-scale corpora.

## 47.15 Closing Statement

Hilbert people space in Ms. Allis is the \(H_{p}\) component of the joint tensor state \(
H_{\mathrm{App}} \otimes H_{\mathrm{geo}} \otimes H_{t} \otimes H_{p}
\), representing “what, where, when, person-in-context” under governance.

People-space inferences occur inside sandboxed internal-state operations, are promoted only under constitutional and role-gated constraints, and are separated from durable personal memory unless properly admitted. By expressing disclosure as the projection \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)\) and limiting identity accordingly, this chapter anchors the non-surveillance promise in concrete sandbox, retention, and projection architecture.

The enforcement points are visible: a schema that does not support dossier queries, roles that cannot reach the vault, an assertion path that answers predicates instead of releasing records, sovereign person subspaces with consent fields stored and isolation validated, and idempotent assertion writes that can be retried safely. Hilbert people space therefore remains a governed, person-aware layer of Ms. Allis’s intelligence, not an instrument of ambient surveillance.
