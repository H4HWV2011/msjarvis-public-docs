## 45.0 Overview

H_geo is the spatial Hilbert body of H_App. It is the structured domain in which place, geometry, jurisdiction, infrastructure, and other spatial relations become part of Ms. Allis’s active semantic state. H_geo does not function as a free-floating map layer or a passive store of coordinates. It is the spatial form of governed semantic presence inside the application-facing Hilbert architecture.

This chapter treats spatial knowledge as a governed state rather than a raw possession. Spatial inferences, candidate updates, and cross-domain alignments may be computed or staged through sandbox reasoning, but they acquire broader authority only after the required gates pass. H_geo therefore belongs simultaneously to geography, cognition, and governance.

As with Chapters 42 through 44, this revision separates design from audit. Sections 45.1 through 45.9 state what the spatial body is and how spatial knowledge is admitted. Sections 45.10 through 45.13 record what is verified as built as of this writing: a three-grain West Virginia vertical slice through the full governed loop, an enforced coverage vocabulary with an honest account of how it once drifted, and a geometry defect discovered and gated in the same week — recorded here because a spatial body that cannot audit its own coordinate systems has no claim to governed-state semantics.

## 45.1 The Meaning of H_geo

H_geo is the spatial body through which Ms. Allis represents land, routes, boundaries, infrastructures, localities, and geographically grounded relationships. In Hilbert terms, it is the spatial subspace of H_App in which place-linked entities and relations are arranged so that they can be compared, composed, measured, and reasoned over as part of a larger semantic whole.

This means H_geo is not limited to geometry in the narrow sense. It includes the semantic significance of geography: counties, communities, parcels, service regions, jurisdictional layers, boundaries of authority, infrastructural adjacency, and the relation between physical place and governed action. A coordinate becomes part of H_geo only when it is incorporated into a meaningful spatial state.

The result is a spatial body rather than a flat map. H_geo gives Ms. Allis a way to hold location-linked meaning inside a formal structure that can participate in reasoning, validation, and promotion.

---

## 45.2 Spatial Knowledge as Governed State

Spatial knowledge in H_geo is governed-state knowledge.

This means a spatial claim, inference, or update does not become authoritative merely because a geospatial service computed it or because a reasoning cycle produced it. Candidate spatial states may be generated, compared, or tested inside the sandbox, but they are treated as provisional until they pass the relevant gate sequence. A staged routing inference, a proposed boundary interpretation, a local infrastructure linkage, or a jurisdiction-sensitive conclusion remains a candidate state until the governance process allows it to cross into promoted system state.

This principle is essential because geography carries consequences. A spatial representation may affect benefits routing, infrastructure interpretation, land-linked records, local resource reasoning, or jurisdictional accountability. For that reason, H_geo must preserve the difference between computed possibility and governed acceptance.

Spatial knowledge is therefore not just stored; it is admitted.

## 45.3 Sandbox Spatial Reasoning

Spatial Hilbert updates and inferences are computed or staged through sandbox reasoning.

When Ms. Allis performs spatial reasoning, she may form candidate linkages among geometries, infrastructure records, community features, administrative layers, and spatially entangled semantic context. Those operations belong first to the sandbox domain, where reasoning remains reversible and does not yet alter authoritative state. A sandbox cycle may generate a candidate spatial update, propose an interpretation of a place-linked condition, or assemble a new spatial inference path across multiple services.

At this stage, the output is a staged spatial state rather than a committed one. It may be held in ephemeral reasoning structures, queued for validation, or compared against current spatial and semantic constraints. The important point is that H_geo is not updated by mere production of a candidate. Spatial transformation is first explored under sandbox conditions.

This makes H_geo compatible with authority-preserving reasoning. The system is free to think spatially before it is allowed to commit spatially.

---

## 45.4 Promotion After Gates Pass

A spatial update becomes part of authoritative H_geo only after the required gates pass.

If a sandbox cycle produces a candidate spatial inference, that candidate must still satisfy the broader governed path: truth-relevant checks where applicable, guardian and constitutional requirements, provenance and coherence review, privacy and security conditions, and any domain-specific spatial constraints. Only then may the candidate cross from provisional staging into promoted spatial state.

This matters because spatial knowledge often carries hidden authority. A route classification, locality linkage, parcel relation, or infrastructure interpretation may appear technical while actually changing the effective way the system understands a place or acts upon it. Promotion gates therefore serve as the boundary between exploratory spatial reasoning and accepted spatial knowledge.

The architecture preserves this distinction by requiring that spatial inference be staged first and admitted later. H_geo becomes stable through governance, not through raw computational momentum.

## 45.5 GeoDB and Infrastructure Cross-Links

H_geo is not isolated from the rest of the system. It is the spatial body that links the Hilbert architecture to GeoDB, infrastructure representations, and the broader place-based service environment.

GeoDB provides the grounded spatial substrate for many of the entities and relations that H_geo organizes semantically. Infrastructure chapters supply the material and civic referents through which spatial reasoning becomes actionable: bridges, facilities, routes, parcels, utility-linked geography, and other place-bound structures. H_geo is where those grounded records participate in the semantic and governed life of the system.

This cross-linking matters because it prevents the spatial chapter from being read as abstract geometry alone. H_geo is where semantic geography meets database grounding and infrastructure reality. It is thus both representational and operational.

As of this writing, that grounding is concrete: the West Virginia GIS corpus is fully imported, catalogued, and source-mapped in the relational system of record, with every layer registered in a governed catalog carrying its category, source system, jurisdiction, spatial grain, authority class, and access class. The catalog is not documentation about the corpus; it is a foreign-key constraint on it — representation and identity tables cannot reference a layer the catalog does not govern.

## 45.6 Locality Restriction

H_geo must also respect locality restriction.

Not every spatial fact should be equally visible, movable, or actionable across every part of the system. Some spatial knowledge is local by design. It belongs to a specific county, jurisdiction, neighborhood, service region, or place-bound governance context. Locality restriction means that place-linked knowledge should remain governed by the relevant locality, rather than being treated as universally portable simply because it has been represented in a formal spatial structure.

This principle applies both to disclosure and to reasoning scope. A candidate spatial inference that is valid within one jurisdiction may not automatically generalize beyond it. A local infrastructure pattern may be meaningful for one place while inappropriate to apply elsewhere. H_geo therefore preserves spatial intelligence by honoring locality rather than flattening it.

Locality restriction is not a limitation of intelligence. It is part of what makes spatial intelligence trustworthy.

---

## 45.7 Jurisdictional Boundaries

Jurisdictional boundaries are part of the semantics of H_geo, not merely external metadata.

A place is not fully represented if its governing boundaries are ignored. Counties, municipalities, districts, service territories, state lines, and other jurisdictional divisions shape what actions are permitted, what rules apply, and how spatial conclusions should be interpreted. H_geo therefore includes not only location and adjacency, but also the jurisdictional context that determines the authority attached to spatial knowledge.

This is especially important for governed-state semantics. A spatial conclusion may be geometrically plausible yet jurisdictionally invalid, or locally accurate yet institutionally inapplicable. For that reason, the meaning of a spatial state includes the boundary conditions under which it can be used, disclosed, or promoted.

H_geo is thus a body of place-with-authority, not only place-with-shape.

---

## 45.8 Governed-State Semantics

The governed-state semantics of H_geo explain how spatial knowledge acquires standing inside Ms. Allis.

A spatial state becomes governed when it is not only representable but also admitted under the relevant authority conditions. This includes locality fit, jurisdictional appropriateness, cross-domain coherence, and passage through the general promotion structure. Governed-state semantics therefore distinguish between at least three spatial conditions:

- computed spatial possibility;
- staged spatial candidate;
- promoted spatial knowledge.

This distinction is crucial for the architecture. It allows the system to reason boldly inside the sandbox without collapsing every inference into accepted geography. It also ensures that spatial authority respects both formal review and the real-world boundary conditions of place.

H_geo is strongest when it preserves this gradation rather than pretending every computed map relation is already an accepted fact.

## 45.9 Spatial Coherence and Cross-Domain Fit

Because H_geo is part of H_App, spatial state must also fit with the broader semantic architecture.

A candidate spatial update may need to align not only with geometry and locality but also with memory, governance, infrastructure interpretation, community context, and other active domains. A spatially elegant inference that fractures these linked domains may remain unfit for promotion. Conversely, a candidate that preserves cross-domain coherence while remaining faithful to locality and jurisdiction may be a strong candidate for governed admission.

This shows why H_geo cannot be treated as a mere GIS annex. It is a Hilbert body, meaning its spatial structure participates in the same broader economy of coherence, validation, and governed promotion that shapes the rest of H_App.

Spatial reasoning is therefore not a side task. It is one of the central ways that place enters the authority structure of the system.

## 45.10 The West Virginia Vertical Slice at Three Grains

The governed-state semantics of this chapter are no longer specification alone. As of July 13, 2026, the West Virginia corpus has been taken through the complete governed loop — canonical identity, representation bridge, feature hashing, coverage computation, versioned belief snapshot, governed prose rendering, vector publication, and retrieval with reverse trace — at three spatial grains:

- **55 counties**, with 220 physical representations (four per county across source variants), published as a governed collection of exactly 55 documents at snapshot version 2;
- **546 census tracts**, one-to-one after the representation bridge was repaired by removing short-form identifier orphans, published as 546 documents at snapshot version 2;
- **1,639 census block groups**, published as 1,639 documents at version 1 — verified against the relational source at 1,639 of 1,639 with zero missing and zero unexpected identifiers.

The block-group grain is the most instructive of the three, because it was not built fresh — it was *brought into compliance*. The block-group snapshot table had been rebuilt at some point and had silently lost the governance contract the county and tract spines satisfy: its version column had reverted to a bare integer, and it carried no canonical belief document and no verification state. Its belief hashes hashed something, but not a canonical document, which means they could not reverse-trace. The remediation aligned the schema with the county convention — versioned text identifiers, a canonical belief document as structured data, a verification state on every row, and the hash recomputed as the digest of the canonical document itself — while retaining all version-1 rows unchanged. History is preserved; the contract is restored.

The lesson generalizes: a governance family name is not a governance contract. Every new grain admitted to H_geo must be tested against the contract itself — versioned snapshots, canonical hashable documents, verification states, and reverse-traceability — not merely named consistently with the grains that satisfy it.

## 45.11 Coverage Honesty and the Vocabulary Guardrail

Governed spatial belief requires the system to speak its ignorance as carefully as its knowledge. The coverage subsystem enforces this at every grain.

Each entity–layer pair carries a coverage state drawn from a fixed vocabulary: **present** (features verified at this location), **confirmed_none** (the layer loaded and verifiably contains no features here), **unknown** (coverage could not be resolved), and **missing_source** (the source layer itself is absent — a known condition, distinct from an unresolvable one). The distinction that carries the most epistemic weight is unknown versus confirmed_none: a layer that failed to project and a layer that was verified absent must never produce the same silence. The block-group belief renderer states this in the governed prose itself — unknown coverage is enumerated and explicitly marked as not to be interpreted as verified absence — matching the honesty discipline the county renderer established.

Two audit findings from the block-group work belong in the permanent record:

1. **The vocabulary must be a constraint, not a convention.** The county and tract coverage tables carry a database CHECK constraint on the coverage vocabulary. The block-group table had lost its constraint, and the vocabulary drifted on a subsequent write. The constraint has been restored, so a future nonconforming write is rejected rather than absorbed. A governance vocabulary enforced only by convention drifts on the first unsupervised write; the guardrail must live in the schema, the same lesson Chapter 43 draws for its status lifecycle.
2. **Presence is not signal.** With 151 feature-bearing layers computed across 1,639 block groups, a layer present in essentially every block group — the state boundary, county boundaries, statewide administrative overlays — is telling you that a block group is inside West Virginia, which is containment context, not local infrastructure. The layer-role taxonomy therefore classifies each layer from the coverage data itself: **containment** (near-universal presence *and* boundary-class in the governed catalog), **ubiquitous_context** (near-universal presence without boundary standing — watersheds, statewide overlays, overlapping administrative geographies), and **feature** (genuine local signal). Rendered belief text groups feature signal by catalog category with counts, keeps containment as a short context clause, and never lets ubiquity masquerade as information. The category guard matters: universality alone misclassifies overlapping geographies as containment, so role assignment requires both the presence threshold and the catalog's boundary classification.

## 45.12 Worked Lesson: The Geometry-Column Defect

One defect discovered during the block-group work belongs in this chapter as an epistemic event, in the same discipline Chapter 52 applies to its recovery narrative.

The block-group representation table was found to carry three geometry columns: one labeled as geographic coordinates (NAD83) while actually holding projected coordinates in meters — a coordinate-system lie embedded in the schema itself; one correctly declared in the projected system (UTM Zone 17N), spatially indexed, and authoritative; and one an unindexed duplicate of the authoritative column, added by a session that did not know the authoritative column already existed.

The mislabeled column is the dangerous one. It carries the default name every query reaches for first, and it hands back projected meters labeled as degrees: every transform computed from it is invalid, and every spatial join against it is silently wrong. Silent wrongness is precisely what governed-state semantics exist to prevent — a promoted spatial belief whose centroid was computed off a mislabeled column would carry full governance metadata around a false coordinate.

The remediation is deliberately gated rather than automatic: prove the duplicate columns are spatially identical, grep every script that references the table, repoint the publication path to the authoritative column, and only then drop the duplicates — with the mislabeled column dropped last, so that anything still referencing it breaks loudly instead of continuing to compute quietly wrong answers. Loud breakage is the desired failure mode; the silence was the defect.

The defect also forced a policy clarification the earlier text left ambiguous. The corpus-wide import standard is NAD83 (EPSG:4269) at the ingestion layer, but grains are normalized to projected systems where computation requires it — the block-group spine is normalized to UTM Zone 17N (EPSG:26917), recorded per-document as the source spatial reference in publication metadata, with centroids and bounding boxes projected to WGS84 (EPSG:4326) for retrieval-facing coordinates. The policy is therefore per-layer and *declared*, not global and assumed: every published document names its source reference system, so no consumer has to guess what the coordinates mean. A spatial Hilbert body that cannot state its own reference frames does not know where its beliefs are.

In the demonstrated/not-yet-demonstrated discipline of Chapter 52:

**Demonstrated:**

- The full governed loop at three grains: 55 counties, 546 tracts, 1,639 block groups, each with canonical identity, representation bridging, coverage computation, versioned snapshots with canonical belief documents and digest hashes, governed prose rendering, and publication as vector collections of exactly the expected cardinality.
- Publication-count verification for the block-group collection: 1,639 relational rows to 1,639 vector documents, zero missing, zero unexpected.

- The layer-role taxonomy (containment, ubiquitous_context, feature) computed from coverage data with the catalog category guard.
- Governed catalog registration as a foreign-key constraint on representation and identity tables.
- Retrieval and reverse trace at the tract grain: an exact-tract query through GIS-RAG resolving to the correct governed document and tracing from vector document identifier back to the source feature in the relational store.

**Not yet demonstrated:**

- The geometry-column cleanup: the equality proof and script grep are prescribed and gated, but the duplicate and mislabeled columns had not been dropped as of this writing, and the publication path's repoint to the authoritative column must be verified in the same change.
- Retrieval-quality validation at the block-group grain equivalent to the tract-grain exact-retrieval test (the count verification passed; the semantic retrieval battery has not been run against the block-group collection).
- Sandbox-staged spatial inference as Section 45.3 describes it: the built loop governs *ingestion and publication* of spatial belief; candidate spatial inferences produced by reasoning cycles do not yet flow through a staged-then-promoted path.
- Locality-restricted disclosure (Section 45.6) as an enforced retrieval condition rather than a design principle.
- Extension beyond West Virginia; the scaling guidance exists, the replication does not.

## 45.14 Closing Statement

H_geo is the spatial Hilbert body of H_App, where place, geometry, infrastructure, locality, and jurisdiction become part of Ms. Allis’s governed semantic state.

Spatial updates and inferences are computed or staged through sandbox reasoning and become authoritative only after the relevant gates pass. By linking H_geo to GeoDB and infrastructure grounding, while preserving locality restriction, jurisdictional boundaries, and governed-state semantics, this chapter defines spatial knowledge as something the system must reason through, validate, and properly admit rather than something it simply possesses.

As of this writing, that definition is load-bearing at three grains of the West Virginia corpus, with the contract violations, vocabulary drift, and coordinate-system defect discovered along the way recorded here rather than repaired in silence. A spatial body earns governed standing not by never being wrong about itself, but by finding out — and saying so in its own text.
