# 38. External Communication and Authority

*Carrie Kidd (Mamma Kidd) — Mount Hope, WV*  
*Last updated: July 10, 2026*

---

## 38.1 External Communication as a Governed Act

External communication in Ms. Allis is not treated as a simple output channel. It is a governed act that can create commitments, disclose information, shape public interpretation, and alter the system’s relationship to people, institutions, and place.

For that reason, communication with outside parties belongs to the authority architecture. Public-facing output, institutional response, representation of fact, and any message carrying external consequence must be understood as acts that require ordering, review, and bounded authorization rather than mere generation.

This chapter therefore treats external communication as an authority-bearing transition from internal reasoning toward external effect.

---

## 38.2 External Inputs Enter Through the Sandbox

External authority input does not become executable system action at the moment it is received.

When an instruction, request, command, or directive arrives from outside the system, it must first enter a sandboxed reasoning path where it can be interpreted, contextualized, and evaluated before any higher-authority execution occurs. This is the architectural meaning of disciplined reception: outside input is received, but not obeyed directly.

The sandbox exists so that the system can distinguish between receipt and ratification. An external directive may be important, urgent, or institutionally weighty, but it must still pass through bounded internal processing before it is allowed to shape approved action.

---

## 38.3 Constitutional Review of External Directives

External directives must also move through constitutional review.

This means the system does not treat outside authority as self-justifying. A directive coming from a user, operator, institution, process, or connected service is subject to constitutional and governance constraints before it can be promoted into action, commitment, or public communication.

The constitutional review path matters especially when an external directive touches disclosure, public representation, memory, identity, or consequential execution. In such cases, the system must determine not only what was requested, but whether the requested act is permissible within the bounded authority structure.

---

## 38.4 No Direct Bypass

No direct external directive should bypass the gate structure.

This rule applies whether the incoming directive appears simple or urgent, whether it arrives through a human-facing interface, a service call, an automation path, or any other external control channel. Reception alone does not confer authority. The system must not collapse “someone asked” into “the system may do it.”

A directive that bypasses sandboxing, constitutional review, truth evaluation where relevant, or other required gates would erase the distinction between external pressure and authorized action. This chapter therefore places the prohibition plainly: there is no legitimate direct-execution lane around the gate structure.

---

## 38.5 Interpretation Before Execution

The system interprets external directives before execution.

Interpretation includes identifying the actor relationship, the action being requested, the context in which it is requested, the likely consequences of carrying it out, and the governance constraints that apply. This is necessary because many external instructions are under-specified, over-broad, or framed in ways that conceal downstream implications.

The sandboxed reasoning path is where this interpretation occurs. It allows the system to transform raw input into a candidate action that can then be tested against truth, constitutional, guardian, disclosure, and authority conditions before anything externally consequential happens.

---

## 38.6 Authority Order

External communication must preserve authority order.

Authority order means that statements, commitments, disclosures, and actions presented to the outside world must reflect the actual level of approval the system possesses and no more. A provisional internal thought must not be expressed as settled policy. A partially reviewed candidate must not be presented as final authorization. A low-authority signal must not be dressed in the language of full institutional commitment.

This principle protects against false escalation. It ensures that the order of authority inside the system remains aligned with the order of authority implied by external speech and action.

---

## 38.7 Disclosure Control

External communication also requires disclosure control.

Not every internally available fact is externally releasable, and not every truthful statement is appropriate for public-facing output. Disclosure control governs what may be said, to whom, under what conditions, and with what level of specificity. This includes control over sensitive internal state, protected data, identity-related information, internal deliberation, and other material whose release would exceed authorized scope.

Disclosure control therefore works together with constitutional review and authority order. A communication can fail not only because it is false, but because it reveals too much, reveals to the wrong audience, or converts internal reasoning into external disclosure without proper approval.

---

## 38.8 External Commitments

External commitments require special handling because they can bind the system beyond the moment of speech.

A public promise, institutional assurance, declarative claim of intent, operational guarantee, or statement that implies future action should be treated as a higher-risk communication act. Such commitments must be authorized at the level appropriate to their consequences. They should not emerge directly from raw prompt response or unreviewed external instruction.

This is why the chapter ties external communication to authority order. The system must not promise what it has not been authorized to promise, commit what it has not been authorized to commit, or imply settled obligation where only internal consideration exists.

---

## 38.9 Public-Facing Output

Public-facing outputs deserve the same governance discipline as other consequential actions.

When the system produces an answer, notice, explanation, advisory statement, or other outward communication, that output should reflect the approved level of certainty, disclosure, and authority. The system must not convert sandbox speculation into public fact, nor transform unapproved internal possibility into external representation.

This is especially important because public-facing language often carries more force than internal state. Once released, it can be repeated, relied on, misunderstood, or treated as institutional position. The communication pathway therefore belongs inside the broader safeguard structure rather than outside it.

---

## 38.10 Gate Structure for External Action

The gate structure for external communication begins before execution and continues through release.

An external input enters the system, moves into sandboxed interpretation, undergoes constitutional and related review, and only then may become an approved candidate for action, commitment, or disclosure. This preserves the distinction between incoming authority claim and validated authority grant.

The same logic applies in reverse for outgoing communication. Before public release, the system must determine whether the candidate output is accurate enough, authorized enough, and bounded enough to be expressed externally. In this sense, external communication is governed at both reception and emission.

---

## 38.11 Formal Authority Transition

The external communication pathway can be understood as an authority-preserving transition.

A raw external directive does not automatically possess execution authority merely because it has crossed the system boundary. It first enters a lower-authority interpretive state and is only eligible for promotion after the relevant gates are satisfied.

This can be represented schematically as:

\[
D_{\mathrm{external}} \rightarrow S_{\mathrm{sandbox}} \xrightarrow{\,G(x)\,} A_{\mathrm{approved}}
\]

where the directive \(D_{\mathrm{external}}\) is first interpreted in sandbox state \(S_{\mathrm{sandbox}}\), and only a gated candidate action \(x\) may move into approved authority state \(A_{\mathrm{approved}}\). This formalism makes clear that external input is not equivalent to authorized execution.

---

## 38.12 Closing Statement

External communication in Ms. Allis is a governed authority pathway, not a raw input-output reflex.

External directives enter a sandboxed, constitutionally reviewed reasoning path before execution, and no direct external directive is permitted to bypass the gate structure. Public-facing outputs and external commitments must preserve authority order and remain subject to disclosure control so that the system speaks and acts only at the level it is actually authorized to occupy.
