# 43. Role-Gated Knowledge Access

*Carrie Kidd (Mamma Kidd) — Mount Hope, WV*  
*Last updated: July 10, 2026*

---

## 43.1 Purpose

Role-gated knowledge access determines who may see what, under which conditions, and at what level of detail.

In Ms. Allis, this applies not only to durable memory stores and established records, but also to sandbox-derived knowledge, reasoning traces, staged outputs, and promotion-adjacent logs. The chapter therefore governs disclosure across both persistent and provisional knowledge domains. What matters is not merely where a piece of information is stored, but whether the requester has the right combination of role, consent, context, purpose, timing, and legal authority to receive it.

This makes access control part of the authority architecture. Knowledge that remains inside the system is not thereby ungoverned.

---

## 43.2 Scope of Role Gating

Role gating should explicitly cover the full range of knowledge-bearing artifacts generated by the system.

That includes:

- reasoning traces;
- sandbox logs;
- sandbox-derived intermediate outputs;
- approved conclusions;
- promotion records and gate-adjacent metadata where disclosure is permitted;
- per-user memory views and related personalized history.

This scope matters because a modern governed system can leak more through traces and logs than through its final answer channel. A chapter on knowledge access is incomplete if it protects only persistent databases while leaving sandbox artifacts comparatively exposed.

---

## 43.3 Reasoning Traces and Sandbox Logs

Reasoning traces and sandbox logs must be role-gated.

These artifacts may contain provisional hypotheses, partial evaluations, failed paths, sensitive contextual associations, and governance-relevant metadata that were never intended for universal disclosure. Access to them should therefore be narrower than access to many final conclusions. In many cases, a user may be entitled to a result without being entitled to the full trace by which the system considered alternative possibilities.

This principle protects both privacy and procedural integrity. It also preserves the distinction between bounded internal reasoning and externally disclosed knowledge.

---

## 43.4 Approved Conclusions and Memory Views

Approved conclusions should remain role-gated even after promotion, and per-user memory views require especially careful treatment.

An approved conclusion has crossed a governance boundary, but that does not mean it becomes universally visible. Access still depends on the requesting role, the relevance of the conclusion to the request, and the permissions associated with the receiving context. Per-user memory views are even more restricted because they often contain individualized history, inferred context, or user-linked records whose disclosure must remain tightly bounded.

The chapter should therefore treat promotion status and disclosure status as different questions. A conclusion may be approved for system use while still being hidden from many viewers.

---

## 43.5 Least-Knowledge Rule

The least-knowledge rule applies to sandbox-derived knowledge as well as to persistent stores.

This means the system should disclose no more than what the requesting role legitimately requires for the present purpose. The rule does not relax merely because the information is ephemeral, temporary, or stored in a sandbox collection rather than in a durable database. On the contrary, provisional knowledge often demands stricter handling because it may be incomplete, unpromoted, or more revealing of internal context than the final authorized output.

The least-knowledge rule therefore spans the whole knowledge lifecycle:

- pre-promotion sandbox traces;
- intermediate and review-stage artifacts;
- approved conclusions;
- persistent memory and per-user records.

The same governance principle applies across all of them: only the minimally appropriate view should be disclosed.

---

## 43.6 Joint Disclosure Conditions

Disclosure depends jointly on more than role alone.

The chapter should state clearly that disclosure is conditioned by:

- consent;
- role;
- context;
- time;
- purpose;
- legal authority.

These variables operate together. A person may hold a role that is generally privileged, yet still lack authorization in a given context, at a given time, for a given purpose, or absent the relevant consent or legal basis. Conversely, lawful or consent-based access may expand what is visible in specific situations without making the information universally available thereafter.

This joint structure prevents simplistic access models in which a role label is treated as a permanent master key.

---

## 43.7 Projection Operator

A useful formal device for this chapter is the projection operator governing disclosure.

Let \(K\) denote the available knowledge state and let the disclosure conditions be represented by consent \(c\), role \(r\), context \(x\), time \(t\), purpose \(p\), and legal authority \(\ell\). Then disclosure can be represented as:

\[
\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)
\]

where \(\Pi\) is a projection operator that returns only the portion of \(K\) that may lawfully and appropriately be disclosed under those joint conditions.

This is important because it shows that disclosure is not a binary property of the knowledge itself. The same underlying knowledge state may project to different visible subsets for different users, times, or purposes. Sandbox traces, logs, approved conclusions, and memory views are all subject to this projection logic.

---

## 43.8 Disclosure as Projection, Not Release

The projection framing clarifies how the chapter should think about access.

The system does not simply “release” knowledge as a single object. Instead, it computes a permitted view. One requester may receive a narrow summary, another may receive a record-level view, and a third may receive nothing at all. This is especially important for sandbox outputs and traces, where the difference between operational necessity and over-disclosure can be large.

Projection also helps maintain coherence between privacy and governance. A role that permits access to a conclusion does not automatically imply access to the underlying trace, and access to a user-specific memory view does not imply access to unrelated system reasoning artifacts.

---

## 43.9 Authority and Knowledge Boundaries

Role-gated access is one of the mechanisms by which authority boundaries are preserved after knowledge has been formed.

A sandbox may generate candidate knowledge, a gate may approve part of it, and memory systems may later retain some derivative of it. At each stage, role gating determines what becomes visible to whom. This prevents the authority needed to produce or inspect an internal trace from being mistaken for authority to disclose it broadly.

The chapter should therefore connect access control to the promotion architecture. Governance does not end when the system knows something; governance continues in the form of controlled visibility.

---

## 43.10 Practical Interpretation

In practical terms, the chapter should support at least the following distinctions:

- access to reasoning traces may be narrower than access to final answers;
- access to sandbox logs may be narrower than access to approved conclusions;
- access to approved conclusions may still exclude sensitive provenance or internal review details;
- access to per-user memory views must remain tied to identity, consent, role, and lawful purpose.

These distinctions help the system avoid both under-sharing and over-sharing. They also align least-knowledge governance with the real structure of the service chain.

---

## 43.11 Closing Statement

Role-gated knowledge access in Ms. Allis covers both persistent knowledge and sandbox-derived knowledge.

Reasoning traces, sandbox logs, approved conclusions, and per-user memory views all belong inside the role-gating framework, and the least-knowledge rule applies across the full lifecycle of knowledge rather than only to durable stores. By expressing disclosure as the projection operator \(\Pi_{c,r,x,t,p,\ell}(K)\), this chapter makes clear that access depends jointly on consent, role, context, time, purpose, and legal authority, so that disclosure remains governed even when the system has already formed or approved the underlying knowledge.
