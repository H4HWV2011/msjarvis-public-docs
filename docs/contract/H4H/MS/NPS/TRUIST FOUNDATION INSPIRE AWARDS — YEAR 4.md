WHAT SPECIFIC PROBLEM ARE YOU SOLVING? (Word Limit: 200)

Building a volunteer tracking app for a rural West Virginia nonprofit revealed
the same wall every time: every digital tool built for a community like Mount
Hope sends the value somewhere else. Google. Amazon. Someone who doesn't live
here.

Rural Appalachian communities — coalfield counties with poverty rates exceeding
40% in some districts, persistent broadband gaps, and generations of resource
extraction — have no locally owned digital infrastructure. Every available tool
for navigating benefits, finding work, or participating in the AI economy was
designed by outsiders, for outsiders — and most penalize participation by
threatening SNAP, SSI, or Medicaid eligibility.

Fayette County has roughly 40,000 residents. West Virginia has 33 economically
distressed counties. Nationally, 43 million rural Americans face the same
compounding crisis: AI is reorganizing work faster than any institution can
respond, and the communities most affected have the least infrastructure to
adapt.

Mount Hope is where we prove a different model is possible. The New River Gorge
corridor — 53 miles, seven gateway communities, a National Park, and no reliable
cell service — is where it scales.

WHAT IS YOUR SOLUTION? (Word Limit: 200)

MountainShares Commons is a community-owned economic and intelligence platform
currently in Phase 0 red team testing in Mount Hope, West Virginia, June 2026.

Three systems work together:

Ms. Allis — Artificial Learning and Location Intelligence System — is a GIS
place-based AI steward grounded in 5.4 million verified geospatial beliefs about
West Virginia. She answers community questions tied to real parcels, roads,
census blocks, and infrastructure. Every answer is traceable. Every constraint
is published. No surveillance — only anonymized, community-consented data. She
supports decisions — she never makes them.

MountainShares is a closed-loop local economy. Participants earn tokens through
volunteering, caregiving, and contribution — redeemable locally. Explicitly
designed safe for people on SNAP, SSI, and Medicaid.

The Commons is the governed marketplace, resource navigator, and community
platform where residents earn, spend, connect, and shape the system itself.

Mount Hope is where we prove it works. The New River Gorge corridor — 53 miles,
seven gateway communities, a National Park, and no reliable cell service — is
where it scales.

Platform: egeria.mountainshares.us/commons

WHO DOES YOUR SOLUTION SERVE? (Word Limit: 200)

MountainShares Commons serves residents of Mount Hope and the New River Gorge
corridor — rural Appalachian communities where poverty, infrastructure
disinvestment, and geographic isolation compound daily. Nearly 1 in 4 residents
lives below the poverty line. Broadband is limited. Public transit is
nonexistent. Trust in outside institutions is low, and earned.

Four overlapping groups are directly served:

  - Benefits recipients on SNAP, SSI, and Medicaid who need economic
    participation tools that don't trigger eligibility cliffs — addressed through
    Benefits-Sensitive Account architecture built into the platform's core

  - Caregivers and volunteers whose labor sustains the community but goes
    unrecognized and uncompensated by the formal economy

  - Small local businesses with no community-anchored alternative to extractive
    retail and e-commerce

  - Residents navigating systems — housing, health, workforce, emergency
    resources — without reliable, local, trustworthy information

Ms. Allis closes the information gap: accurate, place-specific, surveillance-
free answers. MountainShares closes the economic gap: dignified participation
without benefits penalties. The Commons closes the civic gap: a governed space
residents actually own.

As the corridor scales, every gateway community gains the same locally owned
infrastructure — on their own terms.

WHAT MAKES YOUR SOLUTION INNOVATIVE? (Word Limit: 300)

Most AI tools built for underserved communities share a fatal flaw: they
extract. Data leaves. Value leaves. Decisions are made somewhere else.
MountainShares Commons is architecturally designed to invert that model.

Three innovations distinguish this system:

1. A live, place-based Hilbert space — not a chatbot.
Ms. Allis doesn't retrieve web search results. She reasons across a formally
structured H_App ⊗ H_geo tensor product architecture — 5.4 million verified
West Virginia geospatial beliefs indexed in semantic memory, with Phi Probe
semantic coherence measurement and constitutional governance constraints. Every
answer is traceable. Every constraint is published. This is glassbox AI — not
black-box AI making consequential decisions without accountability.

2. A benefits-safe closed-loop economy.
No existing platform has engineered a mutual credit system explicitly structured
around SNAP, SSI, and Medicaid eligibility preservation. The Benefits-Sensitive
Account architecture treats the benefits cliff not as a navigation problem but
as a structural design problem — and solves it in code.

3. Community ownership as infrastructure, not aspiration.
The governance layer, the data, the economic rules — all are constitutionally
constrained, democratically auditable, and locally owned. Communities can
challenge, audit, and shut the system down if it violates their trust.

The broader catalytic potential: West Virginia's rich standardized public GIS
data and tight-knit social networks make it the ideal testbed before this model
travels — to every rural county, every heritage corridor, every community where
platform monopolies have captured local markets and black-box AI has replaced
local accountability.

Mount Hope proves it. The New River Gorge corridor scales it. Appalachia
exports it.

WHAT IS YOUR BUSINESS MODEL? (Word Limit: 300)

MountainShares is a community-owned economic infrastructure system for
Appalachian West Virginia, operated by Harmony for Hope, Inc., a nonprofit based
in Mount Hope, WV. Our model has two integrated layers: a dual-currency local
marketplace (The Commons) and a locally hosted AI navigation system (ALLIS).

The Commons is a neighbor-to-neighbor marketplace where residents of West
Virginia's 55 counties buy, sell, trade, hire, give, and find — paid in U.S.
dollars or in EMS (MountainShares), earned through verified community
contribution. Every listing is posted by a verified County Champion. The
currency is not speculative — it is earned through work that benefits the
community and spent locally.

ALLIS is a locally hosted, place-anchored AI platform that gives organizations
a named intelligence node grounded in verified local data. It runs on locally
managed hardware, operates in degraded connectivity environments, and never
sends community data to an external cloud. Government and nonprofit nodes are
permanently free. Commercial nodes pay for cluster membership.

Who we serve: Coalfield Appalachian residents in counties with high poverty,
infrastructure deficits, and post-extraction economic conditions — people
excluded from digital economic participation not because they lack capability,
but because the infrastructure was never built for them.

Why they need it: The region has no local digital marketplace, no locally owned
AI infrastructure, and no mesh network that functions without cellular.
MountainShares builds all three simultaneously, governed by the community it
serves.

Revenue: The Commons generates transaction and EMS issuance fees. ALLIS
generates deployment, cluster membership, and operations fees from commercial
clients. Government and nonprofit access is permanently free.

FINANCIAL SUSTAINABILITY PLAN (Word Limit: 300)

Harmony for Hope reaches structural self-sustainability through four converging
revenue streams, with the inner-core threshold defined as 315,800 active
participants and the margin-of-safety target at 383,000 — both projected for
2030–2031.

Earned transaction revenue is the primary engine. Every dollar loaded generates
a 3.05% program fee; every merchant payment generates 1.75%; every user-to-user
transfer generates 0.25% — all automatically split across operations, treasury
reserves, infrastructure, and community programs. H4H receives 30% of the fee
streams directly for nonprofit operations. Projected fee revenue scales from
~$1,175 in Phase 0 (2026) to ~$1.3 million in Phase 2 (2028), and ~$24 million
at the 2030 sustainability threshold.

ALLIS commercial node fees from Kidd's Technical Services — deployment, cluster
membership, and ongoing operations contracts with private-sector organizations —
provide a second earned revenue stream fully independent of the commons economy.

West Virginia diaspora premium: The Commons USD storefront accepts any
Mastercard, allowing the hundreds of thousands of West Virginians living outside
the state to purchase authentic Appalachian goods at a premium that flows
directly into the treasury reserve — projected at ~$20 million annually from
Phase 3 forward.

Federal and foundation grants, including Appalachian Regional Commission, USDA
Rural Development, Claude Worthington Benedum Foundation, and WV Community
Foundation, are actively targeted at each phase.

Evidence to date: Kidd's Technical Services has contributed 6,067.5 hours of
senior technical work — $910,125 at $150/hour — fully documented and auditable
for grant in-kind match. Live production infrastructure is operational at 706
Main Street, Mount Hope, WV: 51,000+ modules, 175 services, 6.1 million spatial
records. Phase 0 invite-only beta is active. A fully executed Master Services
Agreement between KTS and H4H establishes all legal and IP foundations.
