## ABOUT YOUR ORGANIZATION

Harmony for Hope, Inc. (H4H) is a Fayette County–based nonprofit founded in 2017 and headquartered at 704 & 708 Main Street in Mount Hope. H4H's mission is to unite West Virginia through music, art, and Appalachian heritage through technology.

H4H operates the Mount Hope Historic Walking Trail — a 1.25-mile heritage trail through sixteen points of architectural significance currently generating 65 documented monthly visitors through Google discovery alone — and the Fayette County Community Arts Center, an active cultural programming facility serving the wider Fayette County region.

H4H sponsors the MountainShares community deployment tier for the Artificial Learning & Location Intelligence System (ALLIS) — a locally hosted mesh intelligence platform operated under a no-cost license, currently live and in active testing at https://egeria.mountainshares.us. The MountainShares sponsorship tier enables no-cost ALLIS node deployment in gateway communities across the New River Gorge corridor.

## PROJECT DESCRIPTION

### The Regional Economic Development Problem

New River Gorge National Park and Preserve is one of the fastest-growing national parks in the United States. Its elevation to national park status accelerated tourism growth across the nine-county New River Highlands region — bringing hundreds of thousands of annual visitors into Fayette, Raleigh, Summers, and the surrounding counties that form the corridor's backbone.

Those visitors move through gateway towns. They stop at trailheads, depots, and main streets. And when they get there, they encounter an infrastructure gap that costs the region real economic activity every season.

Wireless connectivity throughout the New River Gorge corridor is unreliable. The canyon's geology, its narrow valleys, and its distance from population centers combine to make carrier-dependent service inconsistent across much of the Gorge — at trailheads, along river accesses, in canyon communities, and at federally managed sites. Visitors who arrive expecting to load a trail map, check river levels, or access interpretive content discover that the infrastructure they rely on everywhere else does not reliably reach here.

This is not a problem that waiting will solve. It is a structural condition of the landscape — and it is costing the nine-county region economic activity every season.

Heritage assets exist throughout the corridor. NPS has invested in interpretive infrastructure. Local organizations have built trails, signage, and brochure programs. The visitors are coming. The locally governed infrastructure to connect them — to extend their stay, deepen their engagement, and direct them to the living gateway towns where economic activity occurs — is missing.

### Thurmond — The Corridor's Federal Gateway

Thurmond is a federally preserved railroad boomtown — once one of the busiest revenue points on the Chesapeake & Ohio Railway — now maintained by the National Park Service as a living link to the coal and rail economy that built southern West Virginia. Visitors arrive by road, by Amtrak's Cardinal line, and by NPS excursion trains. They step off onto a depot platform at the southern end of the Gorge corridor with no reliable connectivity of any kind.

Thurmond is where the corridor begins for thousands of visitors each year. It is where the story of the New River Gorge — coal, rail, labor, and landscape — is most concentrated and most accessible. And it is where the infrastructure gap is most consequential.

A single Starlink-connected ALLIS node, sited at a point of interest selected jointly by NPS interpretive staff and Thurmond's community governance partners, creates a locally hosted connectivity anchor at the federal gateway to the corridor. Visitors connecting to MS.THURMOND.ALLIS are not routed to a distant cloud server. They connect to a node that lives at the Depot — one that knows the platform they are standing on, the rail grade they are looking at, the river reach below them.

The content that node delivers is not determined by an algorithm or a platform company. It is shaped by the people who know this place: NPS interpretive staff and the corridor communities whose history the Depot represents. That is data sovereignty in practice — the corridor controls the story visitors receive, at the moment they are most ready to receive it.

For regional economic development, the payoff is direct. A visitor who connects at the Thurmond Depot receives locally hosted heritage content, river safety information, and corridor orientation. They learn about the living gateway towns — Hinton, Mount Hope, Fayetteville, Oak Hill — they will pass through on the way out of the Gorge. They leave with the full corridor in their heads, not just the platform they stepped off on. That is visitor yield distributed across the nine-county region, generated from a single well-placed node at the federal anchor of the southern corridor.

### The Solution: Locally Governed Connectivity as Regional Economic Infrastructure

The New River Gorge Heritage & Visitor Connectivity Mesh Pilot deploys the first nodes of a corridor-scale locally governed mesh serving New River Gorge gateway communities. The pilot begins with a deployment in Fayette County — where H4H's existing infrastructure, municipal support, and NPS partnership provide the fastest path to a working reference implementation — and extends the corridor framework to Thurmond as the southern federal gateway.

The system serves three regional economic functions:

**1. Visitor capture and corridor distribution.**
A visitor who connects to a locally hosted ALLIS node at any point along the corridor receives heritage interpretation, safety information, and orientation to the living gateway towns where economic activity occurs. Connectivity at the point of arrival converts pass-through visitors into corridor participants — people who stay longer, spend more, and return.

**2. Community-controlled tourism infrastructure.**
Every node in the mesh is governed by a simple written agreement between H4H, the node host, and the platform operator. Communities contribute site access and content priorities. The platform delivers what those communities choose — not what a distant platform company decides is relevant. This is tourism infrastructure that serves residents, not the other way around.

**3. Seed investment that scales to the full corridor.**
This $9,500 grant funds the pilot deployment that makes every subsequent corridor investment credible. When H4H approaches the Appalachian Regional Commission, USDA Rural Development, and the Economic Development Administration for corridor-scale expansion to Fayetteville, Oak Hill, Hinton, and Ansted, those applications rest on a working, documented, independently validated reference implementation. New River Highlands Development Fund dollars are the seed that grows the corridor.

## ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT IMPACT

**Regional visitor yield:**
- The New River Gorge corridor receives hundreds of thousands of annual visitors. Each node in the mesh converts pass-through visitors into corridor participants directed toward the living gateway towns where economic activity occurs.
- Thurmond's node serves visitors arriving via Amtrak's Cardinal line and NPS excursion trains — a captive audience at the southern gateway with no current connectivity and no current mechanism for directing them to the broader corridor.

**Pilot to corridor scale:**
- The Fayette County pilot node is Node 1 of a planned corridor mesh serving Fayetteville, Oak Hill, Hinton, Ansted, and additional gateway communities throughout the nine-county region.
- Every node added to the mesh multiplies the economic development impact of every existing node — visitors at any point in the corridor receive orientation to the full corridor.

**Grant leverage and multiplier:**
- This $9,500 seed investment is the documented reference implementation that enables H4H to pursue ARC, USDA Rural Development, and EDA corridor-scale grants — funding programs that require a working pilot as their foundation.
- New River Highlands Development Fund dollars create a documented regional multiplier, converting a Fayette County pilot into a nine-county corridor infrastructure network.
