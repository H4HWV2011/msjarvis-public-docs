## ABOUT YOUR ORGANIZATION

### Organizational Narrative

Harmony for Hope, Inc. is a Mount Hope-based 501(c)(3) nonprofit serving southern West Virginia through music, art, Appalachian heritage, public education, and community-centered technology. Harmony for Hope is rooted in Fayette County and operates from the Main Street corridor in Mount Hope, where it supports local arts programming, heritage interpretation, community engagement, and technology-based approaches to rural revitalization.

Harmony for Hope’s mission is to unite West Virginia with music, art, and Appalachian heritage through technology. The organization’s work is grounded in the belief that rural communities should not have to choose between preserving their history and participating in the modern economy. In Mount Hope and the surrounding New River Gorge region, Harmony for Hope works to strengthen both: local heritage and local opportunity.

Harmony for Hope’s major programs and community assets include:

The Mount Hope Historic Walking Trail — a 1.25-mile self-guided heritage trail through sixteen architectural and historical points of interest. The trail helps residents and visitors understand Mount Hope’s story from Indigenous pathways and early settlement, through the coal and rail era, the Fire of 1910, company-town development, labor history, architecture, and the community’s continuing work to preserve and revitalize Main Street.

The Fayette County Community Arts Center — a Main Street cultural anchor supporting local arts, heritage programming, community events, and creative activity in the Mount Hope area.

MountainShares Commons — a community-serving local economy, heritage, and information platform being developed to help local communities share stories, resources, events, services, and place-based information in ways that remain rooted in local governance and local benefit.

ALLIS — the Artificial Learning & Location Intelligence System — a locally hosted, location-aware community information platform developed and supported by Kidd’s Technical Services and made available to Harmony for Hope for community deployment. For this project, ALLIS is not being presented as a finished commercial product or a replacement for existing public systems. It is a carefully scoped pilot tool for locally hosted heritage content, visitor orientation, local business and community information, and rural connectivity testing in a real New River Gorge gateway community setting.

Harmony for Hope’s role in this project is to serve as the nonprofit applicant, community sponsor, heritage content steward, and local implementation lead. Kidd’s Technical Services provides technical infrastructure, no-cost platform access, hardware specification, documentation, and technical support under the existing Harmony for Hope–Kidd’s Technical Services relationship, with conflict-of-interest governance handled through Harmony for Hope’s board processes.

The broader New River Gorge Heritage & Visitor Connectivity Mesh Pilot has received concept-stage and professional support from outside partners, including New River Gorge National Park and Preserve, Starry Eyes Media, the Town of Thurmond, and Dr. Anurag K. Srivastava of West Virginia University in his personal professional capacity. These letters support continued exploration, grant development, and responsible pilot testing. They do not remove the need for permitting, compliance, technical review, or future site-specific approvals.

## NEED / PROBLEM / OPPORTUNITY

The New River Gorge region has the ingredients of strong rural economic development: national visibility, deep Appalachian heritage, outdoor recreation, rail and coal history, historic towns, local businesses, arts organizations, trails, river access points, and visitors who are already coming.

The problem is that the infrastructure connecting those ingredients is incomplete.

Visitors move through the New River Gorge corridor by car, train, trail, river, and Main Street. They stop at overlooks, depots, trailheads, local businesses, and historic communities. But when they arrive, they often depend on commercial cell service, scattered web searches, printed signs, or chance encounters to understand where they are, what they are seeing, where else they should go, and how their visit can support the living communities around them.

In many parts of the Gorge, especially in narrow valleys, rural communities, river corridors, and federally managed sites, cellular service is inconsistent across carriers. Even where a visitor has signal, there is no locally governed digital layer that connects heritage interpretation, safety information, visitor orientation, local businesses, community events, and gateway-town resources in one place.

That gap has economic consequences.

When visitors cannot easily find local heritage content, trail context, nearby businesses, restaurants, shops, events, arts spaces, or community services, they pass through instead of staying longer. When they leave a trailhead, depot, or historic site without learning what else is nearby, the region loses visitor yield. The visitor may have come to the New River Gorge, but the economic benefit does not fully reach the gateway communities that hold the region’s story.

The opportunity is to build a locally governed connectivity and interpretation model that captures visitor attention at the point of interest and directs it toward the broader corridor economy.

This project funds the pilot infrastructure needed to begin that model.

The New River Gorge Heritage & Visitor Connectivity Mesh Pilot will deploy a small, locally hosted ALLIS node and supporting connectivity equipment as the first working reference implementation for a corridor-scale heritage and visitor connectivity system. The initial deployment is centered in Fayette County, where Harmony for Hope already operates, where the Mount Hope Historic Walking Trail and Fayette County Community Arts Center provide an existing community base, and where regional partner engagement creates a realistic path toward future corridor expansion.

The long-term economic development goal is a locally governed mesh of gateway-community nodes serving Mount Hope, Thurmond, Fayetteville, Oak Hill, Hinton, Ansted, and other New River Highlands communities. Each node would help visitors understand where they are, what story the place holds, how to stay safe, and how to find nearby communities, businesses, trails, events, and cultural assets.

This grant request does not ask the New River Highlands Development Fund to fund the full regional vision. It asks the Fund to support the first practical infrastructure deployment — the capital equipment and professional installation needed to prove the model, measure the results, and create a documented foundation for larger state, federal, and regional economic development funding.

New River Highlands Development Fund dollars can serve as the seed investment that turns a Fayette County pilot into a replicable corridor infrastructure model.

## PROJECT DETAILS

The New River Gorge Heritage & Visitor Connectivity Mesh Pilot will install and test the first working node of a locally governed heritage and visitor connectivity system for gateway communities in the New River Gorge region.

The project includes five core components.

### Component 1 — Local ALLIS Server

A small local server will be installed to host heritage content, visitor orientation, corridor information, local resource information, and project data for the pilot node. The server allows content to be hosted close to the community and site it serves rather than relying only on distant cloud platforms or scattered social media posts.

This local hosting model is important because it supports rural resilience, faster local access, and community control over the content being presented to visitors. The server will support the pilot ALLIS deployment, local heritage interpretation, and future testing of gateway-community content layers.

### Component 2 — Starlink Connectivity

The project will install Starlink equipment to provide a reliable connectivity backbone for the pilot node. This connection will support content updates, system access, node operation, and field testing.

Starlink is not presented as a replacement for commercial broadband, cell service, 911, emergency management, or public alert systems. It is a practical connectivity layer for a rural heritage and visitor information pilot in an area where ordinary carrier-dependent service can be inconsistent.

### Component 3 — Trailhead / Point-of-Interest Kiosk Node

The project will install a small kiosk or point-of-interest device that allows visitors to connect to locally hosted content. This device will support heritage interpretation, visitor orientation, safety notes, and local corridor information.

The kiosk node will be installed only after confirming site control, property-owner permission, structural suitability, electrical safety, and any required municipal, local, or partner approvals.

### Component 4 — Solar and Outdoor Power Support

Because the project involves rural outdoor infrastructure, the pilot includes solar and battery support for the kiosk or point-of-interest node. This allows the project to test a practical off-grid or low-grid support model for future gateway-community deployments.

The solar component includes panel, controller, battery, weatherproof housing, mounting hardware, wiring, and grounding supplies. Professional installation is included because safe deployment of outdoor public-facing power systems requires appropriate expertise.

### Component 5 — Low-Power LoRa Backup Layer

The project will install and test a small LoRa backup communication layer. LoRa is a low-power, low-bandwidth technology useful for short project messages, equipment status, basic local information, and resilience testing.

LoRa is not a replacement for cell service, internet service, 911, emergency management, or official public alert systems. Its purpose in this project is narrower: to test whether a low-cost backup layer can help gateway-community infrastructure continue sharing basic information when conventional connectivity is limited.

### Primary Participants — Gateway Community Residents and Local Businesses

Residents and small businesses in gateway communities benefit when visitors are better oriented to the full corridor rather than only to a single trailhead, overlook, depot, or attraction. Economic development partners benefit because the pilot creates a documented, replicable model that can support future funding applications and corridor-scale expansion. A working node gives Harmony for Hope and its partners evidence, metrics, lessons learned, and a practical deployment model for larger grants through state, federal, and regional sources.

## BUDGET NARRATIVE

This budget funds the capital equipment and professional installation needed to deploy the first working pilot node for the New River Gorge Heritage & Visitor Connectivity Mesh Pilot.

The requested funds are not for general operating support. They are for specific equipment, safe installation, outdoor power support, connectivity equipment, field supplies, and contingency needed to install and test the pilot infrastructure.

The hardware line items are one-time capital costs for equipment expected to remain useful beyond the grant period. These items include the local server, kiosk device, solar equipment, outdoor enclosures, mounting hardware, LoRa equipment, backup power accessories, and weatherproofing supplies.

The Starlink equipment provides the connectivity backbone for the pilot node. This is necessary because the project is designed for rural and hard-to-serve areas where ordinary carrier-dependent service may be inconsistent. Reliable connectivity allows the pilot to support content updates, system access, visitor orientation, and testing.

The local server supports the ALLIS content layer. It allows heritage interpretation, visitor orientation, local resource information, and gateway-community content to be hosted close to the community and site being served.

The kiosk node, outdoor enclosure, signage, and cabling support public-facing access to locally hosted content. The solar panel, controller, battery, battery box, mounting hardware, wiring, grounding supplies, and weatherproof enclosure support field testing of an outdoor or low-grid deployment model that can be replicated in future gateway-community locations.

The LoRa equipment supports testing of a low-power backup communication layer. This is not a replacement for emergency systems or internet service, but it allows Harmony for Hope to test whether basic project messages, status information, and local notices can continue moving when conventional connectivity is limited.

The electrician installation line reflects the practical requirements of the work. This project includes outdoor public-facing equipment, solar power, battery storage, DC wiring, weatherproof enclosures, mounting, cable management, grounding, site assessment, and safe installation at locations that may require adjustment based on field conditions. Professional installation protects the public, the equipment, the property owner, and the long-term reliability of the project.

The project contingency allowance is included because outdoor installation in a rural and historic environment can reveal conditions that are not fully knowable until installation begins. Contingency may cover additional mounting hardware, weatherproofing, electrical supplies, replacement parts, safety adjustments, additional cable runs, hardware substitutions, or site-specific requirements that arise during installation.

## WHAT WILL HAPPEN IF FULL FUNDING IS NOT RECEIVED?

If Harmony for Hope does not receive the full requested amount, the project will be reduced in scope and implemented in priority order. All awarded funds will still be used for pilot deployment and documented for the New River Highlands Development Fund.

### Priority 1 — Core Server and Connectivity

The first priority is the local server, Starlink equipment, and networking backbone. This creates the basic infrastructure needed to host local content, operate the pilot node, and begin field testing.

### Priority 2 — Safe Installation and Site Preparation

The second priority is professional installation, site assessment, weatherproofing, grounding, cable management, and safety review. Before public-facing equipment is installed, Harmony for Hope must confirm safe and appropriate installation conditions.

### Priority 3 — Kiosk Node and Solar Support

The third priority is the kiosk or point-of-interest node and solar support. This extends the content layer from the core system to a field-accessible visitor location.

### Priority 4 — LoRa Backup Layer

The fourth priority is the LoRa backup communication layer. It is valuable for resilience testing, but the project can begin with the server, connectivity, safe installation, and kiosk node while the LoRa layer is deferred to a second phase.

### Priority 5 — Expanded Content and Corridor Enhancements

Additional content expansion, signage improvements, and corridor orientation enhancements can be phased in as future funding becomes available.

Any partial award will be deployed, documented, and reported with a revised scope, revised timeline, and plan for closing the remaining gap through additional partners or future grant requests.

## ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT IMPACT

This project creates economic development value in five ways.

First, it improves visitor capture. Visitors who connect to a locally hosted node can receive heritage interpretation, safety information, and orientation to nearby communities, businesses, trails, events, and cultural assets. This helps turn pass-through visitors into corridor participants.

Second, it supports gateway-community discovery. The project helps visitors understand that the New River Gorge is not only a landscape, but also a network of living communities with Main Streets, arts spaces, restaurants, shops, historic sites, and local stories worth discovering.

Third, it strengthens local control over tourism infrastructure. Communities should not have to depend only on distant platforms to tell visitors who they are. A locally governed content layer allows gateway communities to shape the information visitors receive.

Fourth, it creates a working reference implementation. A small, documented pilot gives Harmony for Hope and its partners the evidence needed to pursue larger state, federal, and regional economic development funding.

Fifth, it builds infrastructure that can scale. Each future node added to the corridor can strengthen the value of the whole network. A visitor who connects in one place can be oriented to the full corridor, and every participating community can benefit from that shared system.

New River Highlands Development Fund dollars can help create the first practical step toward a locally governed visitor connectivity network for the New River Gorge region.
