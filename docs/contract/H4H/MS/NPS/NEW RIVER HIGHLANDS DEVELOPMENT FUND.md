## ABOUT YOUR ORGANIZATION

Harmony for Hope, Inc. (H4H) is a Fayette County–based nonprofit founded in 2017 and headquartered at 704 & 708 Main Street in Mount Hope. H4H's mission is to unite West Virginia through music, art, and Appalachian heritage through technology.

H4H operates two active community assets in Mount Hope: the Mount Hope Historic Walking Trail — a 1.25-mile heritage trail through sixteen points of architectural significance, supported by 48 interpretive signs and currently generating 65 documented monthly visitors through Google discovery alone — and the Fayette County Community Arts Center (FCCAC) at 704 Main Street, an active cultural programming facility supporting a resident artist and serving the wider Fayette County region.

H4H also operates MountainShares Commons, a community-serving local economy and heritage platform operated under a no-cost license and currently live at https://egeria.mountainshares.us, and sponsors the MountainShares community deployment tier for the Artificial Learning & Location Intelligence System (ALLIS) — a locally hosted mesh intelligence platform operated under a no-cost license and currently live and in active testing.

## PROJECT DESCRIPTION

### The Economic Development Problem

New River Gorge National Park and Preserve is one of the fastest-growing national parks in the United States. Its designation as a national park accelerated tourism growth across southern West Virginia — and Fayette County sits at the center of that growth corridor. Visitors arrive by the hundreds of thousands annually to hike, climb, raft, and explore.

They move through gateway towns like Mount Hope on the way. And when they get there, they encounter an infrastructure gap that costs Fayette County real economic activity every year.

Wireless connectivity throughout the New River Gorge corridor is unreliable. The canyon's geology, its narrow valleys, and its distance from population centers combine to make carrier-dependent service inconsistent across much of the Gorge — at trailheads, along river accesses, and in the gateway communities that line the corridor. Visitors who arrive expecting to load a trail map, check river levels, or access interpretive content discover that the infrastructure they rely on everywhere else does not reliably reach here.

This is not a problem that waiting will solve. It is a structural condition of the landscape — and it is costing Fayette County economic activity every season.

Visitors arriving in Mount Hope — a coalfield community with a documented 1.25-mile heritage trail, 48 interpretive signs, a historic main street, and a community arts center — cannot reliably connect. They cannot load the interpretive content that would extend their visit. They cannot discover the businesses, events, and experiences that would bring them back. The heritage assets exist. The visitors are coming. The local infrastructure to connect them is missing.

This is not a cultural problem. It is an economic development problem. Every visitor who arrives without connectivity and leaves without engaging the trail, the arts center, or Main Street is a lost economic transaction — for local businesses, for the tax base, and for the regional tourism economy that Fayette County depends on.

### Thurmond — Connectivity at the Corridor's Federal Gateway

Thurmond is a federally preserved railroad boomtown — once one of the busiest revenue points on the Chesapeake & Ohio Railway — now maintained by the National Park Service as a living link to the coal and rail economy that built southern West Virginia. Visitors arrive by road, by Amtrak's Cardinal line, and by NPS excursion trains. They step off onto a depot platform with no reliable connectivity of any kind.

This is the condition of the entire New River Gorge corridor — not an anomaly, a structural reality.

The answer is not to wait for a carrier solution. It is to build local.

A single Starlink-connected ALLIS node at a point of interest selected jointly by Thurmond's community partners and NPS interpretive staff creates a locally hosted connectivity anchor at the federal gateway to the southern corridor. Visitors connecting to MS.THURMOND.ALLIS are not routed to a distant cloud server. They connect to a node that lives at the Depot — that knows the platform they are standing on, the rail grade they are looking at, the river reach below them.

The content that node delivers is not determined by an algorithm or a platform company. It is determined by the people who know this place: NPS interpretive staff, Thurmond's governance partners, and the corridor communities whose history the Depot represents. That is data sovereignty in practice — the community controls the story visitors receive, in the field, at the moment they are most ready to receive it.

For economic development, the implications are direct. A visitor who connects at the Thurmond Depot receives locally hosted heritage content, river safety information, and corridor orientation. They learn about Mount Hope, about Fayetteville, about Hinton — the living gateway towns they will pass through on the way out of the Gorge. They leave with the full corridor in their heads, not just the platform they stepped off on. That is visitor yield for the entire nine-county region, generated from a single well-placed node at the federal anchor of the southern corridor.

Thurmond is not a destination project in this pilot. It is a connectivity gateway — the southern anchor of a mesh that will eventually run the full 53 miles of the Gorge corridor and turn every trailhead, depot, and main street along it into a node in a locally governed, heritage-aware information network that no carrier and no platform company controls.

### The Solution: Heritage Connectivity Infrastructure as Economic Infrastructure

The Mount Hope Heritage Connectivity Infrastructure project installs the physical backbone that converts Mount Hope's existing heritage assets into a functioning economic development node on the New River Gorge tourism corridor, with Thurmond serving as the corridor's southern connectivity gateway.

The system serves three economic functions:

**1. Visitor capture and extension.**
A visitor who connects to the locally hosted ALLIS node at the Historic Walking Trail kiosk in Mount Hope — or at the Thurmond Depot platform — receives trail maps, heritage interpretation, and information about businesses, events, and services across the corridor. Connectivity at the point of arrival is the difference between a visitor who passes through and a visitor who stays, spends, and returns.

**2. Tourism infrastructure for the corridor.**
Mount Hope and Thurmond are the first two nodes in a planned corridor-scale mesh serving New River Gorge gateway communities including Fayetteville, Oak Hill, Hinton, and Ansted. The infrastructure deployed with this grant is the reference implementation that demonstrates the model to every other gateway community in the nine-county region — and positions Fayette County as the economic development leader of the corridor.

**3. Leverage for significantly larger investment.**
This $9,500 grant, combined with a concurrent Greater Mount Hope Community Fund grant, completes the hardware and installation budget for the Mount Hope node. That completed, operational node is the documented proof-of-concept that supports H4H's pending applications to the Appalachian Regional Commission, USDA Rural Development, and the Economic Development Administration for corridor-scale expansion. New River Highlands Development Fund dollars directly enable those larger economic development grants to move forward.

## BUDGET NARRATIVE

This budget funds capital equipment and professional installation — one-time costs for infrastructure with a five-or-more-year useful life. There are no ongoing maintenance or operating expenses in this budget.
