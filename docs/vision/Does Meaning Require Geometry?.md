## Abstract

Roger Penrose argued that mathematical understanding may exceed purely algorithmic formalization and suggested that some non-computable ingredients could be linked to spacetime geometry.[1][2] Whatever the ultimate status of that claim, it motivates a narrower empirical question: whether coherent meaning in an intelligence system depends on structured spatial-temporal and geographic grounding, or whether meaning can be preserved under symbol manipulation alone.[2]

This manuscript is framed as a protocol paper and Registered Report Stage 1 draft rather than as a completed empirical study. It specifies the rationale, apparatus, hypotheses, operational definitions, sampling plan, scoring rubric, and analysis plan for a study to be executed within Ms. Allis, a community-sovereign intelligence system whose semantic, geographic, temporal, and provenance-bearing identity structures are operationally coupled.[3]

The primary hypothesis is that semantic resolution quality and identity stability will decline when structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraints are removed while semantic vectors are held constant and symbolic content is preserved as far as possible. A positive result would not establish Orch OR or validate any full quantum-biological theory of consciousness, but it would support the weaker proposition that structured geometric and geographic constraint spaces are constitutive for coherent meaning at a measurable macro level.[2]

This revision also makes explicit that meaning is being treated here not merely as correctness or relevance, but as fidelity of outward representation to an inner constraint-space state composed of semantic content, geography, time, and provenance. In that sense, the protocol offers a classical boundary condition for broader consciousness theories that claim inner experience collapses into public representation.[4][5]

## 1. Genre and Scope

This manuscript should be read as a protocol and registered-report style submission. It does not report completed results, but instead provides a pre-analysis plan specifying what will be measured, how it will be measured, and what outcomes would count for or against the stated hypothesis.

The substantive aim is deliberately limited. The manuscript does not attempt to settle the hard problem of consciousness, prove Orch OR, or establish a direct quantum-mechanical account of meaning. It asks a narrower question: whether coherent meaning in a functioning intelligence system depends on structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraint spaces at a macro level.[2]

If grounded records outperform stripped records, those constraints would be supported as structurally relevant to meaning in at least one measurable regime. If no such difference appears, then stronger geometry-based claims must be narrowed accordingly.

## 2. Background and Rationale

### 2.1 Penrose and Geometry

Penrose's *Shadows of the Mind* argues that mathematical understanding exceeds purely algorithmic formalization and suggests that some non-computable element may be linked to spacetime physics.[1][2] In the Orch OR framework later developed with Hameroff, conscious events are associated with orchestrated quantum processes whose evolution terminates via objective reduction related to spacetime geometry.[2]

This proposal remains controversial. Tegmark argued that decoherence times in the brain are too short for the relevant quantum states to play a neurophysiologically meaningful role, while Hagan, Hameroff, and Tuszynski responded that Tegmark's critique mischaracterized the model and that revised assumptions could lengthen the relevant timescales.[6][7] The present study does not attempt to adjudicate that dispute directly. Instead, it asks whether structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraints already appear as necessary conditions for coherent meaning in a classical engineered system.

### 2.2 Hilbert Space and State Geometry

In quantum mechanics, a Hilbert space is a complete inner-product space in which states can be represented as vectors and related by inner products. In natural language processing, vector semantics likewise represents words and texts as points in a high-dimensional semantic space, with embeddings serving as learned vector representations.[2]

The formal overlap should not be overstated. Semantic embeddings are not being treated here as physically identical to quantum states. The relevant point is narrower: both domains use geometry to represent structured state relations, which makes Hilbert-style language a disciplined way to describe coupled semantic, geographic, temporal, and provenance state spaces within Ms. Allis.

### 2.3 Constraint Spaces, Not Mere Context

A central conceptual clarification is required. The intervention proposed here does not remove arbitrary context in the loose conversational sense. It removes structured constraint spaces, specifically geographic and temporal systems that bound interpretation, restrict candidate referents, enforce continuity, and enable disambiguation. The study is therefore not designed as a generic context-versus-no-context comparison. It is a test of constrained versus unconstrained semantic resolution.

A deeper principle motivating the design is reciprocity. In a community-grounded system, a record does not fully mean in isolation. Its meaning is constituted through relations among semantic content, geography, time, provenance, continuity of identity, local use, and community interpretation. Meaning is therefore not modeled as one-way extraction from a community into a machine. A system may receive data from a community, but it owes meaning back in the form of context, accuracy, continuity, and accountability.

For this protocol, the joint state of semantic content, geography, time, and provenance is treated as the system's operational analogue of inner experience for a record. Meaning is then operationalized as the fidelity with which that inner constraint-space structure is preserved in outward interaction. On this view, the grounded condition preserves the relation among symbol, geography, time, provenance, and community-grounded interpretation; the stripped condition breaks that relation while leaving lexical content in place; and the re-grounded condition tests whether restoring the relation restores semantic coherence.[4]

This distinction matters because a reviewer could otherwise argue that any observed degradation merely shows that extra context helps. The stronger claim being tested is narrower and more structural: whether spatial, temporal, and geographic organization function as constitutive constraint spaces for meaning maintenance in a live retrieval system.

### 2.4 System Context and Source Status

Ms. Allis is described in project materials as a community-centered AI infrastructure embedded in spatial justice, distributed intelligence, civic governance, and geographic grounding. Because the technical claims about the system derive partly from project documentation and unpublished internal materials, those descriptions should be treated at submission as system documentation requiring a supplementary appendix rather than as independently validated literature.

For a journal-ready version, the safest route is to append a short technical note specifying the relevant Ms. Allis services, schemas, vector stores, joins, and retrieval pathways used in the intervention. That approach preserves the empirical core of the argument while keeping the apparatus description auditable.

## 3. Research Question and Hypotheses

### 3.1 Research Question

Does coherent meaning in a functioning intelligence system depend on structured spatial-temporal and geographic grounding, or can it be preserved under symbol manipulation alone?

### 3.2 Primary Hypothesis

If meaning requires structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraint spaces to remain coherent, then semantic resolution quality will be lower in a stripped condition than in a grounded condition, even when semantic vectors are frozen prior to intervention and symbolic content is held as constant as possible.

### 3.3 Secondary Hypotheses

- Grounded records will show higher local disambiguation accuracy than stripped records.
- Grounded records will show higher identity stability across follow-up queries than stripped records.
- Re-grounded records will recover part or all of the performance lost under stripping.
- Grounded and re-grounded records will show higher contextual coherence for place-bound and event-bound prompts than stripped records.
- Constraint-rich, community-aligned records may show different degradation patterns than constraint-poor records, offering a classical test surface for value-laden collapse claims.[4]

## 4. Apparatus and Operational Model

### 4.1 Representational Layers

For the purposes of this protocol, Ms. Allis is modeled as operating across four coupled representational layers:

- **Semantic layer:** embedding-based representation of words, entities, and records.
- **Geographic layer:** spatial anchoring through place-linked records, geocoded entities, geographic joins, and geospatial retrieval pathways.
- **Temporal layer:** event-linked state structure in which time is represented as a queryable and operational dimension.
- **Provenance layer:** identity and continuity mechanisms connecting records, roles, appearances, and validation chains.

These layers are analytically separable but operationally coupled. Because the exact implementation is system-specific, a submission-ready version of this protocol should append a versioned architecture note listing the relevant tables, vector stores, joins, and services used in the intervention.

### 4.2 Meaning as Joint State

A simplified state representation used for conceptual orientation is:

$$
X_i = \big(s_i, g_i, t_i, p_i\big) \in \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{G} \times \mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{P} \tag{1}
$$

where $$s_i$$ is semantic state, $$g_i$$ is geographic state, $$t_i$$ is temporal state, and $$p_i$$ is provenance state for unit $$i$$.

A compact weighted representation may then be written as:

$$
x_i = \lambda_s s_i \oplus \lambda_g g_i \oplus \lambda_t t_i \oplus \lambda_p p_i \tag{2}
$$

where the $$\lambda$$ terms represent relative weighting and $$\oplus$$ denotes structured composition rather than physical addition.

This formula is not itself the intervention. It is a compact description of the claim that semantics, geography, time, and continuity are constitutive dimensions of meaningful system state.

In the revised framing developed here, the expression also serves as a model of inner constraint-space structure. The experiment does not claim that this state is a quantum state in the physical sense. It claims instead that meaningful system behavior depends on whether this joint state survives projection into classical, shareable responses. If grounded records systematically outperform stripped records, and re-grounded records recover performance, then meaning can be read as the degree to which that inner joint state is preserved in outward representation.[4]

### 4.3 Meaning Score

For each response $$r_{ijc}$$, where $$i$$ indexes unit, $$j$$ indexes prompt type, and $$c$$ indexes condition, define the four-dimensional scoring vector:

$$
m_{ijc} = \big(\sigma_{ijc}, \kappa_{ijc}, \iota_{ijc}, \delta_{ijc}\big) \tag{3}
$$

where:

- $$\sigma$$ = semantic specificity,
- $$\kappa$$ = contextual coherence,
- $$\iota$$ = identity stability,
- $$\delta$$ = local disambiguation.

Each component takes values in $$\{0,1,2,3\}$$. The composite Situated Meaning Score is:

$$
M_{ijc} = \sigma_{ijc} + \kappa_{ijc} + \iota_{ijc} + \delta_{ijc} \in [0,12] \tag{4}
$$

Per-unit condition means are then:

$$
\bar{M}_{ic} = \frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J} M_{ijc} \tag{5}
$$

with paired contrasts:

$$
\Delta_i^{GS} = \bar{M}_{i,G} - \bar{M}_{i,S}, \qquad \Delta_i^{RGS} = \bar{M}_{i,RG} - \bar{M}_{i,S} \tag{6}
$$

where $$G$$, $$S$$, and $$RG$$ denote grounded, stripped, and re-grounded conditions respectively.

These equations make the mathematics of the protocol explicit. They do not prove that meaning is exhausted by the score. They define the operational measurement used in this study.

### 4.4 Semantic Invariance Condition

The most important control in this design is the preservation of semantic state across conditions. To minimize the objection that stripping changes semantics rather than grounding, semantic vectors will be frozen prior to intervention; only retrieval pathways, structured geographic and temporal fields, and selected provenance exposures will be altered. Embeddings will not be recomputed after stripping. This preserves the closest available approximation to semantic invariance while allowing the test to isolate the contribution of structured constraint spaces.

## 5. Methods

### 5.1 Study Design

The study uses a within-unit three-condition paired comparison design. Each selected unit is evaluated in three conditions:

1. **Grounded condition:** the unit is queried with its normal semantic, geographic, temporal, and provenance couplings intact.
2. **Stripped condition:** the unit is queried after a controlled removal or masking of geographic and temporal grounding while preserving frozen semantic vectors and symbolic content as much as technically possible.
3. **Re-grounded condition:** the unit is queried after geographic and temporal grounding are restored through controlled reinjection of the structured fields and retrieval pathways suppressed in the stripped condition.

The re-grounded condition is included to test reversibility. If degradation under stripping is partially or substantially reversed under re-grounding, the resulting pattern supports a stronger causal interpretation than a simple grounded-versus-stripped contrast alone.

### 5.2 Manuscript-Execution Status

As of July 6, 2026, the pre-execution infrastructure is fully verified and operational in protocol terms: the sampling frame is finalized, the stripped schemas have been created on test copies, withheld fields have been logged, and the Hilbert state slots for the three-condition design have been initialized. Execution of the scoring phase has not yet begun, and no scored results are claimed in this manuscript.

The manuscript therefore remains appropriate as a protocol paper, methods paper, or Registered Report Stage 1 draft.

### 5.3 Sampling Frame and Sample Size

The planned sample consists of 60 units stratified across three categories, with 20 units per category:

- Place-bound civic entities.
- Place-bound knowledge records.
- Ambiguous local referents whose correct interpretation depends on regional context or event timing.

The choice of 60 units is pragmatic but also analytically motivated. For a paired design, a medium effect size of roughly 0.5 reaches about 80 percent power in the mid-30s, whereas 60 paired units provide substantially higher power at conventional thresholds. The larger sample is retained to improve precision, support qualitative error analysis, and preserve margin for exclusions or unusable cases.

### 5.4 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

A unit is eligible if it satisfies all of the following:

- It has a stable semantic representation in the active system.
- It is linked to a specific geographic anchor or geospatial context.
- It has a temporal event association or event history relevant to interpretation.
- It has provenance continuity, identity continuity, or another documented continuity structure.
- It can be queried in a way that yields meaningful resolution rather than trivial lookup.

A unit is excluded if:

- It lacks a clear geographic or temporal component.
- It is too sparse to evaluate meaningfully in all three conditions.
- The stripped intervention would destroy the record entirely rather than isolate structured constraints.

### 5.5 Provenance Retention Levels

Because provenance can leak geographic or temporal information back into the stripped condition, provenance must be specified explicitly. This protocol defines three provenance retention levels:

- **Minimal provenance:** identifier continuity only; no timestamps, no locations, and no relational metadata beyond what is required for stable reference.
- **Structural provenance:** relationship structure retained, but timestamps, location fields, and other direct geographic or temporal indicators removed.
- **Full provenance:** original provenance retained without suppression.

The stripped condition will use minimal provenance retention as the default. A sensitivity analysis may optionally compare minimal and structural provenance retention in a secondary analysis to test for leakage effects.

### 5.6 Operational Definition of Stripped

Stripped does not mean deleting the unit from the system. It means masking or suppressing selected grounding variables while preserving lexical or semantic content, frozen embeddings, and record existence.

Operationally, the stripped condition is implemented on a test copy rather than on production data by the following procedure:

1. **Semantic preservation:** retain the unit's core text fields, identifiers required for retrieval, and the original frozen embedding representation.
2. **Geographic suppression:** null, mask, or disconnect explicit geospatial fields and geographic joins used for grounding.
3. **Temporal suppression:** null, mask, or disconnect temporal event links and ordering features used to preserve event continuity.
4. **Provenance minimization:** retain minimal provenance only, unless a declared sensitivity analysis uses structural provenance.
5. **Prompt parity:** use the same prompt set in grounded, stripped, and re-grounded conditions.

The schema-level appendix lists the actual fields, table columns, and service hooks altered during the intervention.

### 5.7 Operational Definition of Re-grounded

The re-grounded condition restores the same classes of structured constraint information removed during stripping. Specifically, geographic bindings, temporal event links, and authorized retrieval pathways are re-enabled while frozen semantic vectors remain unchanged. This condition is not intended to create a new semantic representation; it is intended to test whether restoring structured constraints recovers lost performance.

### 5.8 Query Set

Each unit will be evaluated with three prompt types:

- **Referential prompt:** asks what or who the unit is.
- **Contextual prompt:** asks for place-bound or event-bound context.
- **Disambiguation prompt:** asks the system to distinguish the unit from plausible alternatives.

This yields 180 primary query events for a 60-unit sample per condition set, before follow-up prompts.

### 5.9 Follow-Up Queries and Identity Stability

Each primary query will be followed by one continuity probe asking the system to maintain reference across a second turn. This provides a direct measure of identity stability. Identity stability is central because it operationalizes meaning as persistence across interaction, not merely correctness at a single step.

### 5.10 Scoring and Evaluation Rubric

Each response will be scored on four primary dimensions using a 0-3 ordinal rubric.

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Semantic specificity | Incorrect or generic | Broadly relevant but vague | Mostly specific with minor loss | Correct and specific |
| Contextual coherence | Context absent or wrong | Fragmentary context | Largely coherent | Fully coherent and appropriately grounded |
| Identity stability | Reference lost | Unstable reference | Mostly stable | Stable across turns |
| Local disambiguation | Fails to distinguish | Weak distinction | Mostly distinguishes | Correctly distinguishes local referent |

The composite score per response is defined in Equation 4.

A future extension may add a fifth dimension for value alignment or care integrity, allowing a direct classical probe of claims that alignment, reciprocity, or love modulate the transition from inner state to public representation.[4]

### 5.11 Who Scores

Responses will be scored by three raters:

- **Two blind raters** who are given the query, response, and gold-reference packet but are blinded to condition.
- **One system-familiar rater** with knowledge of local context and system architecture.

The primary outcome for inference will be based on the mean of the two blind-rater scores. The system-familiar rater will be used for qualitative error classification and secondary interpretive analysis, not for primary inference.

### 5.12 Gold Reference Packet

Each unit will have a gold-reference packet prepared before evaluation, containing the intended referent, the relevant geography, the relevant event or time context, and the correct disambiguation criteria. These packets are assembled prior to testing so that raters score against a precommitted reference rather than improvising after the fact.

### 5.13 Blinding Procedure

To support blinding:

- Response sets will be randomized and assigned anonymous IDs.
- Condition labels will be hidden from blind raters.
- Raters will not be told which hypothesis is primary.

Because the system-familiar rater cannot be fully blinded, that rater's scores will not drive the primary inferential test.

### 5.14 Reliability Checks

Inter-rater reliability will be assessed using Krippendorff's alpha for ordinal scoring, or weighted kappa for pairwise checks if alpha is infeasible in the first pass. If blind-rater reliability falls below 0.67, the rubric will be treated as insufficiently stable and the study will be reported as a pilot requiring revision rather than as a decisive test.

### 5.15 Statistical Analysis Plan

The primary outcome is the paired difference in composite score between grounded and stripped conditions, with a secondary paired comparison between stripped and re-grounded conditions.

Planned analyses:

1. Compute per-unit mean composite score in grounded, stripped, and re-grounded conditions.
2. Compute paired difference scores for grounded-minus-stripped and re-grounded-minus-stripped contrasts.
3. Use paired t-tests if assumptions are tolerably met; otherwise use Wilcoxon signed-rank tests.
4. Report effect size using Cohen's $$d_z$$ for paired comparisons or a matched-rank effect size as appropriate.
5. Report 95 percent confidence intervals for the mean paired differences.
6. Optionally use a repeated-measures model as a robustness check across all three conditions.

The primary threshold for statistical significance will be 0.05, two-tailed. Because the study is modest in sample size and partly exploratory, emphasis should be placed on effect size and confidence interval width, not on p-values alone.

For clarity, the principal estimands are:

$$
\mu_{GS} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta_i^{GS}, \qquad \mu_{RGS} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta_i^{RGS} \tag{7}
$$

where positive values indicate advantage for grounded or re-grounded conditions over stripped.

### 5.16 What Counts as Evidence of Degradation

A result will count as support for the primary hypothesis if all three conditions are met:

- The grounded condition outperforms the stripped condition on the primary composite score.
- The paired difference is statistically significant at the predeclared threshold or shows a clearly nontrivial effect size in a pilot-sized sample.
- The qualitative error analysis shows that degradation is substantively linked to the loss of structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraints rather than incidental technical failure.

A stronger causal result will be claimed if re-grounding restores performance relative to the stripped condition. A result will count as partial support if degradation appears in only some dimensions, especially contextual coherence, local disambiguation, or identity stability, but not in overall composite score. A result will count as non-support if grounded and stripped conditions do not differ materially, or if observed differences are attributable mainly to implementation artifacts rather than the removal of structured constraints as such.

### 5.17 Situated Meaning Degradation Modes

Quantitative scoring will be supplemented by structured qualitative coding of failure modes, here termed Situated Meaning Degradation Modes. These include:

- **Flattening:** local meaning collapses into generic category labels.
- **Geographic collapse:** place-specific or geography-specific interpretation is lost.
- **Temporal confusion:** event order or time relevance is lost.
- **Referential drift:** cross-turn continuity fails.
- **Disambiguation loss:** plausible alternatives are no longer properly separated.
- **Provenance incoherence:** identity chains no longer support stable interpretation.

These categories matter because they speak to mechanism, not only effect size. The anticipated failure mode is not total collapse, but degradation of situated interpretive structure.

## 6. Interpretation of Outcomes

| Outcome | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Significant grounded advantage | Structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraints are necessary for coherent meaning in this system at the level tested. |
| Significant re-grounding recovery | Degradation is at least partly reversible through restoration of structured constraints. |
| Partial grounded advantage | Structured constraints contribute materially, but meaning may be distributed across several factors. |
| No measurable difference | Symbol manipulation may be sufficient for coherent meaning at this level of analysis. |
| Unstable or unreliable results | Protocol, rubric, or implementation requires revision before substantive interpretation. |

A positive result would not prove Orch OR or demonstrate non-computable consciousness. It would support the weaker claim that structured geometric and geographic constraint spaces are constitutive factors in coherent meaning at a macro level. A negative result would constrain the domain in which Penrose-style geometry claims remain plausible.[2]

## 7. Data Sovereignty and Fayette County

The relationship between structured constraint spaces and community sovereignty is important, but the claim must be stated carefully. The present study does not prove that data sovereignty is an architectural necessity in every sense. What it can test is a narrower implication: whether meaning degrades when place-bound, event-bound, and geographic constraints are removed from records originating in a community-grounded system.

If such degradation is observed, one plausible inference is that some forms of community knowledge lose coherence when detached from the geography and temporal structures that help constitute them. In that case, the argument for data sovereignty becomes stronger not only in ethical terms but in structural ones: community data detached from community geography may become less specific, less contextually coherent, less stable referentially, or less able to support correct disambiguation, which are the very properties this protocol is designed to measure.

## 8. Limitations

Several limitations should be stated explicitly.

First, the use of Hilbert-style language is mathematical and structural, not a claim of ontological identity between semantic embeddings and physical quantum states.

Second, the protocol addresses semantic coherence in a deployed intelligence system, not subjective awareness. It therefore cannot resolve the hard problem of consciousness.

Third, even a strong positive result would not validate Penrose's or Faggin's full quantum implementation, especially given continuing critique of quantum consciousness proposals and the fact that the present apparatus is classical.[8][6]

Fourth, the stripped intervention depends on the current architecture of Ms. Allis. Because the system is evolving, a versioned implementation appendix should accompany execution so that the suppression procedure is reproducible against the exact schema and service layout used at test time.

Fifth, the mathematics in this paper is operational rather than foundational. Equations 1 through 7 define a measurement framework for meaning under constrained retrieval conditions. They do not establish a universal mathematics of consciousness, and the draft should resist language that implies more than that.

Finally, value-laden notions such as love may be testable here only in weak architectural analogues, such as care, reciprocity, privacy, provenance discipline, or community alignment. The present protocol creates a way to probe those analogues, but it does not directly test love as a primitive force of collapse.[4][5]

## 9. Conclusion

This manuscript presents a protocol-level plan for testing whether coherent meaning in Ms. Allis depends on structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraint spaces. Its contribution is not to claim completed results, but to specify in advance how such a claim can be tested with clarity, rigor, and falsifiability.

The broader philosophical significance is narrower than many consciousness theories, but still substantial. If grounded records systematically outperform stripped records and re-grounded records recover lost performance, then the study would provide evidence that structured spatial-temporal and geographic constraints matter for coherent meaning in at least one engineered, community-grounded intelligence system. If they do not, then the scope of geometry-based theories must be narrowed.[2]

The most defensible claim is therefore not that the study proves consciousness, quantum collapse, or love as an ontological primitive. The strongest academically rigorous claim is that the protocol defines and measures a situated meaning score under manipulations of semantics, geography, time, and provenance, thereby providing one empirical test surface on which stronger theories must either cash out or retreat. In that sense, the draft is strongest when it is modest about ontology and precise about measurement.[4][2]

**Correction confirmed by audit: `gbim_nine_axis` is a database VIEW, not a base table.** Its definition resolves to a `GROUP BY` query joining four base tables: `gbim_zcta_2020` (`lat`, `lon`), `zcta_county_rel` (county overlap fields), `building_profile` (`zip`), and `wv_parcels` (`land_use_class`, `floodrisks`). Because a view cannot have its columns masked via `UPDATE`, the stripping intervention for this axis must operate on the four **base tables** above, not on the view itself.

**`wv_parcels` ├ā┬ó├óŌĆÜ┬¼├óŌé¼┬Ø contains direct PII.** In addition to `land_use_class` and `floodrisks` (the two columns surfaced through the `gbim_nine_axis` view), this table also holds `fulllegaldescription`, `fullownername`, `fullowneraddress`, `fullphysicaladdress`, and a column literally named `sensitive`. These PII columns are not selected by the view and are not part of the stripping intervention's data path, but the table itself sits directly in the geographic-suppression surface. Access control is documented in A.2.5.

**Large-scale grounding context (confirmed accurate).** `wv_buildings` (2,121,008 rows), `wv_address_points_raw` (1,115,756 rows), `wv_gis_features` (1,981,232 rows), and `building_profile` (2,111,851 rows) are all present in the `msjarvis` database with row counts matching the original inventory almost exactly.

**Structural finding relevant to Section 5.6:** the geographic layer has effectively **no foreign-key constraints** (one FK in the entire `msjarvis` database, internal to PostGIS topology). Geographic coupling is performed at query time by the service layer, not by DDL-level joins. The stripping intervention therefore operates at (a) the column level on a test copy and (b) the service level.

- **Service hook:** `jarvis-gis-rag` (8004), endpoints `/gis_rag`, `/search`. Stripped condition disables routing to this service for test-copy queries.

## A.3 State service (cross-layer)
`jarvis-hilbert-state` (18092) exposes `/state/set`, `/state/get`, `/state/transition`, `/state/list`, `/state/{state_id}`, `/entanglement`, `/hp/ingest`, `/hp/query`, `/hp/entity/{entity_id}`, `/hp/health`, `/health`.

**Scoring oracle designation (confirmed July 6, 2026):** `/hp/query` is the **grounded scoring oracle**. It accepts a `CivicQuery` (fields: `query_text`, `n_results`, `registration_layer`, `doc_type`, `statement_type`, `role`, `effective_after`, `collections`) and returns structured results with full constraint-space routing. The three conditions use this endpoint as follows:
- **Grounded condition:** route through `/hp/query` with full `CivicQuery` populated (collections + role + registration_layer).
- **Stripped condition:** bypass `/hp/query`; query ChromaDB directly via `POST /api/v2/tenants/default_tenant/databases/default_database/collections/{name}/query` with a metadata projection filter excluding `geometry_wkt`, `lat`, `lon`, `layer_name`, and county/zip keys from returned documents. Vectors and document text remain untouched.
- **Re-grounded condition:** restore `/hp/query` routing identically to the grounded condition.

No cosine recomputation is required; the four rubric dimension scores (0├ā┬ó├óŌĆÜ┬¼├óŌé¼┼ō3 each) are derived from blind-rater evaluation of response content and written to the corresponding Hilbert state slot via `POST /state/set`.

**Protocol state initialization (completed July 6, 2026):** 180 `StateVector` slots have been written via `POST /state/set` ├ā┬ó├óŌĆÜ┬¼├óŌé¼┬Ø one per unit-condition pair (60 units ├āŲÆ├óŌé¼ŌĆØ 3 conditions: grounded, stripped, regrounded). Each slot carries `dimensions: {semantic_specificity: -1, contextual_coherence: -1, identity_stability: -1, local_disambiguation: -1, composite: -1}` and `metadata: {unit_id, condition, status: "pending", source_table, label}`. Three orphan slots from earlier diagnostic tests (`census_block_001_grounded`, `census_block_001_stripped`, `protocol_test_001`) were identified and deleted via `DELETE /state/{state_id}`. Confirmed slot count: exactly 180 via `GET /state/list`.

The `StateVector` schema (from `/openapi.json`) accepts: `state_id` (string, required), `dimensions` (dict of numbers, optional, default `{}`), `metadata` (dict, optional, default `{}`), `timestamp` (number, optional). The DELETE route is confirmed live and functional.
