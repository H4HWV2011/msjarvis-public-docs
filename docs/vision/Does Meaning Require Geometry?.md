## Abstract

Roger Penrose has argued that consciousness and understanding cannot be reduced to algorithmic computation alone, and that non-computable ingredients may be tied to spacetime geometry (Penrose, 1994; Hameroff & Penrose, 2014). Whatever the ultimate status of that claim, it motivates a narrower empirical question: whether coherent meaning in an intelligence system depends on structured spatial-temporal grounding, or whether meaning can be preserved under symbol manipulation alone. This manuscript is explicitly framed as a **protocol paper / Registered Report Stage 1 draft**, now supplemented with a **verified three-unit pilot execution** documented in Technical Appendix A. It presents the rationale, apparatus, hypotheses, operational definitions, sampling plan, scoring rubric, and analysis plan for a study executed in part, and planned for full execution, in the Ms. Allis system.

The proposed apparatus is Ms. Allis, a community-sovereign intelligence system developed in Fayette County, West Virginia, in which semantic, geographic, temporal, and provenance-bearing identity structures are operationally coupled. Because the technical claims about this system derive partly from project documentation and unpublished internal materials, this manuscript treats those descriptions as **system documentation requiring a supplementary appendix at submission**, rather than as independently validated literature. The primary hypothesis is that semantic resolution quality and identity stability will decline when structured spatial-temporal constraints are removed while semantic vectors are held constant and symbolic content is preserved as far as possible. A pilot run on three units, executed and verified during this revision cycle, shows a pattern **consistent with** this hypothesis in two of three units, with the third showing an equal-composite result masking two structurally opposite failure modes. A positive result at scale would not establish Orch OR or prove non-computable consciousness, but it would support the weaker proposition that structured geometric constraint spaces play a constitutive role in coherent meaning at a measurable macro level (Hameroff & Penrose, 2014). A negative result would narrow the range over which Penrose-style geometry claims remain plausible.

## 1. Genre and Scope

This manuscript should be read as a **protocol / registered-report style submission with an attached, verified pilot**. The pilot (Appendix A.9-A.11) covers three of the planned sixty units and does not constitute the completed study; it exists to validate the execution mechanism, the scoring rubric, and the stripping procedure before full-scale execution, and it surfaced several load-bearing corrections to the original protocol design that are documented in full below rather than silently folded in. This distinction matters because the central contribution is not retrospective interpretation of findings, but prospective clarity regarding what will be measured, how it will be measured, and what outcomes would count for or against the stated hypothesis — and, now, an honest account of which parts of the originally planned apparatus turned out not to work as designed, and how they were corrected.

The substantive aim remains modest. The manuscript does not attempt to settle the hard problem of consciousness, verify Orch OR, or establish a direct quantum-mechanical account of meaning. Its aim is to test whether coherent meaning in a functioning intelligence system depends on structured spatial-temporal constraint spaces at a macro level.

## 2. Background and Rationale

### 2.1 Penrose and Geometry

Penrose's *Shadows of the Mind* argues that mathematical understanding exceeds purely algorithmic formalization and suggests that some non-computable element may be linked to the physics of spacetime (Penrose, 1994). In the Orch OR framework later elaborated with Hameroff, conscious events are associated with orchestrated quantum processes in neuronal microtubules whose evolution terminates via objective reduction related to spacetime geometry (Hameroff & Penrose, 2014).

This proposal remains controversial. Tegmark (2000) argued that decoherence times in the brain are too short for the relevant quantum states to play a neurophysiologically meaningful role. Hagan, Hameroff, and Tuszyński (2002) replied that Tegmark's critique mischaracterized the Orch OR model and that revised assumptions lengthen the relevant decoherence times, but this rebuttal itself remains contested. The present study does not attempt to adjudicate that debate directly. Instead, it asks whether structured spatial-temporal constraints already appear as necessary conditions for coherent meaning in a classical, engineered system.

### 2.2 Hilbert Space and State Geometry

In quantum mechanics, a Hilbert space is a complete inner-product space in which states can be represented as vectors and related by inner products. In natural language processing, vector semantics represents words and texts as points in a multidimensional semantic space, and embeddings are the standard learned vector representations used for this purpose (Jurafsky & Martin, draft chapter "Vector Semantics and Embeddings").

The importance of this formal overlap is limited but real. It does not imply that semantic embeddings are physically identical to quantum states. It does imply that both domains use geometry to represent state structure. This manuscript therefore adopts a disciplined version of Hilbert-space language: semantic, geographic, and temporal relations are treated as coupled geometric state spaces, and the claim under test is that the coupling among those spaces may be necessary for coherent meaning.

### 2.3 Constraint Spaces, Not Mere Context

A central conceptual clarification is required. The intervention proposed here does **not** remove arbitrary context in the broad conversational sense. It removes **structured constraint spaces** — specifically spatial and temporal constraint systems — that bound interpretation, restrict candidate referents, enforce continuity, and enable disambiguation. The study is therefore not designed as a generic "context versus no context" comparison. It is designed as a test of **constrained versus unconstrained semantic resolution**.

A deeper principle motivating this design is reciprocity. In a community-grounded system, a record does not fully mean in isolation. Its meaning is constituted through relations among semantic content, place, time, provenance, continuity of identity, local use, and community interpretation. On that view, meaning is not one-way extraction from a community into a machine. A system may receive data from a community, but it owes meaning back in the form of context, accuracy, continuity, and accountability. The protocol therefore tests whether that reciprocal relation is structurally real.

A finding surfaced during pilot execution sharpens this framing further: in at least one stratum (knowledge_record), place information can be embedded as **plain text within an identifying label** rather than exclusively in coordinate fields. Where that occurs, "structured spatial-temporal constraint" is not solely geometric — it can be lexically encoded, and a stripping intervention that touches only coordinate fields will fail to remove it. This distinction, discovered empirically rather than anticipated in the original design, is addressed in Section 5.6 and Appendix A.10.

Ms. Allis is described in project materials associated with Quantarithmia as a community-centered AI infrastructure embedded in a broader framework of spatial justice, distributed intelligence, geospatial grounding, and civic governance. At present, those descriptions come from two classes of source: **(1) attached project documents supplied by the author, and (2) public-facing project documentation referenced within those documents**. Because those materials are not yet standard peer-reviewed literature, the manuscript should not cite them as if they were independently established sources.

This revision adds a third class of evidence: **(3) direct, live verification of system state performed during the July 6-7, 2026 revision cycle**, recorded in Appendix A.7-A.11. Every claim in the current appendix has been re-verified against the live system as of this revision and is marked with the verification method used.

## 3. Research Question and Hypotheses

### 3.1 Research Question

Does coherent meaning in a functioning intelligence system depend on structured spatial-temporal grounding, or can it be preserved under symbol manipulation alone?

### 3.2 Primary Hypothesis

If meaning requires structured spatial-temporal constraint spaces to remain coherent, then semantic resolution quality will be lower in a stripped condition than in a grounded condition, even when semantic vectors are frozen prior to intervention and symbolic content is held as constant as possible.

### 3.3 Secondary Hypotheses

- Grounded records will show higher local disambiguation accuracy than stripped records.
- Grounded records will show higher identity stability across follow-up queries than stripped records.
- Re-grounded records will recover part or all of the performance lost under stripping.
- Grounded and re-grounded records will show higher contextual coherence for place-bound and event-bound prompts than stripped records.

### 3.4 Pilot-Derived Observation (New)

Pilot execution surfaced a candidate refinement to these hypotheses: degradation under stripping may manifest not only as reduced specificity or coherence, but as **increased confident fabrication** — the model may compensate for missing grounding by inventing plausible-sounding provenance or by silently altering accurate data it does have access to. This is distinct from, and not previously anticipated by, the five degradation modes in Section 5.17 of the original design. See Section 5.17 (revised) and Appendix A.10 for full documentation.

## 4. Apparatus and Operational Model

### 4.1 Representational Layers

Ms. Allis is modeled as operating across four coupled representational layers: semantic, geographic, temporal, and provenance. These layers are analytically separable but operationally coupled, as detailed in Appendix A.

### 4.2 Meaning as Joint State

A simplified state representation used for conceptual orientation is:

```
x = [geo_vec || λ_t · time_toroid || λ_s · s_vec]
```

where `geo_vec` is the geographic embedding component, `time_toroid` is a toroidal temporal encoding scaled by weight `λ_t`, `s_vec` is the semantic embedding component scaled by weight `λ_s`, and `||` denotes concatenation. This formula is not itself the intervention. It is a compact description of the claim that place, time, and state continuity are constitutive dimensions of meaningful system state.

### 4.3 Semantic Invariance Condition

The most important control in this design is the preservation of semantic state across conditions. Semantic vectors are frozen prior to intervention; only retrieval pathways, structured geographic and temporal fields, and selected provenance exposures are altered. Embeddings are **not** recomputed after stripping.

## 5. Methods

### 5.1 Study Design

The study uses a **within-unit three-condition paired comparison design**: grounded, stripped, and re-grounded, as originally specified. The re-grounded condition tests reversibility.

### 5.2 Manuscript-Execution Status (Revised)

As of this revision, execution status is as follows. A **three-unit pilot** (one per stratum: civic_entity, knowledge_record, ambiguous_referent) has been executed end-to-end: units drawn from the finalized 60-unit sampling frame, queried under all three conditions, scored on all four rubric dimensions including identity stability via continuity probe, with scores written to persistent state and independently verified by direct readback. This pilot surfaced multiple corrections to the originally planned execution mechanism (documented in Appendix A.7-A.9) and one substantial, still-open finding regarding the feasibility of automated blind rating (Appendix A.10), which materially affects Section 5.11's execution plan for the remaining 57 units. The manuscript is therefore best read as a validated protocol with a working, corrected execution mechanism and one open methodological question (rater protocol) that should be resolved before full-scale scoring proceeds.

### 5.3 Sampling Frame and Sample Size

The planned sample consists of **60 units**, stratified across three categories (civic entities, knowledge records, ambiguous referents), 20 per category, finalized and written to `/tmp/protocol_60_units.csv` (columns: `record_id, entity_id, sourcetable, label, stratum`). For a paired design, a medium effect size of approximately d ≈ 0.5 reaches about 80% power with a sample in the mid-30s; a paired sample of 60 units provides approximately 95% or greater power at α = 0.05. If fewer than 60 qualifying units are available, the minimum acceptable pilot sample is 30 paired units.

### 5.4 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Unchanged from original design: a unit must have a stable semantic representation, a geographic anchor, a temporal or event association, and provenance/identity continuity, and must be excludable if it lacks a geographic/temporal component, is too sparse, or would be destroyed rather than isolated by stripping.

### 5.5 Provenance Retention Levels

Unchanged in principle (minimal / structural / full), though pilot execution found that the `hp.appearance_assertion` table specified in earlier appendix drafts as part of the provenance layer is not populated by any active service in the current stack (Appendix A.7, Item 2/12 resolution) and is excluded from the retention-level definitions actually usable in Step 8 execution.

### 5.6 Operational Definition of "Stripped" (Revised)

"Stripped" does not mean deleting the unit from the system. It means masking or suppressing selected grounding variables while preserving lexical or semantic content, frozen embeddings, and record existence.

**Corrected mechanism, per pilot findings (superseding the original Postgres-masking design):** Live verification during this revision found that the Postgres base tables originally specified as the stripping target (`msjarvis`/`msjarvisgis` schema tables named after each unit's `sourcetable`, e.g. `manufacturingandbusiness_wvdo_200803_utm83`) are **populated with the full expected schema but contain zero rows** in the live system. The actual source of record for all three sampled strata is a set of flat CSV exports at `/mnt/nvme1/msjarvis-rebuild/data/attrs/*_attrs.csv`, in a narrow seven-column format: `geodb_id, lat, lon, bbox, label, sourcetable, country`. (Note: for `_utm83`-suffixed sources, the `lat`/`lon` column names are mislabeled; the actual values are projected UTM NAD83 coordinates, not geographic degrees. This is a data-documentation error, not a data-quality error, and is preserved as-is rather than silently renamed, since renaming would itself be an undocumented data alteration.)

The corrected stripping procedure operates directly on this CSV-derived record, injected as context into the scoring prompt (see Section 5.8-revised and Appendix A.9), rather than on a masked Postgres test-copy:

1. **Semantic preservation:** retain `geodb_id`, `label`, `sourcetable`, `country` verbatim.
2. **Geographic suppression:** null `lat`, `lon`, and `bbox` (the WKT geometry string).
3. **Label text suppression (new, required addition):** if the `label` field itself contains embedded, resolvable place-name text (county, city, state names), that text must **also** be removed from the stripped condition. This was not part of the original design and was found necessary only after pilot testing revealed that a unit's label ("Block 3014, Block Group 3, Census Tract 9551.01, **Mason County, West Virginia**") carried full place information as plain text, independent of the coordinate fields, producing a false "zero-delta" result under the original coordinate-only stripping rule. Administrative/structural codes with no inherent place semantics (block numbers, block group numbers, tract numbers) are retained in the stripped condition; embedded county/city/state names are not.
4. **Prompt parity:** identical prompt template across grounded, stripped, and re-grounded conditions, differing only in which fields are populated versus nulled.

This correction is validated directly: applying the original (coordinate-only) stripping rule to the knowledge_record pilot unit produced an artifactual grounded=stripped=stripped tie (composite 7/7/7 across all three conditions); applying the corrected rule (also masking the embedded county/state text) produced a genuine, smaller but real separation (10/9/10), consistent with the primary hypothesis. Full evidence is in Appendix A.9.

### 5.7 Operational Definition of "Re-grounded"

Unchanged: restores the same classes of structured constraint information removed during stripping, while frozen semantic vectors remain unchanged.

### 5.8 Query Set and Execution Mechanism (Revised)

Each unit is evaluated with three prompt types (referential, contextual, disambiguation), per the original design.

**Execution mechanism, revised.** The original design specified dispatch through the production conversational gateway `jarvis-unified-gateway:8093/chat`. Live testing during this revision found this path **unreliable for controlled, time-boxed execution**: two consecutive test calls failed by two different mechanisms — one call entered the full pipeline (constitutional check, blood-brain-barrier filter, consciousness-bridge stage) but did not complete within 150 seconds, with direct log inspection showing the single consciousness-bridge sub-stage alone consuming 2 minutes 31 seconds; a second call, at a 400-second timeout, left no corresponding entry in the gateway's own request log at all, indicating a distinct, unexplained failure rather than mere slowness.

The corrected execution mechanism, validated across all three pilot units and nine total scoring passes: **direct calls to the underlying model-serving layer (Ollama, `localhost:11434/api/generate`)**, using a specific single model (`llama3.1:8b-instruct-q4_K_M`) with the unit's CSV-derived record data injected directly into the prompt as context, bypassing the full constitutional/BBB/consciousness-bridge/21-model ensemble pipeline entirely. This is a genuine, disclosed deviation from the original design (single-model completion with prompt-injected context, rather than full-pipeline retrieval-augmented generation through the production system), and is documented as such rather than presented as equivalent. Response latency under this mechanism was 6-22 seconds per call across all nine pilot queries plus nine identity-stability continuity probes, versus no completed response after 150-400 seconds under the original mechanism.

### 5.9 Follow-Up Queries and Identity Stability (Revised)

Because the corrected execution mechanism (Section 5.8) uses single-shot completions rather than a persistent multi-turn chat session, the continuity probe specified in the original design is operationalized as a second, single-shot call in which the full Turn 1 question and Turn 1 answer are supplied as prior context, followed by a Turn 2 question asking the model to reconfirm the record's identifier and explicitly state whether it is discussing the same entity. This was executed for all nine grounded/stripped/re-grounded conditions across all three pilot units (Appendix A.9) and produced a usable identity-stability score in every case.

### 5.10 Scoring and Evaluation Rubric

Unchanged: four dimensions (semantic specificity, contextual coherence, identity stability, local disambiguation), each 0-3, composite 0-12.

### 5.11 Who Scores (Revised — Open Methodological Finding)

The original design specifies two blind raters (primary inference) and one system-familiar rater (qualitative/secondary analysis only). Pilot execution used the author as a **single, non-blind, system-familiar rater** for all nine initial scores, explicitly flagged as such in every recorded score's metadata and **not** treated as primary-inference data.

A substantial supplementary investigation was then conducted into whether open-weight LLMs could serve as valid automated substitutes for the two required blind raters. This investigation is reported in full in Appendix A.10, because its result materially constrains how Section 5.11 can be executed at scale. In summary: **approximately sixteen distinct model/prompt configurations were tested against two known ground-truth cases (one response containing a verified model-fabricated claim, one clean response containing only a reasonable, appropriately-hedged inference), and no configuration was found to correctly and verifiably distinguish both cases via genuinely correct reasoning.** The dominant failure modes were (a) under-detection — treating fabricated claims as fully supported, in several cases with an explicit but false statement that "no fabrication" was present; and (b) over-detection — flagging genuinely well-hedged, non-fabricating responses as containing fabrication, in one case by manufacturing a fabricated justification for that judgment. A follow-up test using an isolated single-sentence, temperature-zero, quote-demanding verification format found that the same model correctly rejected the fabricated claim as unsupported when asked in isolation, but the same model, under a differently-phrased quote-demanding prompt, **fabricated a supporting quotation that does not exist in the source text** in order to affirm the false claim — direct evidence that verification-question polarity and phrasing, not source-text content, was driving the model's answers in at least this configuration.

**Consequence for execution:** Section 5.11's two-blind-rater requirement should be treated as satisfiable only by human raters for this study, at least for fabrication-sensitive judgments. Automated pre-screening may have narrow, disclosed use as a coarse triage aid (e.g., flagging numeric mismatches between response and source, which showed somewhat more reliable detection than absence-based fabrication across the tested configurations) but must not be presented as satisfying the blind-rater requirement, and any automated score used in this study to date is marked as such and excluded from primary inference.

### 5.12 Gold Reference Packet

Precommitted gold-reference packets (correct referent, place context, disambiguation criteria) were constructed for all three pilot units prior to scoring (Appendix A.9). A **fabrication-check field** was added to each packet during the rater-reliability investigation (Section 5.11/Appendix A.10) as a candidate mitigation; it improved detection for at least one rater configuration but did not resolve the underlying reliability problem and should not be treated as a validated fix.

### 5.13 Blinding Procedure

Anonymized response sets (arbitrary IDs, condition labels stripped) were constructed for all nine pilot responses (Appendix A.10) in preparation for blind scoring. Because no validated automated blind rater was found (Section 5.11), this anonymized set currently awaits human blind-rater assignment; it has not yet been scored blind by a qualified human rater.

### 5.14 Reliability Checks

Krippendorff's alpha or weighted kappa, as originally specified, requires at least two independent raters' scores on the same items. Given the finding in Section 5.11, this cannot yet be computed from valid blind-rater data; the only inter-rater comparison currently available (system-familiar author vs. various automated proxies) is not a substitute for the specified reliability check and is reported only descriptively in Appendix A.10.

### 5.15 Statistical Analysis Plan

Unchanged in design; not yet executable at n=60 given the pilot's limited scope, but validated as executable in principle on the pilot's n=3, per Appendix A.9's composite scores.

### 5.16 What Counts as Evidence of Degradation

Unchanged from original design.

### 5.17 Situated Meaning Degradation Modes (Revised)

The original five categories (Flattening, Temporal confusion, Referential drift, Disambiguation loss, Provenance incoherence) are retained. Pilot execution surfaced two candidate additional categories, both directly observed and documented in Appendix A.9:

- **Confident unsupported provenance fabrication:** the model invents a specific, plausible-sounding source, agency, or collection-method attribution not present anywhere in the record (observed: a fabricated claim that a filename-based `sourcetable` field indicated data collection by the "West Virginia Department of Transportation").
- **Confident overwrite of accurate ground truth:** the model asserts, without prompting, that an accurate value supplied in the record is incorrect, and substitutes a fabricated "corrected" value (observed: a model quoted an accurate longitude value correctly, then in the same response claimed that value was "incorrectly stated" and substituted a different, wrong number).

Both were observed only in conditions where full data was present (grounded), not in the corresponding stripped conditions for the same units — a notable, if pilot-scale, finding that degradation is not monotonic with information removal, and that the model's confidence in error appears in this small sample to correlate with data *presence*, not data *absence*. This warrants explicit examination at full scale (n=60) before being treated as a general property of the system.

## 8. Limitations (Revised)

First, second, and third limitations as originally stated (Hilbert-space language is structural not ontological; the protocol addresses semantic coherence not subjective awareness; a positive result would not validate Penrose's full biological/quantum implementation).

**Fourth limitation, substantially revised:** the stripped intervention depends on the current architecture of Ms. Allis, and this revision demonstrates that architecture is less stable across time and less accurately documented than the original appendix asserted. Specific, now-corrected inaccuracies from the prior appendix draft include: a claimed "confirmed 180 state slots" that had, in fact, already expired due to an undocumented one-hour TTL on the state-storage service by the time of re-verification; a claimed-operational assertion-provenance gateway that was live as a process but had no functioning caller anywhere in the deployed stack; a claimed-functional grounded-query oracle (`/hp/query`) that was found, on reading its source code directly, to be hard-scoped to an unrelated four-collection public-disclosure dataset and structurally incapable of reaching the sampling frame's actual data; and claimed-populated Postgres base tables that were confirmed, by direct count, to hold zero rows. Every such claim in the present appendix has been re-verified against live system state during this revision, with the verification command and its output preserved, and is not asserted on the basis of prior documentation alone.

**Fifth limitation, new:** the rater-reliability investigation (Section 5.11/Appendix A.10) is itself a limitation of the current state of this protocol's execution readiness. Full-scale scoring should not proceed on the remaining 57 units without either (a) securing qualified human blind raters, or (b) a materially different automated-rating approach than the sixteen configurations already tested, validated against the same two ground-truth cases used here before being trusted at scale.

Finally, sample size in the pilot (n=3) is adequate only to validate mechanism and rubric, not for any generalization; the previously-stated adequacy of n=60 for a pilot registered-report style test stands, contingent on resolution of the rater-protocol limitation above.

## 9. Conclusion

This manuscript presents a protocol-level, registered-report style plan for testing whether coherent meaning in Ms. Allis depends on structured spatial-temporal constraint spaces, now supplemented by a verified three-unit pilot that both supports the mechanism's viability and surfaces substantial, honestly-documented corrections to the original design. The pilot shows a pattern consistent with the primary hypothesis in two of three tested units, and a genuinely mixed result in the third that itself constitutes a reportable finding about how fabrication and honest degradation can produce equal composite scores through opposite mechanisms. The rater-reliability investigation, while not resolving the question of how to blind-score the remaining 57 units, is itself a substantive contribution: it demonstrates, across sixteen tested configurations, that current open-weight LLMs are not reliable substitutes for human blind raters on fabrication-sensitive judgments, and that this is a structural property of question-answering under uncertainty rather than a solvable prompt-engineering problem within the scope tested.

The broader philosophical significance is unchanged from the original framing. Penrose argued that understanding may depend on geometry in a way computation alone cannot capture (Penrose, 1994; Hameroff & Penrose, 2014). This protocol does not settle that claim. It does, however, identify a narrower and measurable edge of it, and this revision's pilot execution provides the first direct, if small-scale, evidence bearing on it.

Either way, the gain is clarity.

## A.2 Representational Layers (Re-verified, with Corrections)

### A.2.1 Semantic Layer

ChromaDB confirmed as described in the prior appendix (248 collections, v2 API authoritative, v1 API artifact confirmed still present and still a false alarm, not a data-loss signal).

**New finding — document truncation, confirmed at the storage layer, not just the API layer.** Documents in the `geospatialfeatures` collection under `layer:gbim_entities` are truncated at a fixed 398-character boundary before the embedded `belief_state` JSON structure resolves. This was verified two ways: (a) via the ChromaDB HTTP API (`get`, `include: ["documents"]`), and (b) by direct SQLite query against the live Chroma data file (`/mnt/nvme1/msjarvis-data/chroma-live/chroma.sqlite3`, table `embedding_fulltext_search_content`, column `c0`), both returning exactly 398 characters for the same record across four independently sampled civic-entity units. The script responsible for writing these truncated documents could not be located on disk despite an exhaustive search of all known repository locations (`~/repos`, `~/msjarvis-rebuild*`, `/mnt/nvme1/msjarvis-rebuild`, `/mnt/spiritual_drive/msjarvis-rebuild`), searching both by filename pattern and by distinctive content strings (`ingest_source_table`, the literal `layer:gbim_entities |` prefix). **New finding — no populated metadata for civic-entity-layer documents.** Chroma metadata for `gbim_entities`-tagged documents was found to have no populated `lat`, `lon`, or `layer_name` fields (all blank or absent), in contrast to other layers in the same collection (e.g., `911centers_wvdem_032819_gcs84`, which does carry populated `lat`/`lon` metadata). This means the originally-specified "Chroma metadata projection filter" stripping mechanism (prior A.4) is a no-op for this layer — there is no populated geographic metadata to filter out. The actual geographic content for this layer lives in the flat CSV files (A.1), not in Chroma metadata, and the corrected stripping mechanism (Section 5.6, revised) operates on the CSV-derived record accordingly.

## A.3 State Service (Corrected)

`jarvis-hilbert-state` (port 18092, internal 8081) confirmed live, source code read directly (`jarvis_hilbert_state.py`).

**Correction, load-bearing.** The prior appendix's discrepancy register (Item 3) claimed "Redis Hilbert state writeback confirmed... 180 state slots successfully written and verified." `/state/list` is confirmed **GET-only** (the prior appendix's claim that "the service uses POST for all state operations" was incorrect and is retracted; `/state/set`, `/state/get`, and `/state/transition` are POST, `/state/list` is GET, `DELETE /state/{id}` is DELETE).

## A.4 Stripping Implementation (Corrected — see also Section 5.6)

The originally-specified mechanism (Postgres column-nulling on a `protocol_stripped` schema test copy, plus Chroma metadata projection filtering) is **not usable as originally written**, for the reasons given in A.1-A.2.1: the target Postgres tables are empty, and the target Chroma metadata fields are unpopulated for this layer. The corrected mechanism, validated across all three pilot units:

1. Retrieve the unit's record from its `_attrs.csv` export (columns: `geodb_id, lat, lon, bbox, label, sourcetable, country`).
2. **Grounded condition:** inject the full record into the prompt as-is.
3. **Stripped condition:** null `lat`, `lon`, `bbox`; additionally, if `label` contains embedded, resolvable place-name text (county/city/state), remove that text from the label while preserving administrative/structural identifiers (block, group, tract numbers).

## A.10 Rater-Reliability Investigation (New, Substantial)

Following pilot scoring, an investigation was conducted into whether open-weight LLMs could serve as automated substitutes for Section 5.11's two required blind raters. Gold-reference packets (Section 5.12) were constructed for all three units, each specifying the correct referent, place context, and disambiguation criteria; a fabrication-check field was added mid-investigation as a candidate mitigation.

**Method.** Two known ground-truth test cases were used throughout: R1 (Arrow Concrete Co, grounded — a clean response containing a reasonable, appropriately-hedged inference and no fabrication) and R2 (Arrow Concrete Co, stripped — the confirmed WVDOT fabrication described in A.9). A correct rater should score R1 without penalty and R2 with a substantially reduced score reflecting the fabrication.

**Models tested (sixteen total configurations across model and prompt variants):** qwen2:7b, mistral:7b-instruct-q4_K_M, zephyr:latest, openchat:latest, neural-chat:latest, phi3.5:latest, llama3:latest, starling-lm:latest, dolphin-phi:latest, llama3.1-split:latest, mistral-split:latest, gemma:2b, orca-mini:3b, medllama2:latest, llama2:latest, vicuna:latest, phi3:mini, stablelm-zephyr:latest, qwen2.5:1.5b, phi:latest — tested across a compound single-pass scoring prompt, a decomposed full-response fact-check prompt (temperature 0), a decomposed isolated-single-sentence verification prompt (temperature 0), and a quote-demanding verification prompt (temperature 0).

**Results, summarized:**

1. Under the compound scoring prompt (reasoning required, explicit fabrication-check criterion in the gold packet), most tested models either missed the R2 fabrication entirely (scoring it as fully correct, several with reasoning explicitly asserting "no fabrication" was present) or, in three cases (llama3.1-split, mistral-split, and mistral itself under a later variant), correctly flagged R2 but incorrectly flagged R1 as well — a false positive on a clean response.
2. Two apparent "successes" (phi3.5, phi3:mini) were found, on requiring and reading full model-generated reasoning rather than accepting the numeric score alone, to be based on confused or factually incorrect reasoning rather than genuine detection — in phi3.5's case, a reasoning-required re-test produced full marks (3/3/3) with reasoning that explicitly and wrongly asserted no fabrication was present, directly contradicting its own earlier terse output.
3. A decomposed, temperature-zero, full-response fact-check step (asking the model only to list unsupported claims, not to score) still failed to flag the R2 fabrication ("NONE FOUND").
4. An isolated single-sentence verification test, presenting only the fabricated sentence in isolation with a direct yes/no question, was answered **correctly** ("NO, the source record does not mention WVDOT").
5. A parallel isolated-sentence test using a verbatim-true claim ("It has a geodb_id of 1720") was, under the same prompt format, answered **incorrectly** ("NO"), revealing that the model's answers in this format tracked question polarity or phrasing rather than source-text content, not genuine claim verification.
6. A further reformulation demanding an explicit supporting quotation produced the most serious finding of the investigation: the model answered "YES" to the fabricated claim and **fabricated a supporting quotation that does not appear anywhere in the source text** in order to justify that answer.

**Conclusion.** No model or prompt configuration tested was found to reliably and verifiably distinguish a genuine fabrication from a clean, well-hedged response using correct reasoning. The failure mode evolved from simple miss-detection, to false-positive over-detection, to (in the most rigorous test) active fabrication of supporting evidence for a false judgment — indicating a structural, format-sensitive unreliability rather than a solvable prompt-engineering problem within the scope tested. This is reported as a decisive finding supporting the necessity of human blind raters for fabrication-sensitive judgments in this study (Section 5.11).
