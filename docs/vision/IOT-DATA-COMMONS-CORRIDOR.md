## The Original Vision

The Pax mine rail corridor running through Mount Hope, West Virginia, represents a physical spine connecting small rail towns across the New River Gorge. The original concept: deploy solar-powered LoRaWAN sensor nodes along this corridor to collect hyperlocal environmental and infrastructure data — and route the economic value of that data back into the MountainShares treasury and the communities that host the sensors.

The intuition was structural. Rural Appalachian communities generate real-world data — air quality, water levels, soil conditions, rail vibration, flood patterns, atmospheric readings — that research institutions, environmental agencies, infrastructure planners, and insurance actuaries currently cannot access because no sensor infrastructure exists. That absence is not a natural condition. It is a consequence of disinvestment. The data has value. The community should capture it.

LoRaWAN was identified as the appropriate protocol because it operates at low power, requires no cellular infrastructure, achieves multi-mile range in terrain-appropriate deployments, and supports solar-only operation after initial installation. The New River Gorge corridor — 53 miles, seven gateway communities, a National Park — is ideally suited to LoRaWAN mesh deployment.

## The Municipal Layer

The vision extends beyond Harmony for Hope and the MountainShares treasury. Small rail towns — Pax, Thurmond, Minden, Oak Hill, Mount Hope — have no municipal sensor infrastructure of any kind. This absence has concrete consequences:

- Federal grant programs requiring baseline environmental data are inaccessible without it
- Flood damage, industrial incidents, and infrastructure degradation cannot be documented in real time
- Municipal planning and capital investment decisions proceed without evidentiary support
- Negotiating positions with developers, extractive industries, or state agencies are structurally weakened

A community-owned sensor network addresses all four conditions simultaneously. The proposed model treats municipalities not as data purchasers but as **co-owners** of sensor nodes sited on municipal property. Revenue generated by external data licensing — to research institutions, federal agencies, environmental monitors, and commercial buyers — splits across three channels: the MountainShares treasury, direct municipal budgets, and sensor network maintenance reserves.

Small towns with no tax base gain a productive infrastructure asset. The community retains ownership. The data never leaves without community consent and compensation.

A flood sensor reading on Arbuckle Creek does not merely log a data point — it updates the geospatial belief state of every downstream parcel, road segment, and facility in the H_App ⊗ H_geo composite space. The belief propagates through semantic entanglement. Queries about flood risk in that corridor become more accurate. Emergency response routing improves. Insurance risk modeling sharpens.

## The Data Commons Model

What this vision describes has an established name in the academic and policy literature: a **Data Cooperative** or **Data Commons**. Community members collectively own the data-generating infrastructure, govern access and licensing terms, and receive proportional economic benefit from the data their community produces.

Key structural characteristics of the MountainShares Data Commons model as envisioned:

- **Community ownership** — sensor hardware owned collectively, not by any external vendor or platform
- **Governed access** — licensing terms set by community governance, not by outside parties
- **Revenue sharing** — economic value flows to treasury and municipal partners, not extracted to external platforms
- **Sovereignty** — no data leaves the commons without community consent encoded in governance rules
- **Perpetual benefit** — unlike grant-funded projects that end when funding ends, a productive sensor network generates ongoing revenue independent of the grant cycle

This model is consistent with the broader MountainShares constitutional architecture and the Benefits-Sensitive Account design philosophy: participant protection and community ownership are hard design constraints, not aspirational features.

## Corridor Scale

Phase 1 (Mount Hope): Proof of concept deployment on the Pax mine rail corridor and adjacent municipal infrastructure. Environmental baselines, flood monitoring on key creek crossings, rail vibration monitoring.

Phase 2 (Gateway Communities): Replication across seven New River Gorge corridor communities. Each community governs its own node cluster. Revenue flows locally. Ms. Allis belief states update across the full corridor.

Phase 3 (West Virginia): The corridor model exports to other rail and river corridors across the state's 55 counties. County Champions already envisioned in the MountainShares architecture become sensor network stewards.

## Open Questions

The following are unresolved as of the date of this document and are recorded honestly to mark the boundary of current thinking:

1. **Treasury connection mechanics** — The precise mechanism by which sensor data revenue flows into the MountainShares EMS treasury and municipal accounts has not been formally specified. Smart contract architecture on the Arbitrum pathway is the likely mechanism but has not been designed for this use case.

2. **Data licensing framework** — What entity holds licensing rights? What governance process approves external access requests? What pricing model applies to federal agency access versus commercial buyers?

3. **Hardware procurement and maintenance** — Unit economics of LoRaWAN node deployment at corridor scale. Maintenance responsibility. Replacement cycle financing.

4. **Regulatory and liability considerations** — Environmental sensor data carries potential regulatory implications. Rail corridor siting may involve Class III railroad right-of-way negotiations with CSX or Norfolk Southern.

5. **NPS coordination** — New River Gorge National Park corridor engagement is confirmed. The extent to which NPS would participate as a data partner, co-owner, or licensing buyer has not been discussed in the context of sensor infrastructure.
