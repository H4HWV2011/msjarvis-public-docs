## Overview

In 2022–2023, a simple viral question swept TikTok and Instagram: women asked the men in their lives how often they think about the Roman Empire. The responses—millions of them, across age, class, geography, and culture—revealed that large numbers of men think about Rome regularly, often without being able to say why. The trend was followed by formal polling from Ipsos and YouGov, which confirmed a measurable gender gap in Roman imaginative frequency even in representative samples.

This case study treats that sequence—viral question, mass response, formal polling—as an **inadvertent commons probe**: an unplanned act of collective inquiry that made a previously invisible symbolic commons briefly legible. It demonstrates three things of direct relevance to Polymathmatic Geography:

1. How a meme can function as an **unanchored symbolic commons** with real gender-power geometry.
2. How polling firms applied **analytical-track semantics** to what had previously existed only as meaning-track circulation.
3. How **braided semantics** reveals the gap between what the phenomenon measures analytically (a small but real frequency difference) and what it means narratively (identity, belonging, political aesthetics, nostalgia for imperial order).

## Part 1: The Roman Empire as Unanchored Symbolic Commons

### 1.1 What a symbolic commons is

A commons, in Polymathmatic Geography, is an entangled space where material flows, legal rules, genealogies, and symbolic or spiritual orders are woven together and held—however imperfectly—in shared access. Most of the commons studied in this discipline are anchored: English common land has specific parishes, soils, and enclosure acts; Spanish huertas have named canals and time-slots; Quaker meeting commons have particular burial grounds and families.

A **symbolic commons** is a shared imaginative inheritance that circulates without formal governance, recorded ledger, or institutional enforcement. It is held in unwritten custom, felt familiarity, and aesthetic resonance rather than statute. Like grazing rights on an English common before enclosure, it operates primarily through what everyone already knows without having been told.

The Roman Empire, as collective imagination, is one of the largest and most durable symbolic commons in Western culture. Its vocabulary—senate and republic, legions and logistics, triumph and decline, law and empire, aqueducts and roads—has been circulating for over two millennia, reproduced through schools, films, games, political rhetoric, and the interior monologues of people who cannot quite say why they find it compelling.

### 1.2 The commons is unanchored

What makes the Roman imaginary a *polymathmatically problematic* commons is that for most participants it is **radically unanchored**. It floats free of:

- Specific hills, soils, or watersheds.
- Named wage histories or debt records.
- Particular funerals, clinics, or union halls.
- Identified enslaved populations who built the infrastructure.
- The Gauls, Britons, Dacians, or Welsh whose commons were "pacified" to make the empire legible.

In the Semantics chapter's terms, "Rome" for most men who think about it daily is meaning without a where. It functions as a **placeless universal**: a vocabulary of order, hierarchy, and civilizational arc that can be attached to almost any political project without accountability to any actual community or place.

This is precisely what makes it powerful as aesthetic and what makes it dangerous as politics. Like a maximopoly that biases the capital-flow landscape without declaring its preferences, the Roman imaginary biases the **political imagination landscape** without having to argue for itself. It makes certain arrangements of authority—centralized, martial, hierarchical, masculine—feel like gravity rather than choice.

### 1.3 The gender-power geometry

The meme exposed a specific power geometry embedded in the symbolic commons. The Roman imaginary is not equally available to all participants:

- It centers **masculine virtue** (*virtus*): the martial, stoic, duty-bound male body as the measure of political seriousness.
- It centers **conquest as civilization**: expansion read as progress, subjugation read as order.
- It centers **decline as tragedy**: the end of empire mourned rather than the liberation of the colonized celebrated.
- It marginalizes or erases: the enslaved, the colonized, women as political agents, and the commons-based societies that Rome absorbed or destroyed.

The fact that men reported thinking about Rome far more frequently than women—confirmed in both the informal meme responses and the formal YouGov and Ipsos polls—is not a biological fact about male brains. It is a **geometric fact about who the symbolic commons was built for and who it invites in**. The commons has a topology: some people are near its center, others are at its periphery or outside its boundary entirely.

Women asking the question were not simply curious. They were performing an act of **semantic authority**: naming something that had been circulating unnamed, forcing it into explicit language, and opening it to examination. That act is precisely what commons governance is supposed to enable.

## Part 2: The Polling Firms as Analytical-Track Instrument

### 2.1 The meme as meaning-track event

Before Ipsos and YouGov intervened, the Roman Empire phenomenon existed entirely on the **meaning track**:

- Women's surprise and amusement at men's answers: a recognition event, an "I had no idea" moment.
- Men's confessions: often accompanied by sheepishness, defensiveness, or genuine puzzlement at their own habit.
- Viral spread: the meme traveled because it resonated emotionally and socially, not because it reported a finding.
- No controls, no sampling frame, no representative population: pure self-selection and social contagion.

In Polymathmatic Geography's semantic framework, this is meaning-track semantics operating at scale. Two people are "near" in meaning-space if their stories resonate and they share forms of recognition—not because they were sampled from the same demographic bucket. The meme created an enormous, chaotic meaning-track dataset: millions of informal interviews conducted across relationships and cultures, generating a rough but real signal about imaginative habits.

### 2.2 The polling firms' analytical intervention

When Ipsos and YouGov formalized the question, they applied **analytical-track semantics** to the phenomenon:

- Representative sampling frames replaced self-selection.
- Standardized frequency categories ("daily," "weekly," "monthly," "rarely," "never") replaced open-ended confessions.
- Demographic breakdowns (gender, age, education, geography) replaced the undifferentiated viral stream.
- Statistical significance testing replaced viral resonance as the criterion for a "real" finding.

The findings in the analytical track were notably **modest**: a small minority of men think about Rome daily (around 4% in some polls), though the gender gap is real and consistent across surveys. The extreme claims of the meme—"every day, constantly"—were exaggerated. The analytical track did its job: it calibrated the signal, removed self-selection inflation, and produced a testable, comparable result.

### 2.3 What the analytical track cannot see

But the analytical track, taken alone, would suggest the phenomenon is minor: a few percentage points of gender difference in an obscure imaginative habit. It cannot capture:

- **Why** men who do think about Rome find it significant enough to mention unprompted.
- **What work** the Roman imaginary is doing in their political aesthetics and sense of identity.
- **Which version** of Rome each person carries: the senate, the legions, the fall, the law, the spectacle.
- **How the commons is governed**: who set the curriculum, which films got made, which games were designed, which political speeches invoked Roman precedent and to what effect.

These are meaning-track questions, and they are not answered by knowing the frequency.

## Part 3: Braided Semantics and What the Gap Reveals

### 3.1 The braided picture

Polymathmatic Geography's central semantic move is **braiding**: holding the meaning track and the analytical track simultaneously and treating the gap between them as a finding rather than a contradiction to resolve.

For the Roman Empire phenomenon, the braided picture looks like this:

| Dimension | Meaning-track reading | Analytical-track reading |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency | "Men think about it constantly" | ~4% daily; measurable gender gap |
| Significance | Identity, belonging, civilizational aesthetics | Small but real demographic difference |
| Governance | Unwritten, felt, circulated through culture | No formal institution; no ledger |
| Who participates | Primarily men; women as observers/questioners | Gender gap confirmed in representative samples |
| What is named | An invisible habit made visible by a question | A frequency distribution across demographic categories |
| What is missing | Anchors: whose Rome, which bodies, which commons destroyed | Meaning: why it matters, what work it does |

The gap between the two columns is itself the finding. The phenomenon is **semantically large and analytically small**: it matters enormously in meaning-space (it shapes political aesthetics, masculine identity, and the vocabulary of order for millions of people) while measuring modestly in analytical-space (a few percentage points of frequency difference). A system that only used the analytical track would conclude the meme was noise. A system that only used the meaning track would have no way to compare or test. Braided semantics holds both and asks: what does it mean that something analytically modest is semantically enormous?

### 3.2 What braiding reveals about power

The answer, in this case, is that **the symbolic commons is doing its most significant work below the threshold of explicit frequency**. You do not have to think about Rome every day for the Roman imaginary to shape your political vocabulary. The commons operates through aesthetic background, not conscious rumination. Its power is precisely its invisibility—the fact that it does not present itself as an argument to be accepted or rejected, but as an atmosphere to be breathed.

This is why the meme was surprising. The question made explicit what had been implicit. It converted background aesthetic into foreground admission. And the admission revealed the geometry: the commons was built for some people more than others, it invites some imaginations toward certain political aesthetics, and it had been doing this work unobserved.

### 3.3 The meme as methodology: what commons research can learn

The sequence—viral question → mass informal response → formal analytical follow-up—is a compressed version of what Polymathmatic Geography recommends for any commons investigation:

1. **Name the unnamed.** The women who asked the question did what commons councils and feedback loops are designed to do: they surfaced an invisible commons and asked for an account of it.
2. **Collect the meaning-track signal first.** The meme's chaotic, self-selected responses were noisy but real: they established that something was there before formal methods were applied.
3. **Apply analytical instruments to calibrate, not replace.** The Ipsos and YouGov polls did not invalidate the meme; they calibrated it. They told us the phenomenon was real, modestly sized, and demographically structured.
4. **Braid the tracks to find the gap.** The most important finding is not the frequency percentage but the disproportion between analytical size and semantic significance—and what that disproportion tells us about how symbolic commons operate below the threshold of measurement.
5. **Ask the anchor question.** Whose Rome? Which bodies? Which commons was enclosed to make the empire legible? Anchoring the imaginary to specific histories—enslaved aqueduct-builders, Welsh hillforts turned Roman nodes, Gallic commons "pacified"—does not destroy the commons but makes it accountable.

### 3.4 Application to Ms. Jarvis and MountainShares

The Roman Empire case is not merely historical illustration. It raises a direct design question for the Appalachian stack:

**Every commons develops an invisible symbolic layer.** MountainShares, The Commons, and Ms. Jarvis will each accumulate imaginative inheritances: vocabularies of what "serious" governance looks like, aesthetics of who belongs, background assumptions about what counts as a contribution or a harm. Those layers will circulate without formal ledger, shaping participation and exclusion in ways that analytics alone will not detect.

The Roman Empire case suggests three design commitments:

- **Build in periodic naming rituals**: structured moments when participants are invited to name what has been circulating as background assumption—the equivalent of women asking "how often do you think about Rome?"
- **Use braided dashboards**: instruments that show both analytical metrics (participation rates, resource flows, demographic distributions) and meaning-track signals (testimonies, narratives of belonging and exclusion, spiritual and genealogical resonances).
- **Require anchor questions in governance**: when symbolic vocabularies are invoked in proposals, decisions, or narratives—"this is how serious communities do things," "this is what real governance looks like"—the governance process should ask: anchored where? serving whose lineage? erasing which commons?

These are not bureaucratic additions. They are the difference between a commons that reproduces invisible power geometries and one that can see and contest its own symbolic layer.
