# Entangled Space after Kant, Durkheim, and Weber: Toward a Polymathmatic Geography

### Abstract

This paper revisits classical debates on the nature of spatial categories through a contemporary experiment in rural infrastructural design in Central Appalachia. Where Kant treated space as an a priori form of intuition, Durkheim re-grounded categories like space and time in social life, arguing that they arise from "social morphology" and collective rhythms (Durkheim 1995; Jones 2020; Miller 2003). Weber extended this tradition by asking not only how categories are socially produced, but how they become legitimate — how some actors acquire the recognised authority to define, enforce, and redesign the categories through which space is governed (Weber 1978; Swedberg 2005). Building on all three while taking seriously radical and infrastructural geographies' insistence on materiality, labour, and power (Harvey 1973; Massey 2005; Graham and McFarlane 2014; Ernste 2018), the paper proposes entangled space as the core object of a polymathmatic geography — a discipline that integrates quantitative geography, systems theory, AI architectures, theology, and indigenous knowledge around shared spatial problems. Entangled space names those situations where land, law, computation, economy, story, and spirit are braided into a single geometry that can be made visible, contested, and instrumented.

Drawing on the Quantarithmia framework and its instruments — Ms Egeria Allis, a glassbox AI steward system, and MountainShares, a closed-loop community credit — the paper argues that categories of space must now be understood as co-produced by commons institutions, infrastructures, and AI systems (Anonymous 2025a, 2025b). Who holds the authority to define those categories — and on what grounds that authority is recognised as legitimate — is the Weber question that organises the empirical design. Entangled space after Kant, Durkheim, and Weber thus becomes not only a philosophical claim, but also a conceptual, methodological, and empirical program for operational design in communities situated in contemporary regimes of extraction.

## 1. Introduction: Why Categories of Space Still Matter

Critical and radical geography have long shown that how we conceptualise "society–space" shapes what we see as politically possible (Harvey 1973; Massey 2005; Soja 2010; Elden 2007). From early critiques of spatial science to more recent calls for "spatial justice," authors such as Harvey, Massey, and Soja have framed space as produced through social relations, capital flows, and state projects rather than given as a neutral backdrop (Harvey 1973; Massey 2005; Soja 2010; Hubbard et al. 2002). STS-inflected infrastructural geographies have extended this insight to pipes, data centres, roads, and platforms, insisting that infrastructures are not merely backdrops but constitutive media of social relations (Star 1999; Larkin 2013; Graham and McFarlane 2014; Allen and Beecher 2023).

Yet despite these advances, much contemporary work still presupposes that "space" is a background container on or within which infrastructures and social practices operate. Even when we say that space is produced, the categories of space — hazard zone, market, customer, citizen — often appear as pre-given tools rather than as objects of political struggle and legitimation in their own right.

This paper argues that returning to a very old set of questions — what is space, how are its categories produced, and who is recognised as having the authority to define them — matters urgently for contemporary radical and infrastructural geography. It revisits three interlocking debates: Kant's claim that space and time are a priori forms of intuition (Kant 1998); Durkheim's counterclaim that categories such as space and time are collective, social products rooted in social morphology (Durkheim 1995; Jones 2020; Miller 2003); and Weber's subsequent analysis of how socially produced categories become authoritative — how domination (*Herrschaft*) is legitimated through rational-legal, traditional, or charismatic grounds, and how the rationalisation of modern institutions progressively encodes authority in rules, schemas, and bureaucratic procedures (Weber 1978; Swedberg 2005; Kalberg 1980).

Re-examining this triad in the context of contemporary platforms, AI systems, and experimental commons is not an antiquarian exercise. When global logistics firms, cloud providers, and financial giants redraw the spatial conditions of everyday life, they are simultaneously rewriting the categories through which communities perceive and inhabit their geographies and claiming the authority to do so as technically rational, neutral, and therefore legitimate (Harvey 1989; Graham and McFarlane 2014). If those categories are treated as pre-given (Kant), they fall outside political contestation. If, with Durkheim, they are treated as social in origin, then the struggle over who produces them is opened. And if, with Weber, we ask on what grounds authority to produce and enforce spatial categories is recognised as legitimate, then platform design, AI architecture, and commons governance become sites of a specifically Weberian politics — a contest over the rationalisation of space itself.

The paper introduces entangled space and the corresponding disciplinary proposal, polymathmatic geography, as a way of making this contest explicit. Entangled space names places where land, law, computation, economy, story, and spirit are inextricably braided into a single geometry — not only philosophically, but operationally, in data models and institutional rules. Polymathmatic geography proposes to study and reshape these geometries using instruments that are themselves spatial: a glassbox AI steward (Ms Egeria Allis) and a closed-loop community credit system (MountainShares) (Anonymous 2025a, 2025b). The argument is developed through a case situated in Central Appalachia, but claims broader relevance for radical and infrastructural geographies confronting platform capitalism.

### 1.1 Situating the Argument in Existing Literatures

This intervention sits at the intersection of several strands of work.

First, in critical and radical geography, Harvey's analyses of uneven development, Massey's relational conceptions of space, and Soja's theorisation of spatial justice have challenged aspatial social theory and positivist spatial science alike (Harvey 1973; Massey 2005; Soja 2010; Hubbard et al. 2002). The present paper extends this tradition by focusing explicitly on categories of space and how they are now encoded in digital infrastructures — and by adding a Weberian question about who holds authorised capacity to define those categories.

Second, in infrastructural geography and STS, recent reviews highlight an "infrastructural turn" that foregrounds labour, maintenance, and repair as central to spatial politics (Allen and Beecher 2023; Graham and McFarlane 2014). Classic STS work on classification and standards has likewise shown how seemingly technical schemas enact social orders (Star 1999; Bowker and Star 1999). Here, the focus is on how such schemas operate simultaneously as Durkheimian categories and as Weberian claims to rational-legal authority within AI systems and payment networks.

Third, in classical social theory, Durkheim's account of categories as social offers a powerful alternative to Kantian a priorism (Durkheim 1995; Jones 2020; Miller 2003). Weber's theory of rationalisation and legitimate domination extends that insight: it explains not only how categories are socially produced, but how they stabilise as authoritative, taken-for-granted schemas — and how that stabilisation can be contested and redesigned (Weber 1978; Kalberg 1980; Swedberg 2005). That combined insight is taken seriously in a contemporary infrastructural context and extended through the concept of entangled space.

### 1.2 Empirical Materials and Approach

Empirically, the paper draws on design documents, implementation notes, and early-stage testing for the Quantarithmia framework and its instruments, as well as ongoing field-anchored inquiry in Central Appalachia, including walking interviews, community workshops, and informal co-design sessions (Anonymous 2025a, 2025b). A core part of this empirical record is a documented case of real-time design correction: a detection architecture within the MountainShares safety layer was found, during Stage 0 construction, to systematically false-flag intensive authorised family caregiving arrangements under the PALCO self-directed care programme as potentially coercive. The flaw was identified by the researcher recognising her own situation inside the system she was building — an authorised PALCO caregiver whose every behavioural signal matched the detection criteria precisely. The correction, documented in full in accompanying materials (Anonymous 2025c), required reframing the detection layer from a risk-score mechanism to a community-relational query: not "is this arrangement suspicious?" but "does this household have sufficient independent contact density within The Commons?" This case is cited throughout the paper as a worked example of the core methodological and Weberian claim: that legitimate authority over spatial categories must be grounded in the lived conditions of those most affected, not derived solely from technical rationality applied from outside.

## 2. From Kant to Durkheim to Weber: Space, Categories, and Legitimate Authority

### 2.1 Kant: Space as Pre-Given Form

Kant famously treated space and time as a priori forms of sensibility: not derived from experience, but conditions that make experience possible (Kant 1998). On this view, spatiality is hard-wired into the structure of cognition; while the content of experience may vary, the form of spatial organisation does not. This move allowed Kant to secure the universality of geometry and the objectivity of science, but at the cost of placing the most fundamental categories outside historical, social, and political life.

### 2.2 Durkheim: Space as Social Category

Durkheim's intervention in The Elementary Forms of Religious Life is a direct challenge to this position. He argued that categories such as space, time, causality, and class are social in origin: general, stable, impersonal representations generated by the "coexistence and interaction of individuals within a social framework" (Durkheim 1995; Miller 2003). Durkheim's account of space draws explicitly on "social morphology" — the layout of groups, the distribution of settlements, the ways in which land is divided and classified (Durkheim 1995; Jones 2020). Space, he suggests, is "divided, differentiated, arranged" according to social needs, and these divisions become the very categories through which individuals perceive and think.

Recent reassessments of Durkheim's treatment of space have underscored how far-reaching this claim is. For Durkheim, space is not an abstract container but "the collectively produced foundation for all social activity," such that physical space is inherently shaped by social practices of classification and division (Jones 2020; Flores 2023). The cadastral map, the zoning code, and the eligibility boundary occupy the same logical level as the clan's sacred and profane territories: all are collective, institutional artefacts that stabilise categories (Durkheim 1995; Jones 2020).

### 2.3 Weber: Legitimacy and the Rationalisation of Spatial Authority

Where Durkheim explains how categories are produced, Weber explains how they become authoritative — how some actors acquire the recognised right to define and enforce the categories through which life is organised. Weber's typology of legitimate domination identifies three grounds on which authority can be claimed and accepted: traditional authority, rooted in custom and inherited status; charismatic authority, rooted in personal qualities recognised as extraordinary; and rational-legal authority, rooted in formally enacted rules applied impersonally and universally (Weber 1978; Swedberg 2005).

In modernity, Weber argued, rational-legal authority progressively displaces the other two through a process he called rationalisation: the systematic replacement of custom, spirit, and personal judgment with codified rules, calculable procedures, and bureaucratic schemas (Weber 1978; Kalberg 1980). This process produces what Weber famously described as an "iron cage" — a rationalised social order so thoroughly encoded in institutions and rules that it becomes experienced as an objective constraint rather than a social choice.

Applied to categories of space, the Weberian insight is this: when a logistics platform defines a "delivery zone," when a federal benefits system defines an "eligible address," or when an AI system defines a "hazard flag," it is not only producing a spatial category in Durkheim's sense — it is claiming rational-legal authority to enforce that category as binding. The power here is not brute coercion (*Macht* — raw power imposed regardless of resistance) but legitimated domination (*Herrschaft* — authority that is recognised, at least provisionally, as valid) (Weber 1978; Swedberg 2005). The category sticks not because someone is holding a weapon, but because the schema appears technically neutral, formally correct, and procedurally enacted — the hallmarks of rational-legal legitimacy.

This is the specific Weberian danger that radical geography and STS have not yet fully named: platform capitalism does not only extract value and restructure space. It claims rational-legal authority over the categories through which space is defined, measured, and acted upon. Its schemas — delivery zones, credit scores, eligibility rules, risk flags — appear as technical determinations rather than political choices, and therefore as outside the domain of contestation. The iron cage of rationalised space is one in which the most consequential decisions about who belongs where, who is creditworthy, who is at risk, and who is a legitimate actor are made by opaque systems whose authority is taken for granted precisely because it is encoded in code and procedure.

### 2.4 The Combined Stakes

Bringing Kant, Durkheim, and Weber together yields a compound claim that organises the rest of this paper:

- Spatial categories are not pre-given (against Kant).
- They are socially produced through collective life and institutional arrangements (with Durkheim).
- They become authoritative — and resistant to contestation — through processes of legitimation, and specifically through the progressive rationalisation of their production into technical schemas and bureaucratic procedures (with Weber).
- Therefore, contesting spatial categories requires not only showing that they are socially produced, but building alternative legitimate authority — alternative rational-legal grounds on which different categories can be proposed, enforced, and recognised as valid.

This last step is what neither critical geography nor STS has fully addressed. Showing that a hazard map is wrong, or that an eligibility boundary is unjust, does not by itself produce a counter-authority. Someone must build the alternative schema, ground it in evidence and community accountability, and make it legible enough to compete with the dominant rationalisation. That is the specific task that polymathmatic geography and the Quantarithmia framework undertake.

## 3. Entangled Space: Extending the Triad in an Infrastructural Age

### 3.1 From Social Morphology to Infrastructural Morphology

If Durkheim could already see that categories of space are produced by the spatial distribution of social groups, and if Weber could already see that those categories stabilise through processes of rational-legal legitimation, it is reasonable to ask what happens when social morphology is increasingly governed by logistics platforms, cloud infrastructures, and financial indices — and when rational-legal authority over spatial categories is increasingly held by a small number of global corporations and state-platform assemblages.

The term infrastructural morphology is proposed for this contemporary situation. Where Durkheim took the distribution of clans and villages as his empirical starting point, infrastructural morphology takes as its starting point the distribution of fibre-optic cables, fulfilment centres, payment rails, zoning regulations, hazard maps, and data centres. These are the media through which late-capitalist spatial categories are imposed and reproduced — defining who is "near" or "far" from banking, broadband, healthcare, and decision-making in ways that are no less categorical than the clan divisions Durkheim analysed, and no less authoritative than the rational-legal schemas Weber described.

The Weberian dimension of infrastructural morphology is especially important. Platforms do not merely reorganise space; they claim authority over its categories through the appearance of technical neutrality. An algorithm that determines creditworthiness, a route-optimisation system that designates "underserved areas," or an AI model that flags "risk households" all present themselves as applications of rational procedure to objective data. Their schemas are experienced as determinations, not choices. This is rationalised spatial authority at its most effective: the iron cage becomes invisible because it looks like a map.

### 3.2 Defining Entangled Space

Entangled space is proposed as the core object of polymathmatic geography: any "where" in which material flows, legal rules, genealogies, infrastructures, and symbolic orders are inextricably braided. An entangled space is characterised by:

- **Layered materiality** — land, water, buildings, and bodies interlaced with pipes, cables, and sensors.
- **Legal-institutional architectures** — property regimes, zoning codes, benefit rules, and corporate charters.
- **Computational infrastructures** — platforms, payment networks, AI systems, and databases that register and act upon the territory.
- **Narrative and theological orders** — stories of harm and hope, doctrines of justice, and spiritual geographies.
- **Commons and enclosures** — historical and current regimes governing shared resources, from forest commons to community-run broadband.

In entangled space, there is no meaningful sense in which "the digital" is a backdrop to "the physical." Digital and financial infrastructures partially *are* the spatial categories through which residents' lives are organised. Eligibility boundaries in a benefits system, coverage maps for broadband, and delivery zones in a logistics app are all examples of categories of space in Durkheim's sense — stable, impersonal, collectively enforced representations that shape practice — and examples of rational-legal authority in Weber's sense: schemas that are experienced as binding because they are encoded in procedures that appear technically neutral.

The Weberian contribution to the concept of entangled space is therefore this: entanglement is not only a description of complexity. It is a description of overlapping authority claims. In any given entangled space, multiple actors — federal agencies, platforms, commons institutions, congregations, families — are simultaneously asserting rational-legal, traditional, or normative authority over the categories that govern that space. The politics of entangled space is a politics of legitimation: whose schema counts, and on what grounds.

## 4. Polymathmatic Geography: A Discipline for Entangled Space

Polymathmatic geography is an emerging discipline that takes entangled space as its primary object and builds instruments to investigate and reshape it. It is "polymathmatic" in two senses. First, it insists on integrating multiple knowledge traditions — quantitative geography, systems theory, AI research, theology, indigenous knowledge — without collapsing them into a single idiom (Anonymous 2025a). Second, it treats mathematical formalisms as one strand in a broader braid that also includes oral histories, legal analysis, and spiritual testimony.

At the methodological level, polymathmatic geography:

- Adopts field-anchored inquiry, treating towns and hollers as primary texts through walking, listening, and co-mapping with residents.
- Uses commons theory and design principles as a key lens, in conversation with Ostrom and her successors (Ostrom 1990; Nordman 2021; Foster and Iaione 2017).
- Engages critical and radical geography by foregrounding enclosure, racialised inequality, and environmental harm as central spatial processes (Harvey 1973; Massey 2005; Soja 2010).
- Incorporates STS-style infrastructural analysis, treating platforms, ledgers, and AI models as objects of ethnographic and technical scrutiny (Star 1999; Larkin 2013; Graham and McFarlane 2014).
- Takes Weber's question of legitimation as a design criterion: any instrument built under this framework must establish the grounds on which its authority over spatial categories is recognised as valid, and must make those grounds contestable rather than concealed.

This last point distinguishes polymathmatic geography from both neutral technical design and from critique without construction. It is not enough to show that existing schemas are unjust (Durkheimian critique). It is not enough to propose alternative schemas without establishing their authority (Kantian design). It is necessary to build instruments whose authority is grounded in community accountability, transparent evidence, and contestable procedure — Weberian rational-legal legitimacy from below rather than from the platform or the state.

In this sense, the GeoBelief Information Model and MountainShares are not merely technical artefacts. They are proposals for a different rational-legal order of spatial categories — one whose authority derives from the communities most affected rather than from corporate or state schemas applied from outside. This is the specific contribution of polymathmatic geography to the Kant-Durkheim-Weber triad: it attempts to instantiate, in code and institution, the alternative legitimate authority that the triad implies but that critical geography and STS have not yet fully built.

## 5. Case: Quantarithmia in Appalachia

If entangled space is to be more than a metaphor, and if Weberian legitimation is to be more than a critical diagnosis, both must be shown in a concrete setting. Central Appalachia offers a paradigmatic case: a region marked by centuries of resource extraction, company-town logics, deindustrialisation, opioid epidemics, and uneven infrastructural investment (Anonymous 2025a). The "social morphology" Durkheim would have recognised — coal camps, hollers, courthouse towns — is now overlaid by the infrastructural morphology of logistics platforms, fragmented broadband, federal benefits systems, and distant financial indices, each carrying its own rational-legal claim to define what space is, who belongs where, and what constitutes legitimate participation.

The iron cage in this context is specific and visible. Maximopolies — financial giants such as large asset managers — shape which sectors and regions receive capital through index design and portfolio allocation, exercising what Weber would recognise as rational-legal authority at planetary scale: their schemas (indices, ratings, risk models) are binding on regional economies not through coercion but through the legitimate authority of financial procedure (Anonymous 2025a). Megaopolies — logistics and platform giants — reorganise everyday space through fulfilment centres, algorithmic feeds, and data extraction, encoding spatial categories (delivery zones, recommended content, relevant searches) that are experienced as neutral determinations rather than power choices (Anonymous 2025a). And the U.S. state-platform surveillance assemblage — integrating federal data with commercial camera networks, benefits systems, and predictive analytics — lays a monitoring grid over populations that claims rational-legal authority through bureaucratic procedure, making residents legible from above in categories they did not define and cannot easily contest (Anonymous 2025a).

The Quantarithmia framework treats Appalachia as a tacet: a founding key in which general principles of extraction, commons-building, and infrastructural design are stress-tested, and in which the contest over legitimate spatial authority is most sharply visible (Anonymous 2025a). Rather than analysing Appalachia solely as a site of deprivation, Quantarithmia treats it as a frontier where the rationalisation of space — in Weber's precise sense — is being decided in real time, and where alternative rational-legal authority might be grounded in communities rather than platforms.

## 6. Ms Allis, GeoBelief, and the Legitimation of Categories in Code

### 6.1 Ms Egeria Allis as a Counter-Rational-Legal Instrument

Ms Egeria Allis is a glassbox AI steward system designed as a territorial "brain" for rural governance (Anonymous 2025a, 2025b). The Weberian framing clarifies what "glassbox" means here: it is not merely a technical transparency requirement. It is a legitimation strategy. Rational-legal authority, in Weber's account, derives its validity from the formal correctness and impartial application of its procedures (Weber 1978; Swedberg 2005). A system whose reasoning is opaque cannot claim rational-legal legitimacy from below, because no community can verify that its procedures are being applied correctly or that its schemas are grounded in local evidence rather than imported assumptions.

By contrast, Ms Allis's architecture is designed so that every claim can be traced to a specific geometry, a specific time, and a specific evidential stack. This is not only a technical feature; it is the design of a new rational-legal order for spatial claims — one in which authority derives from inspectable, contestable, community-anchored procedure rather than from the opacity of a corporate model.

Rather than treating AI as a placeless optimisation service, Quantarithmia anchors Ms Allis in a specific spatial body (a PostGIS-backed database of West Virginia) and a specific linguistic tacet (Appalachian English, via the Audio-Aligned and Parsed Corpus of Appalachian English, AAPCAppE; Childs 2003; Childs et al. 2024). In Weberian terms, the linguistic anchoring is itself a legitimation move: it refuses the claim that standard English is the neutral medium of rational procedure, and instead grounds the authority of the system's categories in the speech community most directly affected by them.

### 6.2 GeoBelief: Operationalising Legitimate Spatial Authority

The GeoBelief Information Model (GBIM) is the core site where categories are recast as data structures with explicit legitimation conditions. Every belief held by Ms Allis is anchored in a triad: where (geometry), when (temporal metadata), and why (structured evidence). Instead of treating "location" as an incidental attribute, GeoBelief enforces that every claim be tied to a specific geometry and a stack of proofs — documents, testimonies, sensor readings (Anonymous 2025a).

From a Weberian perspective, this design is significant because it makes the rational-legal authority of every spatial claim auditable. A "hazard zone" defined by federal flood maps carries one evidential stack; a "hazard zone" expanded by local memory of landslides and informal drainage carries a different and potentially richer one. GeoBelief does not adjudicate between them automatically — it makes both visible and contestable, establishing the conditions under which a community can challenge a federal determination with locally grounded evidence and have that challenge taken seriously as a legitimate counter-claim.

This is precisely what the iron cage forecloses: not the ability to complain about spatial categories, but the institutional infrastructure through which alternative categories can be proposed, evidenced, and recognised as binding. GeoBelief is, in this sense, a machine for producing Weberian rational-legal counter-authority at the scale of specific geographies.

### 6.3 The PALCO Case: Legitimation Failure and Correction

The legitimation logic of GeoBelief is illustrated concretely by the detection architecture case documented in Anonymous 2025c. A pattern-based safety layer within MountainShares was designed to surface potentially coercive PALCO caregiving arrangements through a signal set — maximum authorised hours, single-source attestation, beneficiary withdrawal from external platforms — that, as the researcher recognised, described her own authorised caregiving arrangement with precision.

The Weberian reading of this flaw is direct. The detection logic claimed rational-legal authority — its schema appeared to be a neutral application of procedure to data. But its neutrality was false: the schema had been built from a view external to the lived conditions it purported to classify. Applied without community-anchored context, it would have exercised authoritative power over the people it claimed to protect, in exactly the manner Weber described as the pathology of rationalisation: procedure that is formally correct but substantively unjust because it cannot see what it cannot measure.

The correction — reframing the detection layer as a community-relational query rather than a risk score — restored the legitimation condition GeoBelief requires: context as primary signal, community knowledge as the ground of authority, and inspectable procedure as the basis for any consequential classification (Anonymous 2025c). The flaw being caught and corrected during Stage 0, before any resident was affected, is not incidental. It is the result of a researcher embedded in the Tacet — a researcher for whom the design is not abstract but personal — exercising exactly the kind of field-anchored authority that the framework requires of all its instruments.

### 6.4 Language, Dialect, and the Sociality of Legitimate Knowledge

Durkheim's interest in the social origins of categories extends to language, and Weber's interest in legitimation extends to whose speech is recognised as authoritative. The use of AAPCAppE as a linguistic tacet addresses both simultaneously. By training Ms Allis to respect Appalachian English morphosyntax (for example, a-prefixing, double modals) as normative rather than erroneous, the system encodes a claim about legitimate knowledge production: the people most affected by spatial categories have the right to name and describe their world in their own tongue, and that speech carries epistemic and political authority that a system calibrated only to standard English would systematically deny (Childs 2003; Childs et al. 2024).

In Weber's terms, a system that treats local dialect as defective is claiming charismatic or traditional authority for standard English — the implicit authority of the educated, the credentialed, the distant. AAPCAppE is a counter-move: it grounds the system's linguistic authority in the community itself, establishing a rational-legal basis for Appalachian speech as a legitimate source of spatial category definition.

## 7. MountainShares and the Legitimation of Economic Space

### 7.1 Economic Space as a Contest of Legitimate Authority

If categories of space are both socially produced and legitimated through rational-legal claims, then economic categories — market, credit, liquidity, work, asset — are also spatial authority claims. The dominant payment and credit infrastructure claims rational-legal authority over these categories: what counts as income, what counts as an asset, what counts as creditworthy, what counts as work are all determinations made by systems whose authority appears procedurally correct and technically neutral (Weber 1978; Harvey 1989).

MountainShares (M$) treats the local economy as an entangled geometry and proposes alternative rational-legal categories with explicit legitimation grounds (Anonymous 2025a). Rather than accepting the categories of the dominant infrastructure, it constructs a closed-loop economy with different definitional authority: Purchased MountainShares (PMS) function as prepaid local credits; Earned MountainShares (EMS) recognise caregiving, mutual aid, and learning as legitimate economic contributions. The distinction is a direct challenge to the dominant schema's definition of work — and it is grounded not in ideological assertion but in a DAO constitution that makes the alternative definition formally enacted, procedurally governed, and therefore rational-legally authoritative on its own terms.

### 7.2 Reserve Ratios, Reciprocity Thresholds, and the Rationalisation of Justice

MountainShares' internal design explicitly encodes Weberian and Durkheimian concerns in formal rules. A reserve ratio — liquid backing assets divided by outstanding spendable M$ — determines which operational band the system is in and triggers automatic adjustments. Phase transitions (Foundation, Growth, Premium Reserve) are governed by explicit thresholds rather than discretionary decision. Benefits-sensitive accounts are defined by formal rules that limit balances and earning rates to protect participants from adverse benefit determinations (Anonymous 2025a).

Each of these mechanisms is an instance of Weberian rational-legal design: the legitimacy of MountainShares' authority over local economic categories derives from the formal correctness and community-accountability of its procedures, not from the personal authority of its founders or the traditional authority of existing institutions. The DAO constitution is the explicit statement of this authority: it is the document that makes the system's power over categories formally enacted and therefore, in Weber's terms, legitimately binding on its participants.

Yet the system also embeds a substantive, Durkheimian check on procedural rationality. The reciprocity threshold — borrowed from Quantarithmia's analysis of social stability — enforces a norm that formal procedure alone cannot: if flows of value and recognition fall below the level required for community reproduction, the system signals a moral failure, not only a technical one (Anonymous 2025a). In this sense, MountainShares deliberately holds Weber's rational-legal form in tension with Durkheim's substantive community obligation — a tension that the framework treats not as a contradiction to be resolved but as a productive design constraint.

### 7.3 Benefits-Sensitive Space: The Iron Cage as Entangled Geometry

Means-tested welfare regimes are among the most visible examples of Weberian rationalisation applied to spatial categories. They carve economic space into classifications — eligible, over-income, asset-limited — that appear as technical determinations but carry enormous power over how people can participate in economic life. MountainShares' benefits-sensitive account design acknowledges that participants live inside multiple overlapping rational-legal schemas simultaneously: the DAO's rules, the state's benefit rules, and federal programme requirements all claim authority over the same person's economic space at the same time (Anonymous 2025a).

Rather than pretending that the DAO schema supersedes the state schema — a claim it cannot legitimate — MountainShares maps the constraints as part of the entangled geometry and designs its procedures to navigate within them. This is a specifically Weberian response to the iron cage: not a frontal assault on state rational-legal authority, which would merely expose participants to risk, but a careful construction of an additional legitimate authority that coexists with, navigates, and over time potentially reforms the dominant schema.

## 8. Implications for Radical Geography and STS

### 8.1 From Critique to Counter-Authority

Radical geography has long argued that space is produced through social relations, capital flows, and state projects (Harvey 1973; Massey 2005; Soja 2010). STS has likewise shown that infrastructures are sites of power, labour, and politics (Star 1999; Larkin 2013; Graham and McFarlane 2014). The Weber-Durkheim extension proposed here pushes this insight to a further level: showing that spatial categories are unjust is necessary but not sufficient. The next task is building legitimate counter-authority — alternative rational-legal grounds on which different categories can be proposed, evidenced, and enforced.

This is what the PALCO correction demonstrates most clearly. The flaw in the detection architecture was not merely technical; it was a failure of legitimate authority — a schema claiming to protect caregivers that had been built without the lived knowledge required to do so. The correction did not only fix a bug; it established the conditions under which the system's authority over that category could be recognised as legitimate: community-anchored context, inspectable procedure, and the designer's own accountability to the conditions being classified. Designing and governing categories should therefore be treated as a core site of radical practice alongside more familiar terrains of movement-building, protest, and policy advocacy (Harvey 1989; Antipode 2024).

### 8.2 Re-situating Classical Theory

Re-reading Kant, Durkheim, and Weber in the light of entangled space suggests a different genealogy for radical geography. Kant names the danger of treating spatial categories as pre-given — and therefore outside politics. Durkheim names the opportunity: categories are social, and therefore changeable. Weber names the obstacle: categories become authoritative through rationalisation, and changing them requires not only showing their social character but building alternative legitimate authority capable of competing with the rational-legal claims of dominant institutions.

Polymathmatic geography's insistence on glassbox design, community-anchored evidence, contestable procedure, and linguistic legitimation can be read as an attempt to instantiate Weberian counter-authority in specific instruments — not as a general theory of resistance, but as a concrete programme for building the alternative rational-legal order that entangled space requires.

### 8.3 Directions for Collaborative Work

For radical geography and STS, the Quantarithmia case suggests at least three lines of collaboration:

- **Empirical co-design.** Working with communities to define categories in GeoBelief or similar systems — combining ethnography, legal analysis, and technical implementation to produce spatial schemas whose authority derives from documented community knowledge.
- **Comparative legitimation studies.** Placing Ms Allis and MountainShares alongside other experimental infrastructures (urban data trusts, municipal broadband, cooperative platforms) to explore different strategies for establishing counter rational-legal authority over spatial categories.
- **Critical genealogy of rationalisation.** Extending the Kant-Durkheim-Weber line to include Marx, Ostrom, and contemporary STS, tracing how the rationalisation of spatial categories has historically proceeded and how it might be redirected toward commons and spatial justice (Ostrom 1990; Foster and Iaione 2017; Larkin 2013).

## 9. Conclusion: Contributions and an Invitation

The argument of this paper has been threefold. Conceptually, it proposes entangled space and infrastructural morphology as extensions of Durkheim's social morphology and Weber's rationalisation into a world saturated with computational and financial infrastructures. The addition of Weber to the Durkheim-Kant pairing is the paper's central theoretical move: it shifts the question from how spatial categories are produced to how they become authoritative, and therefore to how alternative authority can be built. Methodologically, it introduces polymathmatic geography and its instruments — GeoBelief, Ms Allis, and MountainShares — as examples of category design that take rational-legal legitimation from below seriously, grounding authority in community accountability, transparent evidence, and contestable procedure rather than in corporate or state schemas. Empirically, it offers Quantarithmia in Central Appalachia — including the documented correction of the PALCO detection architecture — as a case in which categories of space and economic life are being re-legitimated in code and institutional practice, with the researcher's own positionality inside the Tacet as the primary evidential ground.

For radical geography and STS, the invitation is straightforward and practical: to treat the legitimation of spatial categories as a live object of collaborative work, and to see instruments like Ms Allis and MountainShares as shared experimental sites rather than proprietary solutions. The iron cage of rationalised space is real — but it was built by people, using specific schemas, on specific grounds of authority. It can be rebuilt differently. Entangled space after Kant, Durkheim, and Weber is not only a diagnosis of our moment. It is an open call for a polymathmatic geography capable of building the legitimate counter-authority that communities in extraction economies need — and that the discipline has, until now, largely theorised without constructing.
