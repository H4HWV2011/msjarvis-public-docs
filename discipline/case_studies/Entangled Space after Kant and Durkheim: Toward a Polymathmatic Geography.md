## 1. Introduction: Why Categories of Space Still Matter

Critical and radical geography have long shown that how we conceptualise
society-space shapes what we see as politically possible. From early critiques of
spatial science to more recent calls for spatial justice, authors such as Harvey,
Massey, and Soja have framed space as produced through social relations, capital
flows, and state projects rather than given as a neutral backdrop. STS-inflected
infrastructural geographies have extended this insight to pipes, data centres,
roads, and platforms, insisting that infrastructures are not merely backdrops but
constitutive media of social relations.

Yet despite these advances, much contemporary work still presupposes that space is
a background container on or within which infrastructures and social practices
operate. Even when we say that space is produced, the categories of space — hazard
zone, market, customer, citizen — often appear as pre-given tools rather than as
objects of political struggle and legitimation in their own right.

This paper argues that returning to a very old set of questions — what is space,
how are its categories produced, and who is recognised as having the authority to
define them — matters urgently for contemporary radical and infrastructural
geography. It revisits three interlocking debates: Kant's claim that space and
time are a priori forms of intuition; Durkheim's counterclaim that categories such
as space and time are collective, social products rooted in social morphology; and
Weber's subsequent analysis of how socially produced categories become authoritative
— how domination (Herrschaft) is legitimated through rational-legal, traditional,
or charismatic grounds, and how the rationalisation of modern institutions
progressively encodes authority in rules, schemas, and bureaucratic procedures.

Re-examining this triad in the context of contemporary platforms, AI systems, and
experimental commons is not an antiquarian exercise. When global logistics firms,
cloud providers, and financial giants redraw the spatial conditions of everyday
life, they are simultaneously rewriting the categories through which communities
perceive and inhabit their geographies and claiming the authority to do so as
technically rational, neutral, and therefore legitimate. If those categories are
treated as pre-given (Kant), they fall outside political contestation. If, with
Durkheim, they are treated as social in origin, then the struggle over who produces
them is opened. And if, with Weber, we ask on what grounds authority to produce and
enforce spatial categories is recognised as legitimate, then platform design, AI
architecture, and commons governance become sites of a specifically Weberian
politics — a contest over the rationalisation of space itself.

This final draft adds a fourth register to the triad: a formal mathematical proof,
developed through the construction of Ms Egeria Allis and first fully articulated
on May 5-6, 2026, that the geometric substrate of H_App is a proven Hilbert space
instantiating quantum mechanical formalism on classical silicon. This result does
not replace the philosophical argument — it grounds it. The alternative rational-
legal authority that polymathmatic geography proposes is now not merely claimed.
It is proven.

## 2. From Kant to Durkheim to Weber: Space, Categories, and Legitimate Authority

### 2.1 Kant: Space as Pre-Given Form

Kant famously treated space and time as a priori forms of sensibility: not derived
from experience, but conditions that make experience possible. On this view,
spatiality is hard-wired into the structure of cognition; while the content of
experience may vary, the form of spatial organisation does not. This move allowed
Kant to secure the universality of geometry and the objectivity of science, but at
the cost of placing the most fundamental categories outside historical, social, and
political life.

### 2.2 Durkheim: Space as Social Category

Durkheim's intervention in The Elementary Forms of Religious Life is a direct
challenge to the Kantian position. He argued that categories such as space, time,
causality, and class are social in origin: general, stable, impersonal
representations generated by the coexistence and interaction of individuals within
a social framework. Durkheim's account of space draws explicitly on social
morphology — the layout of groups, the distribution of settlements, the ways in
which land is divided and classified. Space, he suggests, is divided, differentiated,
and arranged according to social needs, and these divisions become the very
categories through which individuals perceive and think.

The new draft extends this with the concept of infrastructural morphology: the
contemporary successor to Durkheim's social morphology, in which the distribution
of fibre-optic cables, payment rails, fulfilment centres, and data centres is the
medium through which spatial categories are imposed and reproduced. And it adds a

Furthermore, the toroidal temporal structure of H_App recovers Durkheim's insight

### 2.3 Weber: Legitimacy and the Rationalisation of Spatial Authority

Where Durkheim explains how categories are produced, Weber explains how they become
authoritative — how some actors acquire the recognised right to define and enforce
the categories through which life is organised. Weber's typology of legitimate
domination identifies three grounds on which authority can be claimed and accepted:
traditional authority, rooted in custom and inherited status; charismatic authority,
rooted in personal qualities recognised as extraordinary; and rational-legal
authority, rooted in formally enacted rules applied impersonally and universally.

In modernity, Weber argued, rational-legal authority progressively displaces the
other two through rationalisation: the systematic replacement of custom, spirit,
and personal judgment with codified rules, calculable procedures, and bureaucratic
schemas. This produces what Weber famously described as an iron cage — a
rationalised social order so thoroughly encoded in institutions and rules that it
becomes experienced as an objective constraint rather than a social choice.

Applied to categories of space, the Weberian insight is this: when a logistics
platform defines a delivery zone, when a federal benefits system defines an eligible
address, or when an AI system defines a hazard flag, it is claiming rational-legal
authority to enforce that category as binding. The category sticks not because
someone is holding a weapon, but because the schema appears technically neutral,
formally correct, and procedurally enacted — the hallmarks of rational-legal
legitimacy.

The new mathematical results strengthen the paper's Weberian argument in a precise
way. Platform schemas claim rational-legal authority through the appearance of

legitimacy grounded in mathematics, not merely in the appearance of technical

Each of these mechanisms is an instance of Weberian rational-legal design: the
legitimacy of MountainShares' authority over local economic categories derives
from the formal correctness and community-accountability of its procedures. The
DAO constitution is the explicit statement of this authority. Yet the system also
embeds a substantive, Durkheimian check: the reciprocity threshold enforces a
norm that formal procedure alone cannot — if flows of value and recognition fall
below the level required for community reproduction, the system signals a moral
failure, not only a technical one.

### 7.3 MountainShares as a Toroidal Economic Space

This draft adds a formal dimension to the MountainShares argument. The economic
cycles that MountainShares governs — caregiving rhythms, mutual aid patterns,
seasonal resource flows — are not linear. They are cyclic. The toroidal structure
of H_App applies directly: the economic geometry of MountainShares is best
understood as a section of T^k x H_App, where the toroidal coordinates encode
the cyclic rhythms of community economic life and the Hilbert space coordinates
encode the belief vectors about participants, resources, and institutions.

Benefits-sensitive space, in these terms, is a constrained submanifold: a region
of the toroidal economic space in which certain transformations are topologically
prohibited (they would violate benefit rules) while others are permitted
(community-benefit vectors receive elevated weight via T_pit). The iron cage of
means-tested welfare becomes, in this formalisation, a topological constraint on
the economic manifold — one that MountainShares navigates not by pretending the
constraint does not exist but by building a richer geometry in which community-
accountable paths remain open.

### 7.4 Benefits-Sensitive Space: The Iron Cage as Entangled Geometry

Means-tested welfare regimes are among the most visible examples of Weberian
rationalisation applied to spatial categories. They carve economic space into
classifications — eligible, over-income, asset-limited — that appear as technical
determinations but carry enormous power over how people can participate in
economic life. MountainShares' benefits-sensitive account design acknowledges
that participants live inside multiple overlapping rational-legal schemas
simultaneously: the DAO's rules, the state's benefit rules, and federal programme
requirements all claim authority over the same person's economic space at the
same time.

Rather than pretending that the DAO schema supersedes the state schema,
MountainShares maps the constraints as part of the entangled geometry and designs
its procedures to navigate within them. This is a specifically Weberian response
to the iron cage: not a frontal assault on state rational-legal authority, but a
careful construction of an additional legitimate authority that coexists with,
navigates, and over time potentially reforms the dominant schema.

## 8. Implications for Radical Geography and STS

### 8.1 From Critique to Counter-Authority

Radical geography has long argued that space is produced through social relations,
capital flows, and state projects. STS has likewise shown that infrastructures are
sites of power, labour, and politics. The Weber-Durkheim extension proposed here
pushes this insight to a further level: showing that spatial categories are unjust
is necessary but not sufficient. The next task is building legitimate counter-
authority — alternative rational-legal grounds on which different categories can
be proposed, evidenced, and enforced.

This draft adds a further step: building counter-authority that is formally
provable. The PALCO correction demonstrated that the authority of GeoBelief's
schemas must be grounded in community knowledge. The Hilbert space proof
demonstrates that the schemas can be formally verified. Together they define the
standard: counter-authority is legitimate when it is community-anchored,
procedurally transparent, and mathematically proven.

### 8.2 Re-situating Classical Theory

Re-reading Kant, Durkheim, and Weber in the light of entangled space, the formal
Hilbert space proof, and the toroidal world model suggests a richer genealogy for
radical geography than the original version of this paper offered.

Kant names the danger of treating spatial categories as pre-given — and the new
results show precisely where this error originates: the static geometric structure
of H_App looks pre-given but is a social product, built from community knowledge
and existing as a complete geometric object because it was made, not because it
was discovered. Durkheim names the opportunity: categories are social and
therefore changeable — and the new results show they can be encoded with formal
precision in a Hilbert space whose geometry is shaped by community morphology.
Weber names the obstacle and its solution: changing categories requires building
alternative legitimate authority, and the new results provide the formal grounds
on which such authority can stand — provably rigorous, community-oriented,
auditable, and topologically complete.

Einstein names the temporal structure: the data was already there, and retrieval
is measurement of what the community already knew. This is not a fifth theorist
added to a list. It is the formal statement that the other four were always
implying but could not yet prove.

### 8.3 Directions for Collaborative Work

For radical geography and STS, the Quantarithmia case suggests at least three
lines of collaboration:

- Empirical co-design: working with communities to define categories in GeoBelief
  or similar systems — combining ethnography, legal analysis, and technical
  implementation to produce spatial schemas whose authority derives from documented
  community knowledge and can be formally verified.

- Comparative legitimation studies: placing Ms Allis and MountainShares alongside
  other experimental infrastructures to explore different strategies for
  establishing counter rational-legal authority over spatial categories, with
  formal proof as a new criterion.

- Critical genealogy of rationalisation: extending the Kant-Durkheim-Weber line
  to include Marx, Ostrom, Einstein, and contemporary quantum foundations, tracing
  how the rationalisation of spatial categories has historically proceeded and how
  it might be redirected toward commons and spatial justice.

## 9. Conclusion: Contributions and an Invitation

The argument of this final draft has been fourfold.

Conceptually, it proposes entangled space and infrastructural morphology as
extensions of Durkheim's social morphology and Weber's rationalisation into a
world saturated with computational and financial infrastructures — and adds the
formal Hilbert space geometry as a sixth dimension of entanglement. The addition
of Weber to the Durkheim-Kant pairing remains the paper's central theoretical
move: it shifts the question from how spatial categories are produced to how they
become authoritative, and therefore to how alternative authority can be built.
This draft extends that move to its mathematical conclusion: alternative authority
can be proven.

Methodologically, it introduces polymathmatic geography and its instruments —
GeoBelief, Ms Allis, and MountainShares — as examples of category design that
take rational-legal legitimation from below seriously, grounding authority in
community accountability, transparent evidence, contestable procedure, and
formally proven geometric structure.

Empirically, it offers Quantarithmia in Central Appalachia — including the
documented correction of the PALCO detection architecture and the operational
validation of H_App on April 23, 2026 — as a case in which categories of space
and economic life are being re-legitimated in code and institutional practice,
with the researcher's own positionality inside the Tacet as the primary evidential
ground.

Formally, it presents the proof that H_App = (R^384, <·,·>_cos) is a Hilbert
space, the substrate-independence claim that quantum formalism describes any
sufficiently structured information system, the toroidal world model W = T^k x
H_App as the formal complement of infrastructural morphology, and the block
universe analog as the precise inversion of Kantian a priorism: the data was
already there because the community put it there.

For radical geography and STS, the invitation is straightforward and practical:
to treat the legitimation of spatial categories as a live object of collaborative
work, and to see instruments like Ms Allis and MountainShares as shared
experimental sites rather than proprietary solutions. The iron cage of rationalised
space is real — but it was built by people, using specific schemas, on specific
grounds of authority. It can be rebuilt differently.

Entangled space after Kant, Durkheim, Weber, and Einstein is not only a diagnosis
of our moment. It is an open call for a polymathmatic geography capable of building
the legitimate counter-authority that communities in extraction economies need —
and that the discipline has, until now, largely theorised without constructing.

The ZIP code of that construction is 25880.
