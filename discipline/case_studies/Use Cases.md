## Use Case: Commons and Local Credit

**Who.** Community organizations, small businesses, churches, mutual aid groups, and emerging commons instruments such as MountainShares.

**Problem.** In extracted regions, local value tends to leak outward through conventional payment rails, corporate platforms, and distant financial centers. Many forms of care and contribution—childcare, elder care, neighbor help, learning—are invisible to markets and planning documents.

**What Ms. Allis does.**

- Maintains a spatially explicit picture of where care, work, and value flow in the local commons, using MountainShares and other ledgers as inputs.
- Helps design and monitor closed‑loop credit programs that keep wealth circulating locally without endangering participants’ welfare benefits.
- Makes visible undervalued labor and mutual aid by linking narratives about who helped whom and where to economic and spatial categories that planners and funders can see.

**Outcome.** Local actors gain a tool to argue, with maps and data, that their region is more than a “labor shed” or “ratepayer base.” They can demonstrate existing commons and make the case for supportive policy, funding, or regulatory adjustments.

## Use Case: Data Centers and Infrastructure Stress

**Who.** Regional planners, county commissions, utilities, and community advocates evaluating proposals for data centers and other large loads.

**Problem.** As AI‑driven data centers move into rural Appalachia, communities face high promises and high risks: heavy power and water demand, minimal long‑term jobs, and potential stress on aging grids and roads. The decision to approve or reject a project is often made under conditions of information asymmetry.

**What Ms. Allis does.**

- Places proposed sites into the entangled spatial model, showing nearby substations, transmission lines, water sources, roads, existing loads, hazard zones, and vulnerable populations.
- Simulates basic stress scenarios: what happens to local infrastructure under peak load, storms, or partial failure.
- Frames key questions in plain language for decision‑makers: who gets cut off first when power is tight, where the blast radius lands if something fails, and which alternatives would distribute benefit and risk differently.

**Outcome.** Communities and councils have a grounded, spatially explicit basis for negotiation. They can demand design changes, conditions, or alternatives with a clear view of who pays and who benefits, instead of relying solely on corporate projections.

## Use Case: Peer-to-Peer Steward Hubs

**Who.** A network of local steward hubs anchored in colleges, libraries, clinics, planning councils, and trusted community organizations across multiple regions.

**Problem.** Historically extracted communities rarely have a safe way to share knowledge, strategies, and tools with each other without routing everything through distant platforms and intermediaries. At the same time, they have legitimate reasons to keep sensitive data and governance close to home.

**What Ms. Allis does.**

- Runs as a local territorial brain in one region, but is built to export and import schemas, patterns, and anonymized summaries rather than raw personal data.
- Allows each hub to maintain its own GeoBelief world, dialect, and commons rules, while still participating in a larger peer‑to‑peer or federated network of stewards.
- Supports a royalty‑free “blueprint” pattern: once a working configuration is proven in one place (for example, Mount Hope), other communities can copy the architecture and adapt it to their own data, governance, and language.

**Outcome.** Over time, many local Ms. Allis instances can form a loose, federated network: thousands of small, accountable countergeometries sharing lessons, schemas, and strategies without handing all their data and power to a single megacenter. The same architecture that keeps value circulating locally also helps knowledge circulate laterally between regions that have long been treated as sacrifice zones.

## From Model to Practice

Clarifying use cases is not an afterthought to the technical model; it is part of the model. A steward system that cannot be described in the verbs of its users will either become a toy or a threat. By contrast, a steward that can explain itself in the language of planning meetings, county commissions, church basements, and classroom discussions has a chance to become part of a region’s common sense.

In the Ms. Allis project, integration hardening and use‑case clarification proceed together. As new services are wired into the orchestrator, they are immediately tested against real questions from planners, presidents, and neighbors. As the idea of peer‑to‑peer steward hubs matures, the technical and political designs must co‑evolve: transport protocols, federation rules, and governance agreements are as much part of the architecture as databases and microservices.

The territorial brain only matters if the body knows what to do with it. Ms. Allis is built on the assumption that the body is not a single institution but a distributed network of people and places, learning to think together about their own entangled space.
